Evidence-Based Public Health (EBPH)

Definition (Brownson 2009) The development, implementation, and evaluation of effective programs and policies in public health through application of principles of scientific reasoning, including systematic uses of data and information systems and appropriate use of behavioural science theory and program planning models.

EBPH Practice — Study Design by PH Question Stage (Prof Azwany L1, Slide 16)

Stage PH Question Evidence Need Study Designs
"Something should be done" How bad is the burden? Who is at risk? Disease burden · Determinants/risk (Type 1) Prevalence study, Morbidity survey, Cross-sectional, Case-control, Cohort, Qualitative study
"Specifically this should be done" Which intervention is effective? Effective Intervention (Type 2) RCT, Quasi-experimental, Mixed Methods, Systematic Reviews, Programme Evaluation
"How it should be implemented" How do we translate evidence into practice? Implementation & contextual (Type 3) Health Impact Assessment (HIA), Mixed Methods, Qualitative study, Surveillance data
Identify PH Problem → Select Effective Intervention → Plan Implementation
Moving through these three stages sequentially is how evidence-based PH works. A common error is applying Type 2 (intervention effectiveness) evidence where Type 3 (implementation contextual) evidence is needed — i.e., knowing an intervention works but not knowing how to implement it in a resource-limited Malaysian setting.

Challenges in PH Evidence

Multifaceted Nature of Population Health ▼
PH interventions are large-scale, long-term, and concerned with external validity ("real world" applicability). Requires multisectoral strategies for multiple types of interventions.
Limitations of Traditional Hierarchy of Evidence ▼
"Adherence to a strict hierarchy of study designs may reinforce an inverse evidence law by which interventions most likely to influence whole populations (e.g., policy change) are least valued in an evidence matrix emphasising randomised designs." RCT randomisation is less feasible in population-based interventions.
Internal vs External Validity Tension ▼
Studies tend to overemphasise internal validity (well-controlled efficacy trials) while giving sparse attention to external validity (translation of science to the various circumstances of practice). In PH, external validity is critical.
Need for Contextual Information (Type 3 evidence) ▼
Contextual factors: community acceptance, integration, engagement, sustainability. Pragmatic considerations: community preferences, political & logistical feasibility, budget. Adoption of evidence requires time-efficient approaches and ongoing training.
Measurement of Outcomes ▼
Should not be limited to endpoints but all stages of programme development and implementation — immediate, intermediate, and long-term outcomes. Challenges arise in measuring intermediate outcomes in research.

RE-AIM Framework (Glasgow et al.)

Used for appraising PH evidence — assesses both internal and external validity of interventions. Requires subjective judgement on feasibility, practicality, and sustainability.

RE-AIM + CONSORT: 7 Steps to Increase External Validity Reporting
  1. State target population for generalisation
  2. Report rate of exclusions, participation rate, representativeness
  3. Report methods of recruiting study settings (exclusion rate, participation rate, representativeness)
  4. Describe participation rate & characteristics of those delivering intervention
  5. Report extent to which different components are delivered as intended
  6. Report specific amounts of time and/or costs to deliver intervention
  7. Report organisational continuance/discontinuance after trial completion

Triangulating Evidence in PH Decision Making

Effective PH decision-making requires triangulating three domains of evidence simultaneously. The intersection of all three domains forms the basis for sound decisions.

Internal Validity

"Does it work in ideal setting?"

Scientific assessment of the efficacy — well-controlled trials under optimal conditions. Most traditional research focuses here.

External Validity

"Does it provide a beneficial effect under real-world circumstances?"

Whether an intervention works for specific populations and in specific sites. PH gives this greater emphasis than EBM.

Population Context

Population characteristics, needs, values, and preferences. Contextual factors specific to community.

Resources & Capacity

Resources including practitioner expertise, organisational capacity (trained workforce, agency leadership), and funds allocation.

Contextual Variables for Type 3 Evidence (Adapting EBI) When adapting an evidence-based intervention for a new setting, consider: (1) Target population characteristics, (2) Interpersonal variables, (3) Organisational variables — capacity, trained workforce, agency leadership, funds allocation, (4) Social norms and culture, (5) Political and economic forces affecting allocation of public resources. Adoption, acceptance and utilisation of evidence is a process requiring time-efficient approaches and ongoing training with consideration of organisational values.

Tools for Critical Appraisal of PH Evidence

CA of PH evidence requires both internal and external validity assessment. Five main tools/approaches are used in PH:

1. Public Health Surveillance

Ongoing systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of health data essential to the planning, implementation, and evaluation of PH practice. Provides population-level evidence on disease burden and trends.

2. Systematic Reviews & Evidence-based Guidelines

Synthesise the best available evidence across multiple studies. Form the basis of clinical and PH guidelines. Highest level of secondary evidence when conducted rigorously.

3. Economic Evaluation

Cost-benefit analysis and cost-effectiveness analysis. With limited funding, investment in an ineffective intervention means a lost chance to invest in something that works. Health Economics Evidence Resource (HEER): Effectiveness → Time period of effectiveness → Cost of intervention → Perspective.

4. Health Impact Assessment (HIA)

Combination of procedures, methods, and tools used to judge potential health effects of a policy, programme, or project. Used for Stage 3 evidence (programme evaluation).

5. Participatory Approach

Community-based participatory research; engages community in evidence generation and appraisal. Ensures contextual and cultural appropriateness of evidence interpretation.

Practice vs Research Uses

  • Practice: Healthcare delivery, cost-benefit, disease prevalence & outcome, disease & risk factor trend, early warning
  • Research: System evaluation (HIA, surveillance), outbreak management, effectiveness of health interventions, evaluate clinical care & clinical outcomes

Study Design & PH Question Matching

Choosing the right study design to provide the evidence is critical. Common reasoning exercises:

Screening test safety (Diagnostic) ▼

Q: Is it safe to accept RTK-Ag test for COVID-19 as pre-operation screening? → Design needed: Cross-sectional diagnostic accuracy study — evaluate sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV against gold standard (PCR). Note: Many screening tests are not only unnecessary but can cause harm. Psychological harms from false positives; delayed treatment from false negatives; some screening tests themselves are harmful.

Workplace exposure risk (Aetiological) ▼

Q: Does length of time workers spend together with a COVID-19 case matter in increasing exposure risk? → Design needed: Retrospective cohort or case-control study — compare duration of exposure between infected and non-infected contacts. Note: This is Type 1 aetiological evidence — establishes that something should be done.

IMRAD Critical Appraisal Template — Public Health Evidence / EBPH
Introduction — Significance & Research Question
Is the public health problem clearly defined with epidemiological significance (burden, mortality, morbidity)?
Is the study question framed as PECO (Population, Exposure/Intervention, Comparison, Outcome)?
Does the study address Type 1, Type 2, or Type 3 evidence — and is this appropriate for the PH question?
Is the rationale for the study design justified given the PH context (population-level vs individual-level)?
Ask: Does this study fill a genuine gap, or is it replicating already-established Type 1 evidence?
Methods — Design, Population & Validity
Is the study design appropriate to the PH question (Type 2 needs interventional; Type 3 needs contextual/qualitative)?
Is the source, eligible, and study population clearly defined (GATE triangle)? Is sampling strategy described?
Is internal validity addressed (confounding control, bias minimisation)?
Is external validity addressed — participant characteristics, setting representativeness, generalisability?
RE-AIM: Are Reach, Adoption, and Implementation parameters described?
Ask: Would this intervention work under Malaysian real-world conditions (pragmatic vs explanatory)?
Results — Effect Estimates & Significance
Are outcomes clearly reported with appropriate measures (prevalence, RR, OR, IRR) and 95% CI?
Is statistical significance distinguished from public health significance (magnitude of effect)?
Are outcomes reported across subgroups (equity lens)?
Ask: Is the magnitude of effect large enough to justify population-wide resource allocation?
Discussion — Validity, Limitations & Applicability
Do authors acknowledge study limitations (bias, confounding, generalisability)?
Internal validity: Are findings free from major threats (selection, information, confounding bias)?
External validity: Can findings be applied to Malaysian population? Consider demographic, cultural, health system differences.
RE-AIM Maintenance: Is long-term sustainability of the intervention discussed?
Overall judgement: Is this Type 1/2/3 evidence, and what action does it support?

Topic 2: Critical Appraisal Methods

GET 512 · Lecture 2 · GATE Frame, CASP, PHO MetaQAT

Learning Outcome

  • Understand how to conduct critical appraisal in public health

Pre-reading: MaHTAS fabric mask rapid evidence update; Jackson et al. GATE frame (ACP Journal Club 2006)

Prof Doug Altman (BMJ Statistical Editor)
"To maximise the benefit to society, you need to not just do research but do it well."
The goal of CA is to determine whether research has been done well enough to trust its conclusions and act on them.

What is Critical Appraisal?

"The process of carefully and systematically examining research to judge its trustworthiness, and its value and relevance in a particular context." — Burls A

CA distinguishes useful from flawed peer-reviewed studies. Flawed studies — if used to guide decisions — may result in no benefit or significant harm. Aim: informed decisions by critically evaluating medical literature.

Pre-Reading Assessment: Is This Article Worth Appraising?

Before full CA, screen the article using two preliminary questions to assess relevance and quality of source (Prof Azwany L2, Slide 12):

1. Recency of Publication

Is the time of publication the most recent evidence available? Older evidence may be superseded. Check: Has a more recent RCT, systematic review, or guideline update been published since? For rapidly evolving topics (e.g., COVID-19, emerging infections), recency is critical.

2. Author Qualifications

Are the authors relevant and qualified to the scope of the study? Check: Are authors from a field appropriate to the study topic? Are they affiliated with reputable institutions? Are there any declared conflicts of interest (funding, industry ties)?

5 Key Questions When Reading an Article (Prof Azwany L2, Slide 10)
  1. What are the key learning points? — What does this study add to existing knowledge?
  2. What is its clinical/PH relevance? — Does it address a problem that matters to practice?
  3. Is the methodology robust? — Is the study design appropriate, sample adequate, measurements valid?
  4. Are the results reproducible? — Could another researcher replicate these findings using the same methods?
  5. Could there be bias or conflict of interest? — Industry funding, selection bias, publication bias?
Use these 5 questions as a quick mental screen before committing to a full CASP/GATE appraisal.

CEBM 4 Screening Questions (Initial CA Filter)

Before applying full CASP/GATE, use these 4 CEBM screening questions to determine whether a study is worth full appraisal:

1

2

Topic 3: Morbidity Surveys

"What is the prevalence of…" — Stage 1 EBPH: Identifying the Problem

Core Question

Is the disease a public health problem? What is the burden of disease?

Definition & Purpose

A morbidity survey is a systematic study of a sample of the population that can represent the whole population of interest. Aimed to provide a more comprehensive picture of morbidity than routine surveillance — specifically estimating the prevalence and/or incidence of disease or diseases in a population, and to monitor the health of a population.

Types of Morbidity Surveys

Prevalence Study

Point or period prevalence of disease/risk factors at one point in time.

Risk Factor Surveys

Prevalence of modifiable risk factors (e.g., smoking, obesity, physical inactivity) in a population.

National Health Morbidity Survey (NHMS)

Malaysia's national population survey. NHMS series (1986–present) provides national estimates for NCDs, mental health, child health, etc. Used as Type 1 evidence base.

Incidence Surveys

Longitudinal surveys tracking new cases over defined period. Less common; require follow-up.

Key Methodological Concepts

Sampling Frame & Representativeness ▼
The sampling frame must cover the entire target population. Complex multistage sampling (as in NHMS) uses stratification and cluster sampling. Assess: Is the sampling frame up-to-date? Are all subgroups included? Is the response rate adequate (≥70%)?
Case Definition & Measurement ▼
Case definition must be explicit, reproducible, and standardised. Measurement instruments (validated questionnaires, lab tests, physical measurements) must be validated in the target population. Standardisation of measurement reduces information bias.
Non-Response Bias ▼
If non-responders differ systematically from responders, prevalence estimates are biased. Assess: response rate, comparison of responder vs non-responder characteristics, sensitivity analysis assuming different prevalence in non-responders.
Prevalence vs Incidence ▼
Prevalence = existing cases at a point in time (P = I × D). Incidence = new cases over a defined time period. Most morbidity surveys measure prevalence. Incidence requires a cohort design or record linkage. Prevalence is affected by disease duration — a high prevalence may reflect long duration rather than high incidence.
Weighting & Complex Survey Analysis ▼
Complex survey data (NHMS) requires survey-weighted analysis to account for unequal selection probabilities and post-stratification weighting. Standard regression without weights produces biased estimates for population parameters.

How to Appraise a Morbidity Survey — Prof Azwany's Framework

Step 0 — Before You Start: Formulate Your PH Question
The community health screening has reported an increasing number of people with blood pressure in the pre-hypertension range. Targeted health education must be tailored to the high-risk group.
→ PH Question: Who are at higher risk of pre-hypertension? (Population-based prevalence + risk factor identification)
→ Search words: prehypertension, prevalence, risk factors, epidemiology + [population/country]
→ Is this a morbidity survey? The article should aim to obtain representative, population-based estimates of disease to inform health policies.
Step 1 — Read the Abstract First
Ask: Will this answer your PH question? Remember: A single article is never enough to answer a PH question. Then assess: (1) Is this a morbidity survey? (2) Does it provide population-based, representative estimates? (3) Is the study design appropriate for generating prevalence data?
Step 2 — Use CA Checklist as a Guide (Not a Tick-Box Exercise)
Use STROBE/CASP as a guide. The goal is to comment — not just tick. For each criterion ask: Why is it appropriate? What would be a better option? Write in sentences, not bullet points. Comment on both adequacy and limitations.

Introduction — What to Comment On

  • Is the background properly explained with scientific rationale?
  • Is the need for the study justified?
  • Are study objectives aimed at answering an important, unanswered query of clinical/PH relevance?
  • Are objectives specific and clear?

Methods — Internal Validity

  • Is the study design appropriate for the objective?
  • Clear, valid data collection methods?
  • Sampling strategy — probability sampling, sample size justification
  • Case definition validated and standardised
  • Analysis appropriate (chi-square, logistic regression, adjustments)

Results — What to Comment On

  • Only report findings of the study without reasoning them out (leave reasoning for Discussion)
  • Present actual values (not just p-values) — proportions, AOR, 95% CI
  • Completeness of variables included
  • Precision of estimation (width of CI)

Discussion — What to Comment On

  • Interpretation of findings — explain what the results mean in PH context
  • Caution in applying findings — study strengths and limitations
  • Can the study answer your PH question?
  • Can findings be applied to your population? (X-factor)

Sample Size Formula — Prevalence Survey

Formula: n = Z²·P·(1−P) / ε² × DEFF

Z = Z-score for desired confidence level (Z=1.96 for 95% CI)
P = Expected/estimated prevalence (use prior data or 0.5 if unknown — maximises sample size)
ε = Acceptable margin of error / precision (e.g., ε=0.05 for ±5%)
DEFF = Design effect for cluster/multistage sampling (typically 1.5–2.0 for national surveys)

Example: If P=0.36, ε=0.03, DEFF=2: n = (1.96²×0.36×0.64)/0.03² × 2 = 1,965 × 2 = 3,930
Add 10–20% for expected non-response.

Worked Example: Moussouni et al. (2022) — Algeria Hypertension Study

Essay Practice Scenario (Prof Azwany GET512):
The community health screening has reported an increasing number of people with blood pressure in the range of pre-hypertension. Targeted health education to increase awareness of risk of hypertension must be tailored to high-risk group.

As an epidemiologist in the non-communicable disease control division, you are tasked to propose health promotion to prevent pre-hypertension and identify the target group. You search for evidence for your proposal. You came across the article: "Prevalence and risk factors of prehypertension and hypertension in Algeria."

Task: Critically appraise the article to provide justification for your proposal and identify your target group.
Citation: Moussouni A, Sidi-yakhlef A, Hamdaoui H, Aouar A, Belkhatir D. Prevalence and risk factors of prehypertension and hypertension in Algeria. BMC Public Health. 2022;22:1571. https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-022-13942-y
Design: Descriptive cross-sectional; secondary analysis of WHO STEPwise Algeria 2016–2017 national survey · n=6,765 adults aged 18–69 years
PH Question (PECOT) ▼
P = Adult Algerian population aged 18–69 years
E = Sociodemographic, behavioural, and metabolic risk factors (age, sex, obesity, smoking, etc.)
C = No exposure / normotensive group
O = Prevalence of prehypertension (SBP 120–139 / DBP 80–89 mmHg, JNC-7) and hypertension (SBP ≥140 / DBP ≥90 mmHg)
T = Cross-sectional; national survey 2016–2017

PH Question: "What is the prevalence and risk factors of prehypertension and hypertension in Algeria, and who should be the target group for prevention?"
Introduction Appraisal ▼
✔ Strength: Background well-established — hypertension described as "silent killer," one of the most common chronic diseases globally. Burden in developing countries including Algeria clearly cited with prior epidemiological data (TAHINA 2005: 24.9%; Algeria STEPS 2003: 26±2.6%; WHO 2010: NCDs = 63% of all Algerian deaths).
✔ Strength: Prehypertension clearly defined using JNC-7 criteria (SBP 120–139/DBP 80–89 mmHg) with clinical rationale — prehypertensives have higher risk of cardiovascular morbidity/mortality.
✔ Strength: Objectives are specific and PH-relevant: to identify prevalence AND risk factors — answering both "how bad is the problem" and "who is at risk."
⚠ Limitation: Study is from Algeria — a North African developing country. Applicability to Malaysian population requires consideration of differences in ethnicity, diet, and healthcare access. However, the WHO STEPwise methodology used is the same tool applied in Malaysia's NHMS, improving methodological comparability.
Methods Appraisal — Internal Validity ▼
Study Design: Descriptive cross-sectional epidemiological study using secondary data from the national WHO STEPwise survey (Algeria 2016–2017). Appropriate design for prevalence estimation — cross-sectional is the standard design for morbidity surveys.

Sample: n=6,765 individuals aged 18–69 years, randomly drawn from households. National survey targeted 7,450 — response rate not explicitly reported in the secondary analysis. Comment: standardised objective measurement reduces information bias; international guideline-based definition allows comparison.

Topic 4: Cross-Sectional Studies

Measuring exposure and outcome simultaneously · GET 512 · Prof Azwany + Dr Aman

Core Design Features

A cross-sectional study measures exposure and outcome status in a defined population at a single point in time. Can function as a prevalence study, association study, or comparative cross-sectional study. Uses primary data (questionnaire ± physical measurements) or secondary data (surveillance, survey databases).

Step 0 — Formulating the PH Question (PECO)

PECO Framework for Cross-Sectional Studies

P = Population (define precisely — e.g., adolescents aged 10–19 using correct MeSH terms; NOT younger children)
E = Exposure (risk factor, behaviour, or characteristic of interest)
C = Comparator — frequently omitted in purely descriptive cross-sectional designs because there is no experimental comparison group
O = Outcome (disease, condition, or risk factor prevalence)

Search strategy tip: If the objective is to inform strategic planning or determine the full disease burden, widen search terms using Boolean "OR" (e.g., "overweight OR obesity") to capture comprehensive data — not just one category.

Step 1 — Appraising Introduction & Study Design (CASP)

What the Introduction Must Show

  • Background properly explained with scientific rationale
  • Objective must answer an important, unanswered query of clinical or PH relevance (e.g., the rising NCD burden due to epidemiological transition)
  • Specific and clear study objectives

Design Appropriateness

  • ✔ Appropriate for simultaneous determination of point prevalence AND multiple associated risk factors in a cost-effective manner
  • ⚠ Absolute limitation — must be explicitly declared: cannot establish temporality (sequence of exposure preceding outcome)
  • Strictly limited to identifying statistical associations and hypothesis generation — cannot prove causality

Step 2 — Sampling, Representativeness & Design Effect (CASP)

"Representativeness" is the single most critical factor when evaluating external validity of cross-sectional surveys. (Prof Azwany)
The sampling frame must closely represent the defined target reference population through objective probability sampling methods.
Sampling TypeQualityIssues

Simple random sampling / systematic sampling with probabilityAcceptableFeasible for small defined populations

Clustering Effect & Design Effect — Critical Appraisal Point ▼
When researchers sample from specific institutions (e.g., selecting students from specific schools), they introduce a clustering effect — students within the same school are more similar to each other than to students from other schools (intra-class correlation, ICC).

What to audit: Did the researchers statistically account for the design effect (DEFF = 1+(n−1)×ICC) in their sample size calculation and analysis?

If ignored: Parameters (ORs, prevalence estimates) and confidence intervals are unreliable — standard errors are underestimated, inflating statistical significance. This is a fatal methodological flaw.

N-value audit: Perform a meticulous flow-diagram check — track exact numbers from initial recruitment → exclusions → final analysis. The study must transparently report handling of missing data, dropouts, and non-responders. High non-response rates introduce non-response bias that threatens statistical power.

Step 3 — Measurement Tools & Information Bias (CASP)

Gold Standard: WHO STEPwise Protocol
Step 1 → Behavioural questionnaire (self-report: tobacco, alcohol, diet, physical activity)
Step 2 → Physical measurements (BP, height, weight, waist circumference — objective, calibrated equipment)
Step 3 → Biochemical tests (fasting glucose, lipids — blood samples)
!
Operational Definitions — Major CA focal point. Cross-check the study's definitions against established gold standards (JNC-7, local CPG). Example: If a study defines hypertension as SBP ≥140 only and omits the prehypertension range (120–139 mmHg), the resulting prevalence is dangerously undervalued and misclassified. Outdated definitions = invalid comparisons with current literature.
!
Self-Reported Data — Inherent biases. Behavioural surveys (dietary habits, physical activity, tobacco) are prone to: (1) Recall bias — inaccurate recall of past behaviours; (2) Social desirability bias — underreporting stigmatised behaviours. These biases cannot be eliminated, only acknowledged and mitigated by validated instruments.
!
BMI from Self-Reported Height/Weight — Fatal misclassification bias. Calculating BMI using self-reported height and weight without calibrated scales introduces severe misclassification bias — individuals systematically underreport weight and overreport height, underestimating true obesity prevalence.

Step 4 — Statistical Rigor & Confounding (CASP)

Prof Azwany's "Plus-Minus Trap" — A Common Statistical Error
Researchers frequently report skewed, non-normal continuous data using Mean ± Standard Deviation — this is incorrect for non-normal distributions.

What appraisers must verify:
  1. Did authors formally test for normality? (Kolmogorov-Smirnov test; Shapiro-Wilk for smaller n)
  2. If data is skewed → must report Median (IQR) and use non-parametric tests (Kruskal-Wallis for ≥3 groups; Mann-Whitney for 2 groups)
  3. Using Mean±SD for skewed data produces invalid p-values

Wide Confidence Interval = Model Instability (Dr Aman)
An exceptionally wide CI (e.g., OR = 88 for fried food intake) is a massive red flag — signals: (1) sparse cell counts in the contingency table, (2) model overfitting, (3) poor statistical precision. Such estimates cannot be used to quantify effect size for policy purposes.

Step 5 — Discussion, Verdict & Transposability

Transposability (Applicability) to Malaysian Context ▼
When formulating the final PH verdict, evaluate the "Transposability" of evidence to the local setting:

Cannot directly import: Exact raw prevalence percentages (e.g., Algeria's 36.2% pre-HT) due to geographical and cultural differences — population demographics, diet, healthcare access differ.

Can reasonably transfer: Underlying biological risk dynamics and behavioural associations (e.g., the relationship between urbanisation, central obesity, and hypertension; ageing and blood pressure) are highly transposable across populations.

Fatal flaw verdict: If methodology has fatal flaws (unadjusted clustering, severe recall bias, missing data, wide CIs, self-reported BMI) → the exact magnitude cannot be trusted to quantify local policy. It can only serve as supportive evidence for broad hypothesis generation.

Topic 5: Cohort Studies

Following exposed → unexposed groups over time · GET 512 · Prof Azwany + Dr Aman

Core Design Logic

A cohort study follows a defined group of people who are free of the outcome at baseline, measures exposure, and tracks them over time to compare disease incidence between exposed and unexposed groups. Core strength: establishes temporality (exposure precedes outcome). Effect measures: Relative Risk (RR) and Hazard Ratio (HR).

Step 0 — PECOT Framework & GATE Frame

PECOT — Cohort Study Question Structure (GATE Frame)

Every epidemiological study hangs on the GATE (Graphic Appraisal Tool for Epidemiology) frame, which structures the clinical public health question.

P = Participants (Population — well-defined, disease-free at baseline)
E = Exposure Group (exposed cohort)
C = Comparison Group (unexposed cohort from the same source population)
O = Outcomes (disease incidence, mortality, morbidity)
T = Time (follow-up duration sufficient for outcome manifestation)

Dr Aman: "The question right — your research question informs your objective; to answer your objective, your business design that you need to look at."

Step 1 — Design Appropriateness & Temporality (CASP Q1 & Q2)

Core Strength — Temporality

  • Exposure measured before the outcome occurs
  • Reduces spurious "effect-cause" (reverse causation) associations
  • Can satisfy Bradford Hill temporal criterion for causality
  • Can track multiple outcomes simultaneously from a single exposure
  • Can calculate absolute incidence (true event rate)

Core Limitations

  • Expensive and time-consuming: "Resources are very costly and if you study for too long, your benefit may go for what is more right." (Dr Aman)
  • Not efficient for rare diseases (need enormous cohort)
  • Long follow-up → high attrition risk
  • Exposure status at baseline may become outdated over time

!
Selection bias: If the cohort is drawn only from those who seek treatment or access medical facilities, mild or asymptomatic individuals are systematically missed → exposed group skewed toward more severe cases → biased RR.
!
Unexposed group must come from the same source population as the exposed group to ensure baseline demographic compatibility. Hospital-based comparison groups introduce comparison bias.
✔

Step 4 — Exposure & Outcome Measurement (CASP Q3 & Q4)

Exposure Measurement — Classification Bias Over Long Follow-up ▼
Dr Aman: "You must have the exposure measured. You must have the outcome measured right."

Major weakness in longitudinal data: Exposure is measured strictly at baseline. Over a long follow-up (e.g., 8 years), a person's lifestyle, urbanisation status, diet, or medication use may change substantially — exposure classification bias — the baseline label no longer accurately reflects true exposure status at the time of outcome.

Mitigation: Repeated exposure measurements at multiple time points during follow-up (increases cost but reduces misclassification).
Outcome Measurement — Equal Ascertainment Across Groups ▼
A standardised system must detect all cases equally across exposed and unexposed groups. If the exposed group receives more frequent monitoring and thus more diagnoses are detected — surveillance bias / detection bias — the RR is falsely inflated.

Topic 6: Case-Control Studies

Backward from outcome → exposure · GET 512 · Prof Azwany + Dr Aman

Core Design Logic

A case-control study starts from a known outcome (disease/condition) and looks backward in time to compare past exposure frequencies between cases and controls. It is the most efficient design for: (1) rare diseases, (2) conditions with long latency periods, and (3) studying multiple exposures simultaneously for a single defined outcome. The effect measure is the Odds Ratio (OR).

Step 0 — PICO & Design Appropriateness (CASP Q1 & Q2)

Critical Appraisal Trap — Do NOT just restate methodology.
Candidates lose marks by writing "The researchers defined cases as…" — this is summarising, not appraising. You must logically justify why the design matches the objective: backward causality, rarity of disease, resource efficiency, ability to examine multiple exposures.

When C-C is Appropriate

  • Investigating rare diseases (cohort would need enormous n)
  • Diseases with extremely long latency (e.g., cancers, TB)
  • Need to determine backward causality
  • Examining multiple risk factors for one outcome simultaneously
  • Limited time and budget — no prospective follow-up required

Absolute Limitations (must state)

  • Cannot calculate incidence or true prevalence — only OR
  • Highly prone to recall bias by design
  • Cannot study rare exposures efficiently
  • Temporality of exposure → outcome cannot always be confirmed
  • Cannot be used for multiple outcomes simultaneously

Step 1 — Case Selection & Misclassification Bias (CASP Q3)

1
Cases must be defined with explicit, objective, validated clinical criteria (laboratory confirmation, imaging, standardised diagnostic criteria) — not visual assessment or symptom checklist.
!
Worked red flag — Childhood Obesity Study (Riyadh): Researchers selected cases/controls based on a visual assessment of weight before taking measurements. Visual cues ≠ true BMI categories → severe misclassification bias even before data collection began.
2
Incident vs prevalent cases: newly diagnosed (incident) cases are preferred to avoid survivor bias (prevalent cases have already survived the disease, distorting exposure profiles).

Step 2 — Control Group Selection: The Paramount Core Strength (CASP Q4)

The fundamental internal strength of a case-control study rests entirely on the validity of the control group. If controls are poorly selected, the entire study collapses.
Control TypeQualityCritical Issue
Community-based controls from the same source population as casesGold standardEnsures the control group represents the population that produced the cases
Hospital/health-centre attendees (hospital controls)Selection bias riskHospital attendees share healthcare-seeking behaviour, potential illnesses, and common underlying risk factors (e.g., malnutrition) with cases — flawed baseline
Friends/neighbours of casesModerateMay over-match on lifestyle factors; shared exposures inflate similarity

Worked red flag — Ethiopian TB Study: Controls were classified as "no outward signs of TB" only — no clinical test or chest X-ray to rule out latent, asymptomatic TB. Latent TB cases contaminate the "healthy" control group → severe misclassification bias → validity collapses. Controls must be clinically confirmed to be disease-free, not just symptom-free.

Step 3 — Exposure Measurement & Recall Bias (CASP Q5)

Recall Bias — Inherent by Design
Cases (who already have the disease) are psychologically primed to more intensely recall past exposures than healthy controls. This differential recall artificially inflates the measured association. It cannot be eliminated, only minimised (objective records, blinded interviewers).
Sources of Information Bias in C-C Studies ▼
  • Recall bias: Cases recall exposure more intensely than controls (e.g., "duration of social contacts over past month" in the TB study)
  • Social desirability bias: Participants under-report stigmatised behaviours (illicit drug use, high-risk sexual behaviour)
  • Unknown exposure: A control may not know if a contact had active TB — they cannot report this accurately
  • Interviewer bias: If interviewers know the case/control status, they may probe cases more deeply
Mitigation: Objective measures (validated clinical records, biological markers) are vastly superior to self-administered questionnaires or face-to-face interviews for exposure measurement.

Step 4 — Matching & Confounding Control (CASP Q6)

Matching Rule — Must Explain WHY, Not Just WHAT
Stating "cases and controls were matched 1:1 by 5-year age band and sex" is insufficient. Award-worthy appraisal explains the causal pathway: age and sex dictate social contact patterns → social contact is the primary exposure under investigation → matching on these variables eliminates their confounding effect, allowing isolation of independent risk factors.

Step 5 — Statistical Rigor (CASP Q7 & Q8)

Dr Aman's FATAL FLAW — Matched Design → Must Use Conditional Logistic Regression

If a case-control study uses a matched design, the researchers MUST use conditional logistic regression. Using standard (unconditional) logistic regression on matched data breaks the assumption of statistical independence between matched pairs → biased AORs.

In the Ethiopian TB study: Despite using a matched design (1:2, age/sex matched), authors used unconditional logistic regression → fatal methodological flaw. All reported AORs are unreliable.

Step 6 — Final Verdict & Applicability (CASP Q9–11)

Writing the Final PH Verdict — Rules ▼
  • DO: State the value, trustworthiness, and transposability of the evidence for local policy
  • DO NOT: Generate new hypotheses or suggest the researchers should have used a different design
  • DO NOT: Tell researchers what they should have done differently
Ethiopian TB Study final verdict example:
The study demonstrates low internal validity due to: (1) hospital-based controls with inherent selection bias, (2) reliance on self-reported social contact (recall bias), (3) fatal use of unconditional regression on matched data, (4) omission of HIV/DM confounders. Findings can only serve as supportive, observational evidence — not as the sole basis for MOH policy. The conceptual risk pathway (overcrowded social contact → TB transmission) is conditionally transposable to urban Malaysian settings, but effect magnitudes (AORs) are unreliable.

Key CA Points — Malaysian School Cluster RCT (Lai et al. 2023) ▼
Design appropriateness: Cluster RCT is highly appropriate here. Schools as clusters prevent contamination — students in the same school could not be randomised individually without cross-contamination of intervention content. Wait-list control is ethical (control group eventually receives the programme).

Cluster design accounting: ICC=0.021 (Malaysian reference) — transparently reported and used in sample size calculation. Design effect applied → 8 clusters (schools), 50 per cluster, total n=400. GLMM analysis accounts for clustering at school level — methodologically correct. This is a strength.

Randomisation: Schools randomly assigned to intervention or control — but method of randomisation (computer sequence, concealment) not fully described. Were allocations concealed from recruiters before school enrolment? Unclear → potential selection bias risk.

Blinding: Participants and teachers were aware of allocation (unavoidable in educational intervention). Outcome assessors (researchers collecting anthropometrics) — blinding not stated. Risk of detection bias for subjective measures (self-efficacy, body satisfaction). BMI/anthropometric measures are objective — lower bias risk.

Theory basis: Social Cognitive Theory (SCT) — appropriate for behavioural change intervention targeting self-efficacy. Theory-based intervention is a strength.

Key results: BMI: 28.10→27.93→27.80 kg/m² (IG, significant); BAZ significantly lower in IG vs CG at 3-month follow-up. Breakfast consumption, self-efficacy, body satisfaction all significantly improved. Physical activity paradoxically decreased during week 10 (haze — jerebu — situation; schools closed/outdoor PE cancelled). This is an important confounder acknowledged honestly by authors.

ITT/attrition: 400 enrolled; 375 (93.8%) completed T1; 321 (~80.3%) completed T2. GLMM can handle missing data (assumes MAR). Differential attrition between arms not detailed — potential attrition bias.

Limitations: (1) Single state (Negeri Sembilan/Seremban) — may not represent all Malaysian adolescents. 81% Malays — limited ethnic diversity. (2) Haze during intervention period confounded physical activity data. (3) No blinding of participants or outcome assessors. (4) Short follow-up (3 months) — long-term sustainability of effects unknown. (5) Sex imbalance at baseline (IG: 94M/106F vs CG: 116M/84F, p=0.028) despite randomisation — residual confounding.

PRECIS-2 classification: Mostly explanatory — researcher-delivered (not routine school staff), structured module (not flexible), selected population (overweight only). However, real-school setting and Malaysian public schools enhance pragmatism. External validity to Malaysian primary health care school programmes: moderate.

Topic 8: Public Health Interventional Studies

Complex, programmatic, context-dependent · GET 512 · Prof Azwany

Why PH Interventions Are Different from Clinical Trials

Public health interventions are inherently large-scale, complex, programmatic, and context-dependent. Unlike RCTs focused on tightly controlled individual-level treatments, PH interventions target communities, policies, and systems. Critical appraisal must go beyond internal validity to heavily emphasise external validity, transferability, implementation fidelity, and context.

Step 0 — PICO for PH Interventions (Prof Azwany)

P = Population (e.g., overweight and obese adolescents in school settings)
I = Intervention (e.g., structured behavioural modification programme)
C = Comparator — Do not restrict too tightly: if "standard care" is over-specified in the search strategy, you miss relevant evidence because standard care varies heavily across countries and settings. Widen the search.
O = Outcomes (BMI, physical activity, dietary behaviour, quality of life)

Goal: Find Type 2 evidence (relative effectiveness) to answer: "Should this specific intervention be implemented here?"

Interrupted Time Series (ITS) — Strengths & Appraisal Points ▼
ITS uses multiple observations at several time points before and after an intervention (the "interruption"). A marked, statistically significant change that diverges from the underlying natural trend provides stronger evidence that the intervention — not background temporal trend — caused the change.

What appraisers check:
  • Minimum number of pre-intervention time points adequate (≥8 recommended for seasonal adjustment)?
  • Autocorrelation (serial correlation between time points) addressed in the model?
  • Segmented regression model used (level change + slope change)?
  • Concurrent events (co-interventions) ruled out as confounders?

Key Validity Threats — Detailed Appraisal

A. Selection & Allocation Bias ▼
  • Selection Bias (External Validity): Volunteers or highly motivated participants ≠ general public → findings non-generalisable
  • Allocation Bias (Internal Validity): In quasi-experimental designs, researchers hand-pick intervention groups → uneven baseline. Appraise: Was randomisation centralised and concealed? (Alternating days / birth dates = NOT valid allocation methods)
B. Confounding — Design & Analysis Control ▼
Dissimilar groups in quasi-experimental studies require confounding control at either:
  • Design stage: Matching, restriction, or stratification
  • Analysis stage: Multivariable regression adjusting for baseline BMI, SES, age, sex
Residual confounding from unmeasured socioeconomic, cultural, or environmental factors is always acknowledged.
C. Blinding & Detection Bias ▼
PH outcomes (dietary changes, quality of life, health behaviours) are frequently subjective. If outcome assessors are not blinded to group allocation → Detection Bias (they may inadvertently prompt or score intervention participants more favourably). Data collection tools must be validated, standardised instruments (e.g., validated accelerometers rather than self-reported physical activity logs).
D. Attrition Bias, ITT & Missing Data ▼
  • High or unequal loss to follow-up between groups destroys baseline comparability
  • Intention-to-Treat (ITT) Principle: Participants must be analysed in the group originally assigned to, regardless of whether they completed the intervention. This preserves randomisation and prevents overestimating efficacy
  • Multiple Imputation: If data missing at random — do NOT simply ignore dropouts (shrinks model, biases estimates). Use Multiple Imputation to mathematically fill missing values. Per-Protocol Analysis alone is insufficient
E. Clustering Effect & GLMM ▼
When interventions are allocated by group (e.g., whole schools), individuals within that group share similarities (intra-class correlation, ICC). Appraisers must verify that authors used advanced statistical modelling — Generalised Linear Mixed Models (GLMM) or multi-level modelling — to adjust for the design effect. Ignoring clustering artificially inflates statistical significance (standard errors underestimated).
F. Intervention Integrity & Type III Error ▼
Type III Error: If an intervention shows no effect, the appraiser must determine: was it a failure of intervention theory (bad idea) or a failure of implementation (Type III error — good idea, poorly delivered)?
Five Dimensions of Intervention Integrity (appraise each):
  1. Adherence — Was the intervention delivered as prescribed (protocol fidelity)?
  2. Exposure — Number and length of sessions actually received
  3. Quality of Delivery — Implementer enthusiasm, training, and preparedness
  4. Participant Responsiveness — Engagement and attendance levels
  5. Program Differentiation — Contamination (did control group accidentally receive the intervention?) / Co-intervention (did intervention group receive additional outside help?)

Step 5 — Final Verdict: Transferability & Unanticipated Effects

Transferability (not just generalisability):
Ask: Is our local baseline prevalence, political environment, and organisational capacity similar enough to the study setting to justify implementing this policy?

Anticipated vs. Unanticipated Effects: Good PH intervention studies must evaluate not just intended goals, but unanticipated harms or benefits (e.g., an intervention might improve BMI but increase social stigma, eating disorders, or health inequalities).

Topic 9: Qualitative Studies

Exploring the WHY behind behaviour · GET 512 · Prof Azwany + Dr Aman

Core Purpose

Qualitative research aims to explore, discover, and understand the subjective experiences, meanings, and cultural contexts of human behaviour — not to test a prior hypothesis. The appraiser evaluates trustworthiness rather than statistical validity. Key tool: CASP Qualitative Checklist + Lincoln & Guba Trustworthiness Framework.

CASP Q1–Q2: Aims & Methodological Appropriateness

Appropriate Qualitative Research Goals

  • Exploring subjective experiences (e.g., why patients do not adhere to diabetic medications)
  • Understanding meanings, values, and cultural context
  • Illuminating actions and decisions in natural settings
  • Generating hypotheses or theoretical frameworks

Not Appropriate For

  • Measuring prevalence or effect sizes
  • Testing prior hypotheses with statistical power
  • Establishing causality or relative risk

CASP Q3: Research Design & Philosophical Congruence

Grounded Theory (GT) Mismatch — Prof Azwany's Teaching Point
Grounded Theory, pioneered by Glaser and Strauss, requires the researcher to start with a "blank canvas" — the theory must emerge entirely grounded in data, with no prior framework imposed. If a study claims GT but acknowledges it was designed as an extension of a prior conceptual framework → destroys theoretical openness → this is NOT genuine Grounded Theory.

CASP Q5–Q6: Data Collection & Reflexivity

Data Collection Adequacy ▼
Appraise: Were IDIs or FGDs audio-recorded and transcribed verbatim? Were topic guides published or referenced? Were sessions conducted until saturation confirmed? Were field notes maintained?
Reflexivity & Power Differentials — Critical Appraisal Point
Reflexivity = the researcher's active, continuous examination of how their own identity, clinical role, preconceptions, and biases shape data collection and interpretation.

Worked red flag — Swarna Nantha et al. (2021), T2D self-management: The sole interviewer was a clinician at the same clinic as the patients being interviewed. This creates a severe doctor-patient power differential — patients may not speak freely (social desirability bias). No reflexive or positional statement was provided by the author to address how this was mitigated → critical appraisal failure point on reflexivity.

CASP Q7–Q8: Ethics & Analytical Rigor

Analytical Rigor — Coding & Verification ▼
  • Data typically coded via: Open coding → Axial coding → Selective coding (GT) or thematic analysis framework
  • Inter-coder reliability (Kappa): While unusual in pure qualitative research, some studies report Cohen's Kappa for coding consistency (≥0.80 = excellent)
  • Member Checking (Respondent Validation): Researchers return transcribed findings to original participants to verify interpretations authentically reflect lived experience. Absence = critical analytical flaw
  • Negative Case Analysis: Actively seek "disconfirming evidence" or rival accounts — proves the theory is robust, not cherry-picked
  • NVivo or ATLAS.ti software: Acceptable tool for systematic audit trail of coding decisions

Topic 10: Mixed Methods Studies

QUAN + QUAL = greater than the sum of parts · GET 512 · Prof Azwany

Core Definition & Philosophy

Mixed Methods Research (MMR) intentionally combines both quantitative and qualitative data collection, analysis, and inference within a single integrated study to gain breadth, depth, and corroboration. It is grounded in Pragmatism — rejecting the "paradigm wars" (incompatibility thesis between positivism and constructivism) in favour of using whatever methods best solve the real-world PH problem.

MMR Study Designs

Rationale for MMR must be explicit. Common rationales: (1) Complementarity — enhance QUAN results with QUAL depth; (2) Development — use QUAL to inform QUAN sampling/instrument; (3) Expansion — extend breadth of inquiry beyond either method alone.

Integration — The Core Heart of MMR

Merely reporting two separate strands side-by-side = NOT mixed methods research. Integration = the explicit interrelating of the QUAN and QUAL components so that meta-inferences emerge that are greater than either strand alone (1+1=3 Integration Challenge).

Forest Plot — Reading & Interpretation

Reading a Forest Plot (from Prof Azwany's MA lecture):
Each row = one included study. Square = point estimate (size of square ∝ study weight). Horizontal line = 95% CI. Diamond at bottom = pooled estimate (width of diamond = 95% CI of pooled estimate). Vertical line = line of no effect (RR/OR=1 or MD/WMD=0).

Example from Prof Azwany's lecture (Baranowski et al. 2003 — dietary intake MA):
I²=76.8%, p=0.000 → substantial heterogeneity → random-effects model appropriate. Pooled WMD=67.03 [5.75, 128.32], favouring higher intake in intervention group. Despite statistical significance, the wide CI and high heterogeneity mean the pooled estimate must be interpreted cautiously.
Heterogeneity Thresholds (I² statistic): 0–25% = low | 25–50% = moderate | 50–75% = substantial | >75% = considerable. If I²>50%, random-effects model should be used and sources of heterogeneity explored via subgroup analysis or meta-regression.

Citation: Oyegoke OO et al. "A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Malaria Test Positivity Outcomes and Programme Interventions in Low Transmission Settings in Southern Africa, 2000–2021." Int J Environ Res Public Health. 2022;19:6776.
Authors: University of KwaZulu-Natal (South Africa) + South African MRC — credibility established by institutional affiliation.
PICO: P=General population in Southern Africa | I=Malaria control programme interventions | C=No/standard intervention | O=Malaria test positivity rate (pooled proportion)
CA Using Prof Azwany's Framework — Malaria SR+MA ▼
Step 1 — Was SR preceded? Yes — title states "Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." The SR component must be appraised first (search strategy, selection, quality assessment) before the MA pooling is assessed.

Step 2 — Focused question? Yes — clearly stated: to pool test positivity rates and evaluate programme intervention effectiveness in low-transmission settings in Southern Africa, 2000–2021. PICO clearly defined.

Step 3 — Credibility of authors? Lead and corresponding authors from University of KwaZulu-Natal and South African MRC — institutional affiliation appropriate for malaria research in sub-Saharan Africa. South African MRC is a premier malaria research organisation. Adequate.

Homogeneity (CEBMA Q9)? Studies span 21 years across multiple Southern African countries with varying transmission intensity, intervention programmes, and surveillance systems — substantial clinical and methodological heterogeneity expected. Random-effects model should be used. I² must be reported and explored via subgroup analysis (by country, year, intervention type).

Practical relevance (CEBMA Q10)? Pooled test positivity rate is relevant for malaria programme monitoring in Southern Africa. For Malaysia (near-elimination setting), this provides Type 1 comparative evidence — however, Southern African (P. falciparum dominance) and Malaysian (P. vivax dominance) epidemiology differ substantially. Indirectness applies — the pooled estimate has limited direct transferability to Malaysia without Southern African → Malaysian applicability assessment.

Are results reported in sufficient detail (CEBMA Q11)? A well-conducted SR+MA should provide: forest plot with individual study estimates, pooled effect with 95% CI, I² statistic, and subgroup/sensitivity analyses. If these are absent, the MA conclusions are not fully verifiable.

Are findings applicable to your context (CEBMA Q12)? For Malaysian public health: Southern Africa (P. falciparum, high transmission background) ≠ Malaysia (P. vivax, near-elimination). Test positivity rate as an outcome metric is relevant to Malaysia's elimination programme (MyMalaria, EPMal). Applicable as indirect comparative evidence — the intervention approach (IRS, LLIN, active surveillance) is transferable; the quantitative pooled estimates are not directly applicable without adjustment for transmission intensity and species composition.
Key Teaching Points — Meta-Analysis (Prof Azwany framework)
  • MA must always be preceded by SR — appraise the SR component first.
  • Homogeneity across subjects, interventions, and outcomes is required before pooling. High I² (>50%) signals substantial heterogeneity — random-effects model; explore with subgroup analysis.
  • Appraising MA = methodological quality of included study designs + level of bias (use CEBMA 12-question checklist).
  • Publication bias — always ask: was a funnel plot reported? Were Egger's test or trim-and-fill conducted?
  • GRADE — the final step after MA: rate certainty of evidence (High → Very Low) based on RoB, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, publication bias.

Scenario: You are looking for evidence of risk factors for IHD. Appraise the article: "A case-control study of risk factors for ischemic heart disease in patients attending tertiary care hospitals in India."

Study Design: Case-control · Setting: 3 tertiary care hospitals, Ahmedabad, India · CASP Case-Control Checklist applicable
Model Answer — 2020/2021 IHD Case-Control ▼
Model Answer: Prof Azwany IMRAD Framework — IHD Case-Control (India)
Abstract & Introduction [~10 marks]
What to evaluate: Is the study question justified? Is the gap in knowledge stated? Is the case-control design appropriate for this question?

Abstract (2m): The abstract concisely communicates the IHD burden in South Asia and identifies the case-control design as appropriate for risk factor investigation. Univariate odds ratios are reported for multiple exposures. However, the absence of multivariate analysis results in the abstract is a critical omission — results presented may overestimate individual risk factor effects due to uncontrolled confounding.
Contextual rationale (3m): The introduction effectively contextualises IHD as a leading cause of mortality, particularly in South Asia, and references the global NCD burden projections. The transition from global to national (India) to local (Ahmedabad) context is systematic and provides adequate justification for the research.
Gap in knowledge (3m): A notable limitation is the absence of an explicit gap statement. The authors do not articulate what is not yet known about IHD risk factors in urban Indian tertiary care populations specifically, nor do they explain the novel contribution of this study beyond existing literature. This weakens the rationale for the study.
Objective (2m): The study objective is stated — to assess the association of risk factors in patients already diagnosed with IHD. However, the framing ("already diagnosed") introduces ambiguity about temporality. A more precise hypothesis (e.g., "to determine whether tobacco use, hypertension, and sedentary lifestyle are associated with IHD") would strengthen the introduction.
Methodology [~40 marks]
Largest section. Covers: study design (5m), population/setting (5m), case selection (5m), control selection (5m), exposure measurement (10m), data analysis (10m).

Study design appropriateness (5m): The case-control design is appropriate for studying risk factors of IHD — a condition with relatively low incidence that would require very large prospective samples to study directly. The design is efficient for hypothesis generation. However, its retrospective nature introduces temporal ambiguity: we cannot definitively establish whether exposures preceded disease onset, which limits causal inference.
Population and setting (5m): Cases and controls were recruited from three tertiary care hospitals in Ahmedabad. This introduces a critical source of selection bias — tertiary care populations differ systematically from community populations in socioeconomic status (SES), health-seeking behaviour, and comorbidity profile. The inclusion of potentially different hospital types (general vs. private) further heterogenises the study population. SES was not adequately controlled, despite being a known confounder for IHD.
Case selection (5m): Cases were defined broadly — angina pectoris, hypertension, myocardial infarction, cerebrovascular disease, congestive heart failure. This heterogeneous case definition risks misclassification bias: mixing IHD subtypes with different aetiological pathways undermines the specificity of the risk factor analysis. Diagnostic criteria were not standardised, and it is unclear which clinical specialty confirmed diagnoses. No sample size calculation was reported — the study may be underpowered to detect modest associations.
Control selection (5m): Controls were recruited from the same hospitals (patients without IHD), matched by age and sex. Hospital controls are convenient but problematic — these individuals already have other conditions that may share risk factors with IHD (e.g., a diabetic patient attending for glycaemic management). This Berkson's bias may underestimate the true risk factor associations. The method used to rule out IHD in controls was not described, raising concern about misclassification of controls.
Exposure measurement (10m): Exposures (tobacco use, alcohol, physical activity, diet, hypertension history, diabetes, family history) were ascertained via structured interviews using a pretested questionnaire administered by medical students. Multiple biases arise: (1) Recall bias — IHD is a chronic condition with long latency; cases may systematically over-recall risk exposures ("rumination effect"), inflating ORs. (2) Social desirability bias — participants may under-report socially stigmatised behaviours (alcohol, tobacco). (3) Exposure identification/interviewer bias — medical students lack training in neutral, standardised interviewing; differential probing between cases and controls may introduce ascertainment bias. (4) Operational definitions — how were "smoking," "sedentary lifestyle," and "obesity" operationally defined and measured? The absence of standardised cut-offs (e.g., WHO BMI criteria, IPAQ for PA) undermines measurement validity.
Data analysis (10m): Epi Info software was used, which is appropriate for case-control data. However, only univariate analysis was conducted — this is the most critical analytical limitation of the study. Univariate odds ratios do not account for potential confounders (e.g., age, SES, comorbidities) or effect modification. In a disease as multifactorial as IHD, where risk factors are inter-correlated (smoking + sedentary lifestyle + obesity frequently co-occur), failing to conduct multivariate logistic regression means observed associations cannot be attributed to individual risk factors. The results are hypothesis-generating at best and cannot support causal or policy conclusions.
Results [~15 marks]
Evaluate: completeness of reporting, reliability of findings, significance of key results.

Baseline characteristics (5m): Sociodemographic characteristics are reported, providing useful context. However, some age strata (above 70 years) have very small cell sizes, limiting subgroup reliability. SES categories were used but not clearly defined in the methods, undermining the validity of SES-stratified reporting.
Main results (7m): Significant associations reported (univariate ORs with 95% CIs) for alcohol consumption, smoking, non-smoking tobacco, hypertension, Type 2 diabetes, obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and family history. These directionally align with established IHD risk factor literature. However, as all ORs are unadjusted, they must be interpreted with extreme caution — confounding may explain part or all of the observed associations. The absence of multivariate ORs is a critical reporting gap.
Premature conclusions from results (3m): Drawing conclusions about "significant risk factors for IHD" based solely on univariate analysis is methodologically premature and potentially misleading. Future studies in this setting should conduct multivariate logistic regression, report adjusted ORs, and account for SES as a potential confounder.
Discussion & Validity [~20 marks]
Covers: internal validity (bias, confounding), external validity, comparison with literature.

Comparison with literature (5m): The discussion compares findings with previous studies, identifying consistent associations (smoking, hypertension, diabetes) with the existing body of evidence. This alignment provides face validity. However, the discussion does not critically explore why certain associations (e.g., alcohol) may differ from international studies — possible explanations (type of alcohol consumed, drinking patterns, SES confounding) are not explored.
Internal validity — bias (8m): The study is subject to multiple forms of bias: selection bias (hospital-based recruitment), recall bias (retrospective exposure ascertainment by cases), interviewer bias (medical student interviewers), and misclassification bias (heterogeneous case definition, inadequate control confirmation). These are collectively substantial and likely inflate reported ORs. The discussion acknowledges hospital-based recruitment as a limitation but does not fully explore its implications for bias direction and magnitude.
Internal validity — confounding (5m): The most critical confounding issue is the absence of multivariate analysis. Confounders such as age (matched, partially addressed), SES, diet, and comorbidities are interrelated with most exposures. Without adjustment, it is impossible to determine the independent contribution of each risk factor to IHD. This fundamentally limits the validity of the study's conclusions.
External validity (2m): Hospital-based sampling from three tertiary facilities in Ahmedabad limits generalisability to the broader Indian urban population, let alone to rural populations or other countries. Sample size constraints (acknowledged by authors) further restrict representativeness.
Applicability to Public Health / Malaysian Context [~15 marks]
Covers: can these findings be applied? What is the level of evidence? Is it relevant to Malaysia?

Level of evidence (5m): This study provides Level III–2 evidence (non-randomised comparative study) based on NHMRC criteria. The methodological limitations — particularly the absence of multivariate analysis and hospital-based selection bias — further downgrade its evidential weight. It is hypothesis-generating, not practice-changing.
Applicability to Malaysian context (7m): The risk factors studied (hypertension, diabetes, obesity, sedentary lifestyle, tobacco) are directly relevant to Malaysia's IHD burden. Malaysia's 2019 National Health and Morbidity Survey (NHMS) identified hypertension (30%), diabetes (18.3%), and obesity (19.7%) as major CVD risk factors — consistent with the study's univariate findings. However, the study's hospital-based Indian population is not representative of Malaysia's multiethnic (Malay, Chinese, Indian, Bumiputera) community population with different SES profiles, dietary patterns, and health-seeking behaviour. Direct application to Malaysian public health policy is not justifiable without multivariate evidence from Malaysian community-based samples.
Conclusion on applicability (3m): This study provides preliminary, directionally informative evidence about IHD risk factors that aligns with established knowledge. It may be used as supporting — not primary — evidence for IHD prevention messaging. Future research in Malaysia should use community-based case-control or prospective cohort designs with multivariate analysis to generate locally applicable, policy-grade evidence.
Examiner's Teaching Point: The absence of multivariate analysis is the single most critical methodological flaw in this study. A case-control study examining a multifactorial disease like IHD without adjusting for confounding cannot validly attribute the observed associations to individual risk factors. This must be explicitly stated and its implications for validity and applicability fully discussed to score full marks in the Methods and Discussion sections.

Critical Framing Note: The question asks you to appraise the article as supportive evidence for a specific policy recommendation (training doulas for home birth in Malaysia). Abstract & Introduction [~10 marks]
Is the review question clear and relevant? Is the gap in evidence identified? Is the study design appropriate?

Review question (3m): The review aims to synthesise evidence on the role of trained/professional doulas in supporting pregnant and birthing women. The question is relevant to maternity care policy. However, an integrative review — which combines quantitative and qualitative evidence without a strict systematic protocol — represents a lower level of evidence than a full systematic review or meta-analysis.
Gap identification (3m): The authors identify a gap in synthesised evidence on doula effectiveness, particularly distinguishing trained/professional doulas from informal support persons. This is a legitimate knowledge gap. In the Malaysian context, the gap is amplified — traditional birth attendants (Bidan Kampung) have been regulated and mostly replaced by hospital-based delivery; the reintroduction of trained birth companions for home birth represents a significant policy shift requiring strong evidence.
Design appropriateness (4m): An integrative review is appropriate for exploring a topic where evidence is heterogeneous (RCTs, qualitative studies, observational). However, it is a methodologically weaker design than a systematic review — the lack of pre-registered protocol, potential for unsystematic searching, and narrative synthesis limit the strength of conclusions. For a policy recommendation as significant as training doulas for home birth in Malaysia, a systematic review with meta-analysis would provide stronger evidentiary support.
Methodology [~40 marks]
Assess search strategy, inclusion/exclusion, quality assessment of included studies, and synthesis approach.

Search strategy — Prof Azwany Component 1 (10m): Were multiple databases searched (PubMed, Embase, CINAHL, Cochrane)? Were MeSH terms and Boolean operators documented? Was grey literature included? Were language restrictions applied? An integrative review that does not document a reproducible search strategy is at high risk of selection bias — studies supporting doula effectiveness may be preferentially included.
Review process — Prof Azwany Component 2 (10m): Were inclusion/exclusion criteria pre-specified? Did two independent reviewers screen titles/abstracts and full texts? Were disagreements resolved by consensus? A PRISMA-style flow diagram is expected even for integrative reviews. Without dual independent screening, reviewer bias in study selection is a critical concern.
Quality assessment — Prof Azwany Component 3 (10m): Included studies should be critically appraised using appropriate tools (CASP RCT, NOS, JBI qualitative appraisal). Was the quality of each included study rated? Were low-quality studies excluded or sensitivity-analysed? In an integrative review, failure to weight evidence by quality risks basing conclusions on weak or biased primary studies — particularly important for a policy recommendation on home birth safety.
Synthesis (10m): Integrative reviews typically use narrative synthesis. Was a conceptual framework used to organise findings? Were outcomes categorised (maternal outcomes, neonatal outcomes, satisfaction, cost)? For Malaysian applicability, outcomes relevant to Malaysia's health system (home birth safety, referral systems, doula training standards) must be identifiable from the synthesis. If the included studies are predominantly from high-income Western countries, the synthesis has significant indirectness for Malaysian application.
Results [~15 marks]
What outcomes were reported? How robust are the findings?

Reported outcomes (8m): The review likely reports benefits of doula support including reduced caesarean section rates, reduced analgesic use, improved maternal satisfaction, and improved breastfeeding initiation — outcomes consistently identified in Cochrane reviews of continuous support in labour (Hodnett et al.). However, effectiveness in home birth settings specifically versus hospital-based support must be distinguished. Evidence on home birth safety outcomes (intrapartum mortality, emergency transfer rates) is critical for the Malaysian policy question but may be sparse in the included literature.
Strength of evidence (7m): The integrative design means findings are from heterogeneous studies of variable quality. If the review does not provide quality ratings for included studies, the reader cannot assess how much weight to place on specific findings. Overall, the evidence base for doula effectiveness in hospital settings is reasonably established; evidence for trained doulas in home birth settings is less robust and more context-dependent.
Discussion & Validity [~20 marks]
Internal validity of the review; comparison with other reviews; limitations acknowledged?

Internal validity of the review (8m): Key validity threats: (1) Search bias — non-systematic searching may miss relevant studies; (2) Publication bias — positive studies more likely published and included; (3) Heterogeneity — pooling outcomes across very different healthcare settings (hospital vs home, high-income vs low-income countries) without stratification undermines conclusion reliability; (4) Temporal bias — older included studies may not reflect contemporary doula training standards or perinatal care practices.
Comparison with higher-level evidence (7m): The Cochrane Systematic Review by Hodnett et al. (2012; updated) on continuous support for women during childbirth provides higher-quality pooled evidence (17,000+ participants, 22 trials) and should be the primary reference for any policy recommendation on doula effectiveness. The integrative review's conclusions should be compared against this stronger evidence base. If they are consistent, the integrative review provides corroborating evidence; if inconsistent, the higher-quality Cochrane review takes precedence.
Limitations acknowledged (5m): Authors should explicitly acknowledge: the heterogeneity of included study designs, potential for publication bias, and the context-dependency of doula effectiveness across different healthcare systems.
Applicability to Malaysia [~15 marks]
Islamic & cultural considerations (5m): Home birth with doula support must be evaluated within Malaysia's Islamic healthcare framework. Traditional Bidan Kampung (traditional birth attendants) have a historical role, but MOH Malaysia discourages unassisted home birth due to EmONC (Emergency Obstetric and Neonatal Care) access concerns. A trained doula model integrated with hospital or birthing centre care may be more transferable than pure home birth models.
Policy verdict (10m): The integrative review provides moderate-quality supportive evidence that continuous doula support improves maternal satisfaction and reduces unnecessary interventions. It is NOT sufficient as a sole basis for Malaysian MOH home birth policy change. It can supplement the higher-quality Cochrane SR evidence to advocate for trained doula-equivalent support within existing Malaysian maternity care structures (hospital-based continuous support programmes).
Examiner's Teaching Point: When appraising an integrative review, always compare it against higher-level Cochrane SR evidence. An integrative review without PRISMA compliance or quality assessment of included studies can only serve as supportive, hypothesis-generating evidence — not primary policy evidence.
