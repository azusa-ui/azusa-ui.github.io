1. Occupational & Environmental Epidemiology
Source: Dr Suhaily Mohd Hairon · Special Issues in Occupational Epid 2026; Environmental Health 2026 · Checkoway et al. 2007
🏭 Introduction

Occupational epidemiology involves the application of epidemiologic methods to populations of workers. It is a systematic study of illnesses & injuries related to the workplace environment (Checkoway et al. 2004). Studies determine if occupational exposures result in the risk of adverse health outcomes.

Historical milestone: The first epidemiologic occupational study was published in 1775 by Sir Percival Pott — high scrotal cancer occurrence among London chimney sweeps (exposure to soot/coal tar).

Main Objectives

  • Identify consequences of workplace exposure on health
  • Produce & analyse results from specific settings
  • Reduce or eliminate hazards
  • Guide prevention strategies

Role of Occupational Epid

  • Establish causes & determinants of ill-health
  • Ensure recognition & quantification of exposures
  • Determine occupational exposure limits (OEL)
  • Study rare exposures/outcomes
  • Recommend remedial measures
📊 Levels of Application
1. Surveillance ▼

Identify & quantify illness, injury or excessive exposure. Describe occurrence of illness in different industry types, over time and between geographic areas. Provide early warning signals of unrecognized occupational hazards.

2. Generation & Testing of Hypotheses ▼

That a given exposure may be harmful; quantification of an effect; evaluation of an intervention by measuring changes in health status of a population over time.

3. Epidemiological Investigation Approach ▼
  • Recognize a disease cluster among workers from particular occupations
  • Conduct a survey in the industry to determine magnitude of problem
  • Consider other diseases which may occur at excess
  • Determine exposure to a known or unknown hazardous agent
  • OR start with a particular exposure & conduct medical surveillance
🧪 Exposure Assessment

Assessment of exposures is a critical step in identifying workplace hazards. It focuses on characterizing the exposure experience of workers.

Process of Exposure Assessment

  1. Compile a list of potential toxic agents in work environment
  2. Determine how exposure occurs & how likely they are to vary among employees
  3. Select appropriate measures for quantifying exposures
  4. Collect data

Important Assessment Factors

  • Identification of the relevant agents
  • Determination of their presence & concentrations in appropriate environmental media
  • Assessment of likely routes of entry (inhalation, dermal, ingestion), time course & cumulative duration
  • Evaluation of effectiveness of engineering & personal controls
  • Host factors that may modulate target organ concentrations

🔬 Study Designs in Occupational Epidemiology
Key principle (Checkoway 2007): Choice of study design is nearly always determined by the research question AND feasibility constraints. All designs require a clearly defined source population (study base).
Cross-Sectional Study ▼

Definition: Prevalence of a disease or symptoms is investigated at a single time-point. Comparisons are made in the frequency of ill-health between exposed and non-exposed workers, or between workers with different exposure levels.

Outcome measure: Prevalence rate

Best for: Persistent/chronic conditions — musculoskeletal disorders, chronic respiratory impairment, pneumoconioses, physiological abnormalities (lung function, liver enzymes), chromosome abnormalities.

Example: Chest X-ray survey of quarry workers — workers in high quartz exposure quarries have higher prevalence of pneumoconiosis.

Limitation: Only active workers usually investigated → survivor bias. Reverse causation possible — exposure status may be an effect of the disease. Cannot establish temporality.
Cohort Study ▼

Definition: Comparisons in incidence of ill-health (or mortality) are made between exposed & non-exposed cohorts, or between subsets of the same cohort with different degrees of exposure.

Types: Prospective (following forward in time) or Retrospective/Historical (using historical records — most common in occupational studies for mortality/chronic disease).

Best for: Chronic diseases with long latency (e.g., cancers, chronic lung disease), mortality studies.

Example: Workers followed during employment in a benzene-exposed factory → those with high cumulative benzene exposure had higher mortality from leukaemia compared to control population.

Outcome measure: Incidence rate, Relative Risk (RR), Hazard Ratio (HR), Standardized Mortality Ratio (SMR)

Key limitation: Long follow-up needed. Historical cohort studies often limited to mortality outcomes because non-fatal outcome data are rarely available in records. Absent/sparse past exposure data is common.
Case-Control Study ▼

Definition: 'Cases' of disease are compared to a carefully matched reference population of 'controls' to determine retrospectively what differences there may have been in their occupational exposure histories.

Best for: Rare diseases; exploratory studies; investigating multiple exposures for one disease.

Example: Patients with adenocarcinoma of the nose had a disproportionately greater likelihood of having worked in the hardwood industry compared to patients with other pathology.

Outcome measure: Odds Ratio (OR)

Issues: Recall bias. Controls' exposures must represent the exposure experience of the source population (underlying validity principle).
Nested Case-Control Study ▼

Definition: Embedded within a cohort study. Leukemia cases identified during follow-up are used as the cases; leukemia-free workers are used as controls.

Best for: Workplace hazards of particular interest that cannot be studied efficiently with a full cohort analysis (e.g., when detailed exposure reconstruction is needed only for a subset).

Example: Solvents → Leukemia. Rather than reconstructing exposures for the entire cohort, only reconstruct for cases + matched controls.

Advantage: More efficient than full cohort analysis; reduces cost; allows more detailed exposure assessment for a subset.

Registry-Based Case-Control ▼

Uses registries (cancer registry, hospital admissions, insurance claims, disability pension awards) to define cases and controls. Most lack detailed exposure data — often only industry/job title known. Mostly used for screening hypotheses.

Case Series ▼

Identification & reporting of a disease cluster among the workforce. Very useful to start an investigation, especially when the disease is extremely rare and causal factors are unknown. Clusters can be misleading — could be entirely due to chance.

Intervention / Experimental Study ▼
  • Field trials: Large sample required (e.g., study of preventive effect of respirators)
  • Community intervention trials: E.g., change in a plant from one production method to another
  • Randomized clinical trials: Test new treatments
Design selection depends on: Feasibility (funds, manpower, study material), Efficiency, Validity. Longitudinal study is preferable over cross-sectional.
⚠️ Bias & Confounding in Occupational Studies

Types of Bias

1. Selection Bias ▼

Present when individuals have different probabilities of being included according to relevant study characteristics. Results from comparison groups not coming from the same study base.

Sources: Procedures used to select subjects; factors influencing study participation; factors influencing participant attrition.

2. Information/Measurement Bias ▼

Method of gathering information yields systematic errors in measurement of exposures or outcomes. Results in misclassification.

Types of misclassification:

  • Non-differential: Misclassification unrelated to disease/exposure → tends to underestimate association (biased towards null)
  • Differential: Misclassification related to disease/exposure → effects harder to predict

Specific Bias Types

BiasDefinitionPrevention
Recall biasInaccurate recall of past exposure (exposure identification bias)Verification from study subjects; objective markers; cohort/nested case-control design
Interviewer biasFail to follow questionnaire protocol; differential probingCareful design; quality assurance; mask interviewer to case-control status
Observer biasOutcome ascertainment not independent from knowledge of exposure status → differential outcome misclassificationMask observers to exposure status; multiple observer classification
Respondent biasOutcome information from participant responses during cohort follow-upConfirm outcomes by objective means (hospital records)
Non-differential misclassification example: True OR = 1.80. After 10% misclassification of all cases & controls → OR = 1.56. Association is attenuated towards null.

👷 Healthy Worker Effect (HWE)
Definition (Last, 1995): "Workers usually exhibit lower overall death rates than the general population because the severely ill and chronically disabled are ordinarily excluded from employment."

HWE is a type of bias/confounding. It reduces mortality/morbidity in occupational cohorts compared to the general population. Most studies indicate HWE will reduce the observed association by 20–30%.

Factors Affecting HWE

FactorEffect
Age at hire↑ in young age group; decreases with advancing age; disappears after retirement
Duration of employment↑ in beginning of employment
Socioeconomic status↑ in better social categories (mortality comparatively low in higher SES)
Gender↑ for men than women

Strategies to Minimize HWE

1. Avoid Using General Population as Reference Group ▼

General population includes non-workers in poor health, exaggerating HWE. Instead compare to active workers from another industry without the same exposure. Example: Compare coal miners to construction workers (similar physical demands, different exposures).

2. Start Study After a Latency Period (1–5 years) ▼

Initiating the study after a latency period helps exclude healthiest workers who might leave the job early. Example: For asbestos study in shipyard workers, start follow-up only after workers have been employed for at least 5 years.

3. Dichotomize Exposed Cohort into Heavily vs Lightly Exposed ▼

Breaking down cohort based on exposure levels helps understand dose-response relationship and control HWE within the same cohort. Example: Chemical plant workers subgrouped as high-exposure vs low-exposure — compare rates of respiratory disease.

4. Include Every Person Who Ever Worked in a Factory ▼

Including ALL workers regardless of current employment status captures the full spectrum of health outcomes, including those who left due to health issues. Example: In a lead smelter study, include all workers who ever worked there, including those who left due to lead poisoning.

5. Not Computing Person-Years Before 5–10 Years of Employment ▼

Allows the health status of workers to deteriorate before comparing to the general population, reducing the initial disparity.

6. Advanced Statistical Methods (MSM, G-estimation) ▼

Marginal Structural Models (MSMs): Use inverse probability weighting to deal with time-dependent confounding, creating a pseudo-population. G-estimation: Developed by Robins — addresses time-dependent confounding by comparing individuals with similar exposure histories.

🌍 Environmental Epidemiology
WHO Definition: Environmental health addresses all physical, chemical, and biological factors external to a person, and all related factors impacting behaviours. It encompasses assessment and control of environmental factors that can potentially affect health.

Environmental epidemiology studies the relationship between environmental exposures (chemicals, radiation, microbiological agents, etc.) and human health. Observational studies predominate because humans cannot ethically be experimentally exposed to suspected disease-causing agents.

10 Essential Environmental Public Health Services

  1. Monitor environmental and health status
  2. Diagnose and investigate environmental health problems
  3. Inform, educate and empower people
  4. Mobilize community partnerships
  5. Develop policies and plans
  6. Enforce laws and regulations
  7. Link people to needed services
  8. Assure a competent environmental health workforce
  9. Evaluate effectiveness of services
  10. Research for new insights
📐 Study Designs in Environmental Epidemiology
Cross-Sectional Study ▼

Estimates health status/disease pattern at a specific time. Can measure several exposures and be the first step in exploring cause. Quick, inexpensive, less time-consuming. Example: Late 1960s studies on blackfoot disease associated with arsenic in drinking water in Taiwan (Tseng 1977).

Case-Control Study ▼

Cases selected on basis of disease; controls without disease at enrolment. Useful for rare environmental exposures or diseases.

Case-Crossover Study ▼

To study effects of transient short-term exposures on acute events. Cases compare their own exposure during a risk period to exposure during control periods. Each case serves as his or her own control. Eliminates confounding from fixed individual characteristics.

Birth Cohort Study ▼

Pre-conceptual and prenatal exposures assessed. Follow-up tracks development, variations in exposures & health outcomes over time. Can measure DNA methylation profiles at birth in easily obtained tissues (saliva, cord blood, placenta).

Example: National Children's Study — 21-year prospective cohort of 100,000 US-born children studying environmental influences on health and development (called off 2014).

Challenge: Requires extremely large sample before disease onset; sufficient follow-up time; selection bias if participation/follow-up is related to exposure and outcome.
Two-Stage Design ▼

When exposure and disease are both rare. Stage 1 (screening): exposure & disease ascertained on a large sample. Stage 2: complete covariate information & refined exposure measurement collected on a subsample only. Sampling fractions for Stage 2 can depend jointly on disease & exposure status. More efficient and less expensive.

Twin & Family-Based Study ▼

Estimate genetic basis of traits and heritability of epigenetic profiles. Assess individual variation in sensitivity to exposure. Family-based studies of gene–environment interaction may be more powerful than population-based studies.

Measurement of Environmental Exposure — Key Issues

  • Details of exposure timing — critical window likely differs for different phenotypes
  • Single measurement may not be reliable for investigating long-term chronic effects
  • Need to incorporate different exposure assessment techniques
  • Exposure effects differ across life stages (chemicals have different dose-response curves by life stage)

Improved Measurement Tools

GIS (Geographic Information Systems) GPS (Global Positioning System) Personal monitoring Biomonitoring Geospatial + statistical models

Mendelian Randomization (MR)

Using established associations between genetic variations and exposure (intermediate phenotypes) to maximize causal inference. Genetic variations mimic modifiable exposure effects — serve as natural randomization. Provides approach for causal inference without randomized trial. Key method in environmental & genetic epidemiology.
📄 Key Articles — Summary
Checkoway, Pearce & Kriebel (2007) — Selecting Appropriate Study Designs in Occupational Epid ▼

Key message: Choice of study design is determined by research question + feasibility. All designs require clearly defined source population (study base). Incidence — not prevalence — is the fundamental measure. Chronic diseases often have continuous disease onset that is difficult to epidemiologically characterize.

CONSTANCES Cohort — Population-Based Prospective Cohort for Occ & Env Epid ▼

Design: General prospective population-based cohort study in France. Large-scale cohort for occupational and environmental epidemiology research.

Purpose: Study long-term effects of occupational and environmental exposures. Covers a wide range of health outcomes. Detailed occupational history collected at baseline and follow-up.

Key feature: Linkage with national health databases (mortality, hospitalisation, cancer registry, social security) enables comprehensive outcome ascertainment without active follow-up bias.

Lesson for study design: Large-scale cohorts with administrative data linkage can overcome limitations of individual follow-up and provide efficient, comprehensive outcome ascertainment in occupational and environmental studies.
Marant Micallef et al. (2018) — Occupational Exposures & Cancer: Review of Agents & RR Estimates ▼

Design: Review of attributable fractions and relative risk estimates for occupational exposures and cancer at multiple sites.

How exposure was identified & quantified: Systematic review of published cohort and case-control studies; IARC monograph classifications; dose-response data from occupational hygiene records and job exposure matrices (JEM).

Risk assessment approach: Population attributable fractions (PAF) calculated from RR estimates and prevalence of occupational exposure in the workforce. Multiple cancer sites analysed including lung, bladder, mesothelioma, nasal/sinus.

Key findings: Significant proportion of cancers attributable to occupational exposures — particularly in occupations with heavy chemical, dust, radiation or carcinogen exposures. Lung cancer and bladder cancer strongly associated with occupational carcinogens.

Pesticides & Prostate Cancer — Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis ▼

Design: Systematic review and meta-analysis of studies on occupational exposure to pesticides and prostate cancer risk.

Exposure assessment issues: Heterogeneity in how pesticide exposure was defined and measured across studies. Job title vs. self-report vs. biomonitoring gave variable results. Specific pesticide compounds (organochlorines, organophosphates, herbicides) analysed separately.

Key finding: Suggestive elevated risk of prostate cancer with pesticide exposure, though heterogeneity limits definitive conclusions. Highlights importance of specific compound-level analysis rather than broad "pesticide" category.

Methodological lesson: Exposure misclassification in pesticide studies is major. Using job titles alone is insufficient — specific compound, duration, and dose data needed.

🧬 Definition & Scope
Buck Louis & Platt (2011) [R1]: Reproductive and perinatal epidemiology is the study of the distribution, determinants, and sequelae of reproductive and/or perinatal processes and events. The field is defined by reproductive and perinatal outcomes (not exposures).
WHO Reproductive Health Definition [R2]: Addresses reproductive processes, functions and system at all stages of life — implies people are able to have a responsible, satisfying and safe sex life, the capability to reproduce, and the freedom to decide if, when and how often to do so.

Two Research Domains [R1, Ch.1]

  • Reproductive epidemiology:
    • Domain 1: Fecundity & Fertility
    • Domain 2: Pregnancy
  • Perinatal epidemiology:
    • Domain 3: Pregnancy outcomes
    • Domain 4: The Neonate

Why Reproductive Epid is Special [R2]

  • Outcome-based epidemiology (defined by outcome, not exposure)
  • Window of vulnerability / sensitive periods critical
  • Unit of interest can be: mother, child, father, OR dyad
  • Pregnancies are repeated events (complicates analysis)
  • Exposure timing relative to conception matters enormously
  • Both male and female reproductive health included

⏱️ Sensitive Windows & Life Course Perspective
Sensitive windows [R1, Ch.2]: Specific time periods in human reproduction and development during which exposures have disproportionate effects on subsequent health. Critical for study design — must know when to measure exposure.

Life Course Framework [R2]

  • Lifecourse Theory: Health status at any point in life is the result of lifetime accumulation of biological, behavioural, and psychosocial risk factors
  • Barker's Hypothesis (DOHaD): Developmental Origins of Health and Disease — LBW linked to maternal undernutrition during pregnancy → increased adult risk of CVD, type 2 diabetes, obesity [R1, Ch.3]
  • Social determinants: Incorporated into modern frameworks

Exposome Concept [R2]

New framework: Exposome = total environmental exposure measurement from pre-conception through sensitive windows to outcome and complications. Captures the full environmental exposure burden over the life course. Introduced by Wild (2005); operationalised in reproductive epid by Buck Louis group.

🔬 Study Designs in Reproductive Epidemiology
From lecture [R2]: "Observational studies are usual — survey or cohort. In survey/cross-sectional: only done during/after booking clinic — miscarriage, abortion not declared? In cohort: when will pregnancy occur? Different pregnancy times create period effects."
Prospective Birth Cohort (Gold Standard) ▼

Definition: Enrol women early (ideally pre-conception), collect exposure data prospectively, follow offspring through development. [R1, Ch.4]

Advantages: Avoids recall bias for exposure; can assess multiple outcomes; establishes temporality; can collect biological samples at key time points (cord blood, placenta, serial sampling).

Examples: ALSPAC (UK), Born in Bradford, Nurses' Health Study II, Malaysia Birth Cohort Studies.

Limitation: Expensive; long follow-up; large samples; loss to follow-up; selection bias if participation/follow-up related to exposure & outcome.

Retrospective Cohort ▼

Uses historical records (pharmacy, medical, occupational, environmental monitoring). Faster and cheaper than prospective cohort.

Example (PYQ 2018/19 Q3a [R10]): Retrospective cohort studying microcephaly cases in Perlis potentially linked to consumption of illegal pickles from Thailand.

Limitation: Relies on accuracy of existing records; recall bias if supplemented by self-report; exposure misclassification.

Case-Control Study ▼

Cases = infants/mothers with outcome; Controls = unaffected. Exposure histories compared retrospectively. Efficient for rare outcomes (specific congenital malformations). [R1, Ch.4]

Major limitation: Recall bias — mothers of affected children may recall exposures differently. "Case-parent triad design" is a special variant used in genetic epidemiology [R2].

Novel Designs [R1, Ch.17; R2] ▼

Computerised "square dance" design [R2]: Allows paternal gene factor influence adjustment with maternal siblings' genes; women serve as their own controls — adjusting for confounders. Concerns: time period changes, change in partners.

Case-parent triad design (genetic epid) [R1, Ch.17]: Uses cases + both parents as genetic controls — eliminates population stratification confounding. Particularly powerful for studying maternal vs foetal genetic effects.

Time-to-pregnancy (TTP) design [R1, Ch.2]: Couples trying to conceive are followed prospectively. Each menstrual cycle is the unit of observation. Fecundability per cycle is the outcome. Efficient for fertility research.

⚠️ Methodological Issues in Reproductive Epidemiology
PYQ Context (2018/19 Q3 [R10]): Microcephaly increasing in Perlis — suspected due to illegal pickle (asam jeruk) consumption from Thailand. Design: Retrospective cohort study.
Issue 1: Defining & Selecting the Study Population [R1, Ch.4] ▼

Must include not only pregnant women but also:

  • Women of childbearing age (15–49) — may become pregnant during study
  • Family members in same household (father/spouse) — for genetic confounding
  • Frequent consumers of similar products

Unit of analysis: Mother? Child? Father? Dyad? Household? Community? — each has different implications for study validity and generalizability [R1, Ch.1].

If study population is not comprehensive: Selection bias — results not generalizable to broader population at risk.
Issue 2: Exposure Misclassification / Hidden Data [R1, Ch.6] ▼

Consumption of illegal pickles is sensitive — pregnant women may underreport due to fear of legal repercussions or social stigma → exposure misclassification bias.

  • Non-differential misclassification: Dilutes observed effect (underestimates true association)
  • Differential misclassification: Unpredictable bias direction

Solutions: Anonymous self-administered questionnaires; biomarkers of exposure; indirect questioning techniques; validate with biological markers.

Issue 3: Pregnancies as Repeated Events (Multiplicity) [R1, Ch.4] ▼

Women may have multiple pregnancies during follow-up — each pregnancy is not independent. Statistical methods must account for:

  • Within-woman correlation (repeated outcomes)
  • Changes in exposure between pregnancies
  • Changing partner (different paternal genetic contribution)
  • Period effects (calendar time changes in environment)

Solution: Generalized estimating equations (GEE) or multilevel/mixed models that account for clustering of pregnancies within women.

Issue 4: Confounding — Genetic, Social & Environmental Factors ▼

Microcephaly can have genetic causes (primary microcephaly). Familial clustering may be due to shared genetics not shared exposure. Key confounders:

  • Maternal age, parity, SES, nutritional status
  • Other dietary exposures, alcohol, smoking
  • Infections (Zika virus — differential diagnosis!) [R10, PYQ 2016/17]
  • Paternal genetic contribution
Issue 5: Survivor Bias / Left Truncation — Fetuses-at-Risk [R1, Ch.13–14] ▼

Pregnancies lost before the study window are not captured (spontaneous abortions, stillbirths). Only surviving pregnancies/births are observed — "the fetuses-at-risk" problem [R1, Ch.13].

Fetuses-at-risk approach: Uses appropriate denominators accounting for competing risks (delivery can be spontaneous or iatrogenic). Particularly important for preterm birth and fetal growth outcomes.

Issue 6: Birth Weight Paradox — Collider Bias [R1, Ch.15] ▼

Birth weight paradox [R1, Ch.15]: Among infants of smokers, LBW infants have lower mortality than LBW infants of non-smokers — paradoxical since smoking causes LBW and smoking harms infants.

This is a classic example of collider bias — conditioning on birth weight (a collider) opens a spurious path. LBW of smokers are a different population from LBW of non-smokers (different competing causes of LBW).

Lesson: Conditioning on intermediate variables (colliders) induces spurious associations. Requires careful use of directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) to avoid.

Birth weight paradoxConditioning on birth weight opens a collider path (smoking → LBW ← unmeasured cause → infant death) → reversal of apparent effect direction. Classic collider stratification bias (Ch 12).
Sensitive periodsOrganogenesis (weeks 3–8) most vulnerable. Periconceptional and first-trimester exposures require separate analysis from later pregnancy exposures.
Study designsProspective pre-conception cohorts optimal. Register-based (Scandinavian) studies allow record linkage at scale. Case-control for rare malformations.

3. Genetic Epidemiology
Sources: See references ↓
🧬 Definition & Scope
Merikangas (2012) [R3]: Genetic epidemiology is the study of the distribution and risk factors for diseases, and the genetic and environmental causes of familial resemblance. It focuses on how genetic factors and their interactions with other risk factors increase vulnerability to, or protection against, disease.
Reid-Lombardo & Petersen (2010) [R4]: "The role of genetic factors and their interaction with environmental factors in the occurrence of disease in human populations." The goal is to establish whether a genetic component exists to a disease, taking into account environmental influences as well.

Key Aims [R9]

  • Evaluate research methods used to investigate genetic influences on diseases
  • Understand gene–environment interactions in disease causation
  • Interpret genetic association results for public health

What Enabled the Field [R3, R4]

  • Human Genome Project (2001): First comprehensive DNA sequence — ~30,000 genes
  • International HapMap Project (2003): Genotype, haplotype and SNP frequencies across populations
  • Rapid fall in genotyping cost: Now <$100 to genotype 1 million SNPs
Underlying design logic [R3]: Designs either control for genetic background while letting the environment vary (migrant studies, separated twins, half-siblings) OR control for environment while allowing variance in genetic background (siblings, twins, adoptees).
🔬 Classical Study Designs
Family Studies & Pedigree Analysis [R3] ▼

Familial aggregation is generally the first source of evidence for genetic factors [R3]. The key indicator is the relative risk ratio (λ) = rate of disorder in families of affected persons ÷ rate in families of controls.

  • First-degree relatives share 50% of genes; second-degree 25%; third-degree 12.5%
  • If wholly attributable to genes: should see ~50% decrease in disease risk per increase in degree of relatedness
  • λ > 20 for most autosomal dominant diseases; λ = 2–5 for complex disorders [R3]
  • Decrease by MORE than 50% across degrees → implies more than one locus (polygenic)

Application (PYQ 2018/19 Q3c [R10]): Familial microcephaly — pedigree analysis to identify transmission pattern (autosomal recessive most likely for primary microcephaly) and carriers.

Twin Studies [R3] ▼

Compare concordance rates between monozygotic (MZ) twins (same genotype) and dizygotic (DZ) twins (~50% shared genes) [R3].

Heritability estimate: h² ≈ 2 × (ConcordanceMZ − ConcordanceDZ)

ACE model (path analysis) [R3]: Partitions phenotypic variance into:

  • A: Additive genetic effects (heritability)
  • C: Common/shared environmental effects
  • E: Unique environmental effects + measurement error

Other applications [R3]:

  • Identify genetic and environmental sources of sex differences
  • Identify environmental exposures through discordant MZ twins
  • Twin family design — yields heritability + multigenerational patterns
Limitation: "Equal environments assumption" — assumes MZ and DZ twins share environment equally. May not hold in all conditions [R3].
Adoption Studies [R3] ▼

The major source of evidence for joint contribution of genetic and environmental factors [R3]. Compare similarity between adoptee and biological relatives vs adoptive relatives.

More powerful approach: Compare biological relatives of affected adoptees vs biological relatives of unaffected control adoptees — eliminates environmental confounding.

Current limitation [R3]: Becoming less feasible due to trends toward selective adoption. Reconstituted families (full + half-siblings) offer a new avenue.

Migration Studies [R3] ▼

The most powerful design to identify environmental and cultural risk factors — holds genes relatively constant while changing environment [R3].

Classic example [R3]: Asian immigrants to USA showed significantly increased rates of cancer and heart disease — demonstrating environmental contribution to diseases previously thought to be genetic (Kolonel et al. 2004).

Major threat to validity: Selection bias — migrants may differ systematically from non-migrants in health status, SES, and personality traits. Always consider selection as alternative explanation [R3].
🗺️ Molecular Genetic Approaches — From Candidate Genes to GWAS

Key Terminology [R4]

TermDefinition
SNPSingle nucleotide polymorphism — single base-pair variation. Most common form of genetic variation. Characterised by minor allele frequency (MAF). Synonymous (no aa change) vs non-synonymous (changes amino acid) [R4].
Linkage Disequilibrium (LD)Non-random co-inheritance of nearby alleles. Allows a representative "tag SNP" to stand in for several neighbouring variants — enables GWAS efficiency [R4].
HaplotypeSet of consecutive alleles along a chromosome inherited together. HapMap Project (2003) characterised haplotype structure across populations [R4].
Minor Allele Frequency (MAF)Frequency of the less-common allele at a SNP. MAF >5% = common variant; MAF <1% = rare variant [R4].
Copy Number Variation (CNV)Structural variation — segments repeated (duplication) or absent (deletion). Not captured by SNP arrays alone [R4].
Candidate Gene Studies [R3, R4] ▼

Hypothesis-driven: Select genes a priori from suspected biological pathway. Test association between specific gene variants and disease. [R3]

Advantages: Smaller sample size; biologically grounded; cheaper.

Disadvantages: Cannot discover loci outside the hypothesis; high false-positive rate without replication; many early findings have not replicated in GWAS [R4].

Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS) [R3, R4, R5] ▼

Hypothesis-free: Scan hundreds of thousands to millions of tag SNPs. Typically case-control samples ≥1,000 pairs. Requires independent replication [R4].

Example [R4]: Colorectal cancer susceptibility locus at 8q24.21 identified by scanning 550,000 SNPs.

Statistical threshold: p < 5×10⁻⁸ (genome-wide significance) — Bonferroni correction for ~1 million SNPs [R4].

GWAS Catalog (as of 2017 [R5]): 2,854 publications; 33,674 unique SNP-trait associations.

Effect sizes [R3]: GWAS-identified common variants have modest effects — OR approximately 1.2–1.5. Most complex diseases are polygenic — many loci, each with small effect.

Most important confounder — Population Stratification [R3, R4]:
Differing allele AND disease frequencies across ancestral subgroups generate spurious associations. Solution: Principal component analysis (PCA) to adjust for ancestry; genomic control (λGC); restrict to single ancestry.
Whole Exome/Genome Sequencing (WES/WGS) [R9] ▼

WES: Sequences all protein-coding regions (~1% of genome, ~85% of disease-causing mutations). High diagnostic yield for rare Mendelian disorders [R9].

WGS: Sequences entire genome — also captures non-coding regulatory regions. More comprehensive but more expensive.

Application (PYQ Q3c [R10]): In familial microcephaly — WES identifies mutations in MCPH1, CDK5RAP2, ASPM, CENPJ associated with primary microcephaly.

Chromosomal Microarray Analysis (CMA): Detects CNVs — deletions/duplications too small for karyotyping. Often first-line test for developmental abnormalities.

Polygenic Risk Scores (PRS) [R5, R6] ▼

Aggregate effect of many variants into a single score = weighted sum of risk alleles (weights from GWAS summary statistics) [R5].

Diversity problem [R6]: Vast majority of GWAS participants are of European ancestry. PRS derived from European GWAS have limited portability to non-European populations (different LD structure, allele frequencies, environmental context). Non-European populations — including Malaysians, Africans, South Asians — receive less accurate risk estimates. This is a major health equity issue.

COVID-19 example [R7]: Human genetics studies identified chromosome 3 locus (LZTFL1, SLC6A20, CCR2), TLR7 variants in severe COVID-19 — PRS inform who is at highest risk. CCR5Δ32 (HIV protection) and sickle cell trait (malaria protection) are classical infectious disease examples.

🎲 Mendelian Randomization (MR)
Concept [R5, R6]: MR uses genetic variation to improve causal inference. A genetic variant associated with an exposure (genetic instrument) is used to test the causal relationship — exploiting random allocation of alleles at conception (like randomisation in a trial). Paternoster, Tilling & Davey Smith, PLoS Genetics 2017.

Three Core Assumptions [R5]

  1. Relevance: Genetic instrument is associated with the exposure
  2. Independence: Genetic instrument is independent of confounders of exposure-outcome
  3. Exclusion restriction: Genetic instrument affects outcome only through the exposure (no horizontal pleiotropy)

What MR Has Demonstrated [R5]

Evidence of causal relationships [R5]:
Obesity → CHD ✓
Blood pressure → CHD ✓
LDL cholesterol → coronary events ✓
Argued against causality [R5]:
C-reactive protein → CHD ✗
C-reactive protein → diabetes ✗
Moderate alcohol → CHD protective ✗
(debunked the J-curve hypothesis)
Collider Bias in MR Disease Progression Studies [R5] ▼

Collider: A variable caused by two or more other variables in a DAG. Conditioning on a collider opens a spurious path between its causes [R5].

In case-only MR for disease progression: Selecting only cases makes disease incidence a collider — inducing associations among all independent risk factors for disease within the case group.

Consequence: Violates MR assumption 2 ("instrument is independent of confounders") — conditioning on case status induces new confounding pathways.

Key finding from Paternoster et al. 2017 [R5]: Only 1 of 28 identified MR progression studies acknowledged this issue. This is a genuine unresolved special issue in genetic epidemiology.
Horizontal Pleiotropy — Violation of Exclusion Restriction [R5] ▼

Vertical pleiotropy (instrument → exposure → outcome) is acceptable in MR. Horizontal pleiotropy (instrument → outcome via pathway independent of the exposure) violates exclusion restriction [R5].

Detection methods: MR-Egger intercept test; Weighted median MR; MR-PRESSO (outlier detection); leave-one-out sensitivity analysis.

MR for Drug Target Identification [R5] ▼

GWAS of disease incidence may not identify good therapeutic targets — treatment should affect disease progression [R5]. Only ~8% of GWAS catalog associations relate to disease progression (as of 2017).

Gap: 90% of identified MR progression studies have n < 5,000 — underpowered. Investigating disease progression offers considerable opportunity for identifying treatment targets.

🔀 Gene-Environment (G×E) Interaction
Definition: Gene-environment interaction occurs when the effect of an environmental exposure on disease risk differs depending on genotype — or equivalently, the genetic effect differs by level of environmental exposure.
Autism Spectrum Disorder — G×E Example [R8] ▼

Source: "Genetic Epidemiology and Insights into Biological Psychiatry: Interactive Genetic and Environmental Effects in ASD" [R8]

  • ASD heritability: h² ~80% from twin studies [R8] — but not fully genetic
  • Rare de novo mutations contribute significantly; higher rate with increasing paternal age
  • Environmental risk factors: advanced parental age, prenatal valproate, maternal infection, air pollution
  • G×E: prenatal environment effects may depend on genetic susceptibility (e.g., SHANK3 mutations)
  • PRS for ASD explains only ~5–11% of variance — large "missing heritability"
Public health implication: Even for highly heritable disorders, environmental factors remain actionable. G×E interaction studies identify which genetically susceptible individuals are most vulnerable to specific exposures — enabling targeted prevention.
Detecting G×E Interaction [R3, R6] ▼

Statistical model: logit(p) = β₁G + β₂E + β₃G×E. β₃ = the interaction term.

Scales of interaction:

  • Additive scale: Excess risk attributable to interaction (RERI, AP, SI) — preferred for public health (absolute risk) [R3]
  • Multiplicative scale: Interaction term in logistic regression — for biological mechanisms

Challenge: Requires very large sample sizes (~4× compared to main effects); gene-environment independence assumption must hold.

COVID-19 Host Genetic Epidemiology [R7] ▼

Source: Niemi MEK et al. (COVID-19 Host Genetics Initiative). Nature Reviews Genetics 2022 [R7]

Key genetic findings:

  • Chromosome 3 locus (LZTFL1, SLC6A20, CCR2) — most strongly associated with severe COVID-19
  • TLR7 loss-of-function variants → severe COVID-19 in young men (X-linked); impairs interferon response
  • IFNAR2 variants → COVID-19 severity (interferon receptor pathway)
  • ABO blood group — O blood group associated with lower infection risk
  • CCR5Δ32 → HIV protection; sickle cell trait → malaria protection (classic examples) [R7]

Study designs used: Case-control GWAS; biobank cohort linkage; rare variant studies; multi-ancestry consortia (COVID-19 HGI) [R7].

Outbreak Investigation in Hospital Setting [H3] ▼

PYQ context (GET513 2020/21 Q3 [H3]): An outbreak of Klebsiella pneumoniae ESBL-producing in a neonatal ICU. Steps:

  1. Verify diagnosis — confirm cases are genuine; confirm laboratory ID of organism and resistance pattern (ESBL); rule out pseudo-outbreak (lab contamination)
  2. Confirm the outbreak — compare observed rate to expected (baseline historical rate); if observed > expected → outbreak
  3. Define a case — case definition (person, place, time): e.g., "any neonate admitted to NICU from [date] with positive culture of Klebsiella pneumoniae ESBL from blood/CSF/urine"
  4. Identify and count cases — line listing; descriptive epidemiology (who, where, when)
  5. Draw epidemic curve — common source vs propagated; point source vs continuous source; helps determine exposure period
  6. Generate hypotheses — possible source: HCW hands, contaminated equipment, breast milk, IV fluids; transmission route
  7. Test hypotheses — case-control or cohort study among NICU patients; environmental sampling; HCW carriage screening
  8. Implement control measures — contact precautions; cohorting; enhanced hand hygiene; equipment decontamination; antibiotic review
  9. Communicate findings — report to hospital management, MOH, if required; publish if novel or significant
  10. Evaluate control measures — monitor for new cases; declare outbreak over when 2× maximum incubation period passes with no new cases

⚠️ Challenges in Hospital Infection Prevention & Control
Methodological Challenges in HAI Surveillance ▼
  • Case ascertainment: HAIs are underdiagnosed — passive surveillance under-reports; active surveillance resource-intensive; no universal registry in Malaysia
  • Standardisation of definitions: CDC/NHSN definitions updated regularly; different hospitals use different diagnostic criteria → comparability issues
  • Denominator accuracy: Patient-days and device-days require accurate recording; incomplete data inflates or deflates rates
  • Confounding by case-mix: Higher HAI rates in ICU reflect patient severity, not necessarily poor IPC. Must risk-adjust when benchmarking
  • Attribution: Distinguishing community-acquired vs hospital-acquired infection (especially for 48-hour rule) is not always clear
Operational & Systemic Challenges ▼
  • Hand hygiene compliance: Remains the single most effective IPC measure, yet compliance rarely exceeds 60–70% globally despite campaigns; Hawthorne effect during audits
  • Infrastructure: Overcrowding, insufficient isolation rooms, inadequate ventilation, inadequate sink-to-bed ratio — endemic in many Malaysian public hospitals
  • Antibiotic stewardship resistance: Clinician buy-in for AMS is variable; prescribing culture varies by specialty; fear of under-treating
  • AMR organisms: Multidrug-resistant organisms (MRSA, ESBL-producers, CRE, Acinetobacter) are increasingly prevalent — limit treatment options; costly to control
  • Staffing: Shortage of trained IPC nurses and hospital epidemiologists relative to hospital bed capacity
  • COVID-19 lessons: PPE supply chain disruption; healthcare cohorting challenges; aerosol transmission uncertainty; need for resilient IPC frameworks

Vaccine Efficacy (VE) — definition [V3]:
VE = [(IU − IV) / IU] × 100% = (1 − RR) × 100%
IU = incidence rate in unvaccinated; IV = incidence rate in vaccinated
WHO minimum target for COVID-19 vaccines: lower bound of 95% CI > 30% [V3]
Efficacy vs Effectiveness [V2]: Efficacy = performance under ideal controlled trial conditions. Effectiveness = performance in real-world settings (lower due to: cold chain issues, heterogeneous populations, waning immunity, emerging variants, programme delivery).
Why vaccine trials are uniquely complex [V2, V4]: Trials in healthy individuals; very large sample sizes needed to observe enough cases; ethical issues with placebo in high-risk settings; emerging variants alter the endpoint; pandemic timelines compress development.

🔬 Traditional vs Adaptive Trial Designs [V2, V3]
Traditional Fixed Design [V2] ▼

Conventional Phase 3 RCTs adopt a parallel, two-group, fixed-allocation-ratio design analysed with frequentist methods at a single pre-specified endpoint [V2].

  • Sample size and timing of analysis fixed at design stage based on predicted event rates
  • No modifications allowed once trial starts
  • Single pre-specified primary endpoint and analysis time point
  • Statistical test at one time point — controls Type I error straightforwardly

Limitation: Inflexible; cannot respond to emerging pandemic data; may continue unnecessarily if efficacy overwhelming or futility evident.

Adaptive Trial Designs [V2, V4] ▼
Definition [V2]: Adaptive designs allow pre-planned modifications to trial parameters (sample size, allocation ratio, stopping early for efficacy/futility) based on accumulating data, while maintaining validity and integrity.

Five COVID-19 Phase 3 RCTs incorporated adaptive methods, enabling trials to stop early when vaccine efficacy was declared [V2].

Sample size re-estimationAdjust N at interim based on observed event rates, without unblinding treatment allocationIf COVID-19 incidence lower than expected, enrol more to maintain power

Statistical Issues — Interim Analyses & Multiple Testing [V3] ▼

Problem (Hofner et al. 2023 [V3]): The fast accrual of COVID-19 cases led to multiple data updates within short timeframes — each update constitutes an additional "look" at the data, inflating Type I error.

IssueExplanationSolution
Type I error inflationEach interim analysis is an additional opportunity to find a "significant" result by chanceAlpha spending functions (O'Brien-Fleming, Lan-DeMets); pre-specify number and timing of interim analyses
Confirmatory vs updated analysesRolling reviews meant additional efficacy estimates were submitted post-conditional approval — these "updated" analyses were not all confirmatoryHofner et al. propose standardised terminology: "confirmatory analysis" vs "updated estimates" should be clearly distinguished
Confidence interval widthEarly interim analyses based on few events → very wide CIs around VE estimates (uncertainty high)Hofner et al. recommend reporting CIs around updated VE to convey uncertainty; not just point estimate

Bayesian Methods & Estimand Framework [V2] ▼

Bayesian approaches in vaccine trials [V2]: Combine prior knowledge (from preclinical, Phase 1/2 data, related vaccines) with observed trial data to update the probability of efficacy. More flexible — can give probability that VE > threshold.

  • Allows borrowing of information from historical trials or platform data
  • Prior can be informative (strong prior from closely related vaccine) or weakly informative (uncertainty)
  • Output is posterior probability — e.g., P(VE > 50%) = 0.99 — more intuitively meaningful than p-value

Estimand framework [V2]: Clarifies the exact question each endpoint is trying to answer — ensures the statistical analysis directly addresses the primary question of interest. Four components: population, treatment, endpoint, intercurrent events handling.

⚠️ Challenges in Vaccine Efficacy Trials for Emerging Infectious Diseases [V4]
Source: Madewell et al. 2021 (Epidemics) [V4]: Outbreaks of emerging pathogens pose unique methodological and practical challenges for the design, implementation, and evaluation of vaccine efficacy trials.
Challenge 1: Unpredictable Incidence & Waning Transmission [V4] ▼

Unlike endemic diseases, emerging infectious disease outbreaks are unpredictable in timing, location, and duration. Sites with reliable incidence may not exist. A vaccine trial may be planned but the outbreak subsides before enough cases accrue to power the trial.

Solutions [V4]:

  • Ring vaccination trial design — follows the epidemic as it moves geographically
  • Adaptive sample size re-estimation based on observed incidence
  • Multi-site trials in multiple countries to maximise case capture
  • Mathematical modelling to predict epidemic trajectory and optimal trial timing/location
Challenge 2: Ethical Issues — Placebo Controls [V4, V5] ▼

In a high-mortality outbreak, withholding an experimental (but promising) vaccine from the control group is ethically contentious. The standard "placebo-controlled" design may be unacceptable to IRBs and communities.

Solutions [V4]:

  • Delayed vaccination design (immediate vs deferred) — all participants eventually vaccinated
  • Stepped-wedge design — all clusters eventually get vaccine; controls serve as controls only until their turn
  • Ring vaccination — no participant with known exposure is left unvaccinated beyond the study period
  • Active control (another licensed vaccine) instead of placebo — for diseases with available but incomplete protection
Special ethical issue: Paediatric trials [V5]: Most vaccines target children — but children cannot consent. Kulkarni (PCR-4-80) highlights: parental consent challenges especially in LMICs; hostel warden consent controversy (India HPV vaccine); CIOMS guideline requires demonstrating that research "cannot be equally well carried out in adults" before involving children.
Challenge 3: Emergency Use Authorisation (EUA) vs Full Approval [V3, V4] ▼

EUA streamlines regulatory approval to enable rapid deployment. However [V4]:

  • Once a vaccine is approved under EUA, using placebo controls in further trials becomes ethically impossible — slows evidence generation for new variants or dose regimens
  • EUA based on shorter follow-up → unknown long-term efficacy and safety
  • Political pressure to hasten authorisation may release a potentially less efficacious or harmful vaccine
  • Expedited trials → higher vaccine hesitancy due to perceived lack of rigour

COVID-19 EMA approach [V3]: Conditional Marketing Authorization (CMA) — allows authorization on less complete data with obligation to provide further data within specified timelines. Rolling reviews enabled real-time assessment during development.

Challenge 4: Equity, Access & Global Distribution [V1b] ▼

Source: Agampodi et al. 2024 [V1b]: COVID-19 vaccination efforts revealed critical shortcomings in achieving equitable vaccine access.

  • High-income countries (HICs) contracted and received the majority of early vaccine doses
  • COVAX facility aimed at 20% coverage in LMICs by end of 2021 — fell short significantly
  • African continent: received vaccines latest; lowest vaccination rates; limited local manufacturing
  • Intellectual property (IP) rights: voluntary IP licensing and TRIPs waiver debates — delayed technology transfer to LMIC manufacturers
  • Cold chain: mRNA vaccines require ultra-cold storage (−20°C to −70°C) — major challenge in LMICs with poor infrastructure
  • Misinformation: rampant vaccine hesitancy fuelled by social media — particularly during rapid deployment
Future recommendations [V1b]: Cultivate leadership in vaccinology; guarantee equitable access to diagnostics/therapeutics/vaccines; secure adequate R&D funding; ensure fair distribution; strategically allocate biomedical manufacturing facilities globally — especially Africa and Asia.

VE Statistical Models [V3] ▼
  • Binomial/Poisson model: Events per arm follow Poisson distribution with fixed rates; number of events conditional on total events follows binomial → simple test for VE. Adjusted for follow-up time as offset.
  • Cox proportional hazards: Models time-to-event; handles variable follow-up naturally; yields hazard ratio → VE = 1 − HR. Standard for most modern Phase 3 trials.
  • Negative binomial regression: Count model allowing overdispersion; used when event counts cluster.
  • Logistic regression: For binary outcomes; yields OR → approximates RR when disease rare; VE = 1 − OR.

⚠️ Special Methodological Issues in Cancer Epidemiology
Latency, Induction Period & Time-Windowed Exposure ▼

Most cancers have long latency periods (10–40 years between exposure and detectable disease). This creates methodological challenges:

  • Latency window analysis: Different exposure time windows (e.g., 0–5, 5–10, 10–20 years before diagnosis) are tested to identify the etiologically relevant window
  • Induction period: Time from initiation to promotion to detectable cancer — longer for initiators than promoters
  • Immortal time bias: In cohort studies, if exposure status requires patients to "survive" to a certain time to be classified as exposed — survival advantage falsely attributed to exposure
  • Preclinical disease effect: Subclinical cancer may alter behaviour/diet before diagnosis — reverse causation in case-control studies
Cancer Registry & Surveillance ▼
  • Population-based cancer registries (PBCRs): Capture all new cancer diagnoses in a defined geographic area. Gold standard for incidence data. Malaysia: National Cancer Registry (NCR) under MOH — publishes Malaysian Cancer Statistics.
  • Hospital-based cancer registries: Capture cases attending a specific hospital — useful for clinical research but not population-based rates
  • Standardised Incidence Ratio (SIR): Observed cases ÷ expected cases (based on general population rates) × 100 — standard measure in occupational cancer cohort studies
  • Age-standardised rate (ASR): Allows comparison across populations with different age structures — World Standard Population used by IARC/GLOBOCAN

♿ Disabilities Research — Definition & Scope
Lesson Plan (Dr Nor Azwany Yaacob, USM [D1]): Seminar covers: (1) Definition of disability; (2) Rationale of research on disability; (3) Scope of disability research; (4) Challenges of disability research.
Main Resource [D2]: Rios D, Magasi S, Novak C, Harniss M. "Conducting Accessible Research: Including People With Disabilities in Public Health, Epidemiological, and Outcomes Studies." Am J Public Health. 2016;106:2137–2144.

Definition of Disability

  • Functional impairment model: 54–60 million Americans live with disabilities — one of the largest minority groups
  • Disability rates increase with age: 2.3% (<3 yr) → 12.2% (school-age) → 21% (15+ yr) → ~50% (65+ yr)
  • Disability is a "near-universal experience" across the lifespan [D2]
  • ICF (International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health, WHO): Disability as interaction between health condition and contextual factors (environmental, personal)

Why Research on Disability Matters [D2]

  • People with disabilities (PWD) experience poorer health, greater chronic conditions, higher healthcare expenditure
  • Adults with disabilities: ~$400 billion/year in healthcare costs (US) — ~25% of total healthcare expenditure
  • Children with disabilities: 4× the healthcare expenses of non-disabled peers
  • PWD largely excluded from mainstream health research — explicit or implicit exclusion
  • NIH mandates inclusion of women and minorities — but NOT yet disability
🔬 Conducting Accessible Research — Including PWD [D2]
Problem (Rios et al. 2016 [D2]): Exclusion of PWD from research is often: (a) explicit — poorly justified exclusion criteria; or (b) implicit — inaccessible study documents, interventions, or measures. Both reduce external validity and equity.
Why PWD Are Excluded — and Why That's Wrong [D2] ▼

Explicit exclusion: Eligibility criteria that exclude PWD without scientific justification (e.g., "able to walk 10 metres" for a dietary study).

Implicit exclusion: Study instruments not accessible (print-only surveys for visual impairment; audio-only for hearing impairment; cognitive burden in questionnaires). Physical inaccessibility of research sites. Use of outcome measures that lack validity for PWD (e.g., SF-36 not validated in motor disability).

Consequence: Research findings cannot be meaningfully translated to the PWD population — major threat to external validity. PWD as one of the greatest potential beneficiaries of healthcare services remain under-represented as research participants.
Accessible Research Design Model — 3 Tiers [D2] ▼

Rios et al. (2016) propose a 3-tier model for accessible research design:

TierDescriptionExample
Tier 1: Universal DesignDesign research to be accessible to ALL participants from the outset — proactive, not reactiveElectronic data collection with screen-reader compatibility; plain language consent forms; accessible research venues; flexible scheduling
Tier 2: AccommodationsStandard adjustments available to all who need them, without requiring individual special requestsOffering large-print versions of all documents; telephone interview option; sign language interpreter available
Tier 3: ModificationsIndividualized changes to study procedures for specific participants when Tiers 1–2 insufficientAltering a specific assessment procedure for a participant with severe motor impairment; surrogate reporter for cognitive impairment
Application: Making these efforts will "enhance the interpretability of findings for a significant patient population" — improves external validity and equity of health research.
Methodological Challenges in Disability Epidemiology [D4] ▼

Source: Scopus AI synthesis (2026) [D4]

  • Measurement heterogeneity: No universally accepted, harmonised tool to measure disability across studies and countries — limits meta-analysis and cross-national comparison
  • Attrition in longitudinal studies: PWD have higher dropout rates, illness-related withdrawal, and death — leads to survivor bias; limits follow-up data quality
  • Cross-sectional limitation: Most disability studies are cross-sectional — cannot establish temporality or causality between exposures and disability outcomes
  • Confounding by disease severity: Disability is closely linked to underlying disease — difficult to separate "disability" effect from the disease itself
  • Socioeconomic disparities: SES (education, income, rurality) modifies disability onset, progression, and recovery — must be addressed as confounder or effect modifier
  • LMIC data gap: Limited genomic/biomarker data; insufficient evidence on early-life interventions; high-consanguinity populations understudied

🔭 Spectrum of Ageing Research [D3]
Campisi et al. (2019, Nature [D3a]): Environmental and genetic factors exert influences on key cellular processes and pathways — the "hallmarks of ageing." These pathways create a chronic inflammatory state → increase risk for chronic diseases of ageing, together with disease-specific risk factors.
Biological Hallmarks of Ageing (Campisi et al. 2019) [D3a] ▼

Source: Campisi et al. "From discoveries in ageing research to therapeutics for healthy ageing." Nature 2019;571:183–192. [D3a]

  • Key initial finding: Caloric restriction in mice/rats (1939) increased lifespan — foundational discovery
  • Gene variants extending lifespan identified in C. elegans (late 1980s) — gave biological credibility to ageing research
  • Hallmarks of ageing: Genomic instability, telomere attrition, epigenetic alterations, loss of proteostasis, deregulated nutrient sensing, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence, stem cell exhaustion, altered intercellular communication
  • Cellular senescence → creates senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) → chronic inflammation ("inflammageing")
  • mTOR pathway (nutrient sensing) — rapamycin inhibition extends lifespan in mice; clinical trials in humans underway
  • Senolytics (drugs targeting senescent cells): dasatinib, quercetin — now in clinical trials for age-related disease

⚠️ Challenges in Ageing Research [D3]
Challenge 1: Evidence Base Derived from Younger Populations (Studenski 2008) [D3] ▼

Evidence for care of older adults is often derived from studies of younger, less complex populations — leading to discrepancy between the evidence base and its clinical application in older patients [D3].

  • Traditional study designs designed for isolated diseases — do not work well for complex, multisystem issues in ageing
  • As chronic illnesses accumulate with age, research must focus on interactions between ageing, diseases, geriatric syndromes, and outcomes like function and quality of life
  • RCTs commonly exclude participants over 70–75 — limits applicability of trial results to clinical practice
Challenge 2: Longevity Clinical Trial Design (Sehgal & Raghav 2022) [D3] ▼
  • Exclusion criteria paradox: Longevity trials often exclude older adults or those with multiple comorbidities — counterproductive as these are the people most likely to benefit
  • Intervention heterogeneity: Longevity interventions range from pharmaceuticals to supplements and lifestyle changes — difficult to compare across trials
  • Outcome measurement: What is the appropriate outcome? Lifespan? Healthspan? Functional status? Biomarkers? — No consensus
  • Translational failure: Many interventions promising in animal models fail in humans — genetic and environmental differences between species
  • Follow-up duration: Need very long follow-up to observe lifespan outcomes — expensive, high attrition, cohort effects over time

Biological Pathways Linking Ageing, Disability & Chronic Disease [D4] ▼

Source: Scopus AI synthesis [D4]

  • Chronic inflammation ("inflammageing"): Elevated IL-6, CRP, TNF-α with ageing → muscle wasting (sarcopenia), cognitive decline, frailty, cardiovascular disease
  • Metabolic disturbances: Insulin resistance, dyslipidaemia accumulate with age → increased disability; hyperleptinaemia linked to physical disability
  • Neurovascular dysfunction: Cerebral small vessel disease links physical disability and cognitive decline — shared pathway
  • Sarcopenia: Age-related loss of muscle mass and function — directly causes functional disability; shares biological pathways with osteoporosis (osteosarcopenia)

🧬 Risk Factors: Genetic, Social & Environmental
Genetic Epidemiology [S2,S4] ▼

Family history is the most potent and consistent risk factor for drug use disorders. [S2]

  • Family studies: Drug use disorders prevalent in families; first-degree relatives and offspring of probands with drug disorders have elevated risk — both across drug classes and for specific drug classes.
  • Twin studies: Significant genetic contribution to familial clustering (monozygotic > dizygotic concordance).
  • Adoption studies: Genetic factors independent of shared environment.
Key distinction: Genetics have a stronger impact on the development of drug use disorders (addiction) than on initial drug use. Environmental factors are more crucial for preventing onset of use; genetic vulnerability manifests more in progression to dependence. [S2,S4]

Complexity: Drug use disorders are polygenic — arise from multiple genes with small effects, gene×gene interactions, and gene×environment (G×E) interactions. Specific causal genes have not yet been identified. Large prospective genetically-informative community studies are needed. [S2]

Social Epidemiology [S2,S4] ▼

Most research has historically focused on individual-level risk factors using additive models in a single domain. A more comprehensive approach requires examining multiple interrelated factors and their interactions across levels. [S2]

A near-universal finding: drug use increases from adolescence to young adulthood, then gradually declines. Adolescence marks the key risk window. [S2]

  • Adolescents who began alcohol use before age 14 had a 40% higher risk of substance abuse problems in early adulthood [S1]

  • Vulnerability factors in youth: curiosity, susceptibility to peer pressure, poor self-worth, risk-taking behaviour [S1]
Prevention implication: Neuroimaging and developmental research show adolescence is a period of active brain development (cognitive/emotional). Assessing gonadarche, adrenarche, and pubertal growth alongside drug use onset can clarify biological timing of risk. [S4]

🔬 Clinical & Genetic Epidemiology
Gateway Hypothesis [S2,S4] ▼

Observation that initial use of cigarettes or alcohol may lead to marijuana use, which may then lead to use of other drugs (hallucinogens, cocaine, heroin).

Controversy: The gateway hypothesis remains debated. Two competing explanations:
(1) Gateway substances cause later drug use through pharmacological priming.
(2) An underlying tendency (common liability) to use drugs of all sorts — the gateway drug merely reflects this predisposition. [S2,S4]

Mixed empirical support. However, the sequence (tobacco/alcohol → marijuana → hard drugs) is consistently observed epidemiologically, regardless of causal mechanism.

Prevention Trials & Early Childhood Interventions [S2,S4] ▼

Clinical epidemiology uses randomised field trials to test hypothesised mechanisms in prevention studies. Key examples:

  • Kellam & Anthony: Boys in a 2-year behaviour-improving classroom program were less likely to begin smoking in early adolescence compared to controls.
  • Hawkins et al.: Early childhood interventions focusing on school and family bonding had long-lasting effects in delaying drug use onset.
These studies simultaneously provide potentially useful prevention interventions AND rigorous tests of etiological hypotheses about drug use risk factors — a dual function of clinical epidemiology. [S2]

Study Design — Prospective Cohort Study

A prospective cohort study is the most appropriate design because:

  • Establishes clear temporal sequence between hazardous gas exposure and health outcomes (supports causal inference)
  • Enables investigation of multiple health outcomes from a single exposure (respiratory disease, neurological, cardiovascular, cancer)
  • Well-suited for capturing long-term effects and latency periods (years to decades)
  • Allows calculation of Relative Risk (RR) or Hazard Ratio (HR) to quantify strength of association
  • Can assess dose-response relationships across different exposure gradients
Cohort Selection
Exposed Group

Individuals directly exposed to hazardous gases during the March 2019 incident: residents within a defined proximity to the dumping site, workers at nearby facilities, emergency responders.

Inclusion Criteria

Individuals residing within a defined proximity to the dumping site (e.g., ≤2km); workers employed at facilities near the site during the incident; emergency responders involved in the aftermath.

Exclusion Criteria

Pre-existing health conditions that may mimic/exacerbate symptoms; those who relocated immediately after (no prolonged exposure); those unable to provide informed consent.

Unexposed (Control) Group

Individuals from a similar geographic area/community not exposed to hazardous gases. Matched on age, gender, socioeconomic status, baseline health status to reduce confounding.

Exposure Assessment
  • Identify and characterize specific hazardous gases released (environmental monitoring, chemical composition analysis, toxicology consultation)
  • Assess routes of exposure: inhalation, dermal contact, ingestion
  • Deploy monitoring equipment to measure concentrations in air, water, and soil
  • Collect biological samples (blood, urine, hair) for biomarkers of exposure (specific metabolites/adducts)
  • Document duration and frequency of exposure; account for variability (wind patterns, seasonal changes, PPE use)
  • Acknowledge challenges: exposure misclassification; difficulty capturing cumulative exposure retrospectively; individual variation in metabolism
Follow-Up Strategy
  • Implement health surveillance systems — collaborate with local health authorities, hospitals, registries
  • Access medical records, disease registries, mortality databases
  • Account for long latency periods (cancers, neurological disorders may take years to manifest)
  • Strategies to minimize loss to follow-up: regular contact, maintain updated contact info, use national cancer registry linkage
  • Document percentage and demographics of those lost to follow-up in reporting
Outcome Assessment
  • Define specific health outcomes: respiratory diseases (chronic bronchitis, asthma), cardiovascular disorders, neurological effects, reproductive health issues, cancer
  • Focus on chronic effects considering latency periods
  • Standardized diagnostic criteria for case ascertainment
  • Minimize observer bias: standardize measurement protocols, train assessors, blind assessors to exposure status
Analysis
  • Calculate incidence rates in exposed vs unexposed groups
  • Estimate RR or HR using Cox proportional hazards regression
  • Multivariable analysis to adjust for confounders (SES, lifestyle, smoking, healthcare access)
  • Survival analysis for time-to-event outcomes
  • Stratified analysis for vulnerable subgroups (children, elderly)
Limitations to acknowledge: Exposure misclassification; recall bias for historical exposure; loss to follow-up; healthy survivor effect; confounding by other environmental exposures; expensive and labour-intensive; long study duration.

A cohort study is considered the most scientifically rigorous observational design for establishing cause-effect relationships between exposure and cancer. It can be prospective (following forward from exposure) or retrospective (using historical data).

Q4 Vaccine Trials (AFIQ) 25 marks
Discuss challenges and methodological options in vaccine efficacy trials during public health emergencies. (25 marks)
Marks guide (25 marks): Introduction (~3) + Study design challenges + methodological solutions (~8) + Analytical challenges + solutions (~8) + Estimating VE challenges + solutions (~4) + Conclusion (~2) — each challenge-solution pair ~2-3 marks
Introduction

Vaccine efficacy trials during public health emergencies face unique challenges that affect trial processes and result interpretation. These include rapid development needs, ethical dilemmas, diverse population requirements, and analytical complexities. Rigorous trial design, adaptive methodologies, and robust analytical approaches are essential to generate reliable data for informed public health decisions.

Study Design Challenges & Methodological Solutions
Challenge 1: Shortened Timelines → Solution: Adaptive Trial Design

Shortened timelines limit preliminary studies. Adaptive trial designs allow modifications (sample size, treatment regimens) based on interim results; add new candidates or drop ineffective ones. Increases flexibility and speed of identifying effective vaccines.

Challenge 2: Ethical Dilemmas (Placebo Group) → Solution: Crossover Design

Withholding vaccine from controls during high-mortality outbreak raises ethical concerns. Crossover design — participants switch from control to vaccine arm after a period — ensures all eventually receive vaccine while still collecting control data.

Challenge 3: Non-Representative Population → Solution: Multinational & Multicentre Trials

Rapid enrolment risks non-diverse populations. Multinational/multicentre trials capture broad, diverse demographics — increases generalizability to global population.

Analytical Challenges & Solutions
Variable Follow-Up Times → Cox Proportional Hazards Model

Staggered enrolment creates variable follow-up periods. Cox model accommodates different follow-up times — compares hazard rates between vaccinated and unvaccinated without requiring consistent follow-up duration.

High Incidence Rates → Multiple Interim Analyses

Rapid case accumulation may skew early efficacy estimates. Multiple interim analyses update efficacy estimates as more data accumulates — more accurate and reliable estimates over time.

Loss to Follow-Up / Missing Data → Multiple Imputation + ITT Analysis

Chaotic emergency environment increases missing data. Multiple imputation + sensitivity analysis assesses impact of missing data. Intention-to-treat (ITT) analysis includes all participants as originally assigned — maintains randomization benefits and reduces bias.

Challenges in Estimating Vaccine Efficacy
Changing Epidemiology & Emerging Variants

New variants can render efficacy estimates outdated. Continuous epidemiological monitoring + variant-specific sub-analyses address this.

Control Group Contamination

Control participants may seek vaccine outside the trial — reduces observed difference. Rigorous monitoring + ITT analysis + per-protocol analysis as complement to address contamination.

Conclusion

These challenges require a multi-pronged approach: adaptive designs for flexibility; ethical crossover designs; diverse enrolment; robust statistical methods (Cox model, ITT, multiple imputation); and continuous monitoring of evolving epidemiology. Together, these ensure vaccine efficacy trials during emergencies produce reliable, actionable data for pandemic control.

Additional points (from lecture notes): Uncertainty in recruitment of participants, follow-up period, and endpoint criterion. Randomization and blinding (unit: area). Interim analysis at certain points to add, drop or adjust the intervention.

Marks guide (20 marks): Definition (~2) + Types (~4) + Goals (4–5 points, ~5) + Data collection sources (~5) + Issues/challenges (~4)
Definition

A cancer registry is an ongoing, systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of health data specifically related to cancer. It plays a crucial role in public health practice by providing comprehensive data on cancer incidence, types, stages, treatment, and outcomes — closely integrated with timely dissemination of information to healthcare providers, researchers, and policymakers.

Types
  • Hospital-Based Registry: Collects data on cancer patients seen at a specific hospital. Captures treatment and outcomes data. Useful for evaluating quality of care at institutional level.
  • Population-Based Registry: Collects data on ALL new cancer cases in a defined geographic population. Calculates true incidence rates. Gold standard for epidemiological studies.
  • State Cancer Registries (Malaysia): 13 state + 2 federal territory registries. Population-based; report to the National Cancer Registry (NCR).
  • National Cancer Registry (NCR): Established in Malaysia in 1993. Publishes the Malaysia National Cancer Registry Report (MNCR) based on data from state registries.
Goals
  • Monitor cancer incidence & trends: Track new cancer cases over time; identify increases/decreases in specific cancers
  • Assess cancer mortality: Monitor cancer-related deaths; assess effectiveness of cancer control measures
  • Evaluate treatment & outcomes: Analyze treatment patterns and patient outcomes; identify best practices
  • Facilitate research: Provide data for research on cancer aetiology, prevention, and treatment
  • Support public health planning & policy: Inform resource allocation, screening programme planning, public health campaigns
  • Improve prevention & early detection: Identify gaps in screening utilization; monitor effectiveness of early detection efforts
  • Enhance public awareness: Disseminate information on cancer incidence, mortality, survival rates
Data Collection Sources
  • Public & private hospitals: Standardized cancer notification forms; first contact for cancer patients
  • Pathology laboratories: Definitive diagnosis, histological characteristics, staging — most accurate source
  • Cancer treatment centers: Treatment types, duration, intensity, response — tracks patient progress and survival
  • Death registries: Cancer-related mortality from death certificates — cancer mortality rates and trends
Issues & Challenges
  • Timeliness: Delays in case notification hinder prompt monitoring of cancer trends
  • Acceptability & simplicity: Complex notification process reduces compliance; simplifying coding increases reporting rates
  • Limitations in coverage: Gaps in data — especially in remote areas; cases in traditional/alternative medicine settings not captured
  • Lack of mandatory reporting: Malaysia has no mandatory reporting requirement → underreporting and incomplete data

Q1 Environmental Health (TIONG) 30 marks
Scenario: Dumping of waste in Sungai Kim Kim. Discuss the research methodologies to determine the long-term effect of exposure to hazardous material using appropriate study design. (30 marks)
Marks guide (30 marks): Introduction + objectives (~4) + Study design justification (~4) + Setting & participants + criteria (~5) + Exposure assessment + data sources (~5) + Follow-up strategy (~4) + Statistical methods (~4) + Ethics & strengths/limitations (~4)
Introduction & Background

The Sungai Kim Kim incident involved seven chemicals including benzene, which can cause cancer (leukaemia) and has long-term health implications. The long-term health effects of exposure remain uncertain. A systematic epidemiological study is needed to investigate these effects in the exposed population.

Primary Objective: To determine the incidence of cancer (specifically leukaemia) in primary school students exposed to benzene near Sungai Kim Kim compared to a control group.

Secondary Objective: To assess the association between benzene exposure levels and other potential health outcomes (COPD, mental health impacts).

Study Design — Prospective Cohort Study

Prospective cohort study is chosen because:

  • Establishes temporality — exposure documented prospectively before outcomes develop
  • Allows assessment of multiple health outcomes from a single exposure
  • Appropriate for studying long-term effects with long latency periods (cancer)
  • Inclusion of unexposed comparison group enhances validity
Setting & Participants
Exposed Group

Students aged 7–12 years attending primary schools within 2 km radius of Sungai Kim Kim for at least 5 years, with no known pre-existing medical illnesses.

Unexposed Group

Students aged 7–12 years attending primary schools in a rural area of Johor with no known industrial activity or environmental contamination — matched by age and sex to the exposed group.

Key Variables

Exposure: Residence proximity to Sungai Kim Kim, duration of residence, urinary benzene levels.
Outcomes: Confirmed diagnoses of cancer (leukaemia), COPD, mental health conditions (DASS-21).

Data Collection
  • Informed consent from parents/guardians before enrolment
  • Baseline health assessments (medical history, physical exam) + blood tests (FBC, renal profile, LFT)
  • Quarterly environmental monitoring of benzene air concentration at schools and residential areas
  • Annual urinary benzene samples from participants
  • Follow-up questionnaires at 5-year and 10-year points — sociodemographic info, additional benzene exposure sources, lifestyle risk factors, DASS-21
  • Quality control: standardized protocols; double data entry; blind coding of biomarker samples
Follow-Up Strategy
  • Active follow-up through school visits for annual urine sampling
  • Phone calls, home visits, or postal mail for absentees — minimize loss to follow-up
  • Plan for 40% attrition rate in sample size calculation
  • Data anonymized and stored securely in password-protected database with regular backups
Statistical Methods
  • Descriptive statistics to summarize participant characteristics and exposure levels
  • Cox proportional hazards regression: Estimate HR for developing cancer (leukaemia) in exposed vs control group — adjusting for confounders (sociodemographic factors, smoking/alcohol consumption)
  • Logistic regression: Assess associations between benzene exposure levels and other outcomes (COPD, mental health)
  • DASS-21 for mental health screening
Ethics & Strengths/Limitations
  • Ethics: IRB approval; informed consent in local language; confidentiality; right to withdraw
  • Dissemination: Peer-reviewed publications; scientific conferences; community reports in local language
  • Strengths: Prospective design supports causality; multiple outcomes; unexposed comparison group; multifaceted exposure assessment; long-term follow-up
  • Limitations: Potential loss to follow-up over 10 years; self-report bias; difficulty isolating specific effects of benzene from co-exposures; expensive and labour-intensive
Q2 Occupational Health (TIONG) 10 marks
Describe how to minimize Healthy Worker Effect (HWE). (10 marks)
Marks guide (10 marks): Definition of HWE (~2) + 4 strategies with examples (2 marks each) = 8 marks. Total ~10 marks.
Definition of HWE

The Healthy Worker Effect (HWE) is a form of selection bias/confounding resulting from improper choice of a reference group (e.g., comparing employed workers to the general population). In contrast to the general population (heterogeneous health), the employed workforce tends to have fewer sick people — leading to underestimation of occupational health risks. Most studies indicate HWE will reduce the observed association by 20–30%.

Strategies to Minimize HWE
1. Avoid Using General Population as Reference Group (use internal comparator)

Using the general population as a reference exaggerates HWE because it includes non-workers who may be in poor health. Instead, compare to active workers from another industry without the same exposure. Example: Studying coal miners exposed to coal dust — compare health outcomes to construction workers (similar physical demands, different exposures).

2. Start Study After a Latency Period (1–5 years)

Initiating follow-up after a latency period excludes the healthiest workers who might leave early, reducing the initial disparity. Example: Asbestos study in shipyard workers — start follow-up only after workers have been employed for at least 5 years.

3. Dichotomize Exposed Cohort into Heavily vs Lightly Exposed

Breaking down the cohort based on exposure levels helps understand dose-response relationship and controls for HWE within the same cohort. Example: Chemical plant workers — high-exposure vs low-exposure subgroups — compare respiratory disease rates between these groups.

4. Include Every Person Who Ever Worked in a Particular Factory

Including ALL workers regardless of current employment status captures the full spectrum of health outcomes — including those who left due to health issues. Example: In a lead smelter study, include all who ever worked there, including those who left due to lead poisoning symptoms.

5. Advanced Methods: Marginal Structural Models (MSMs) / G-estimation

MSMs use inverse probability weighting to deal with time-dependent confounding — creating a pseudo-population without the HWE bias. G-estimation compares individuals with similar exposure histories. These are sophisticated methods when standard adjustment is insufficient.

Q3 Reproductive / Genetic Epid (AMALINA) 20 marks
Scenario: Microcephaly increasing in trend in Perlis. People said due to consumption of illegal pickles (asam jeruk) from Thailand.

(a) Name an appropriate study design. (1 mark)
(b) Outline 3 methodological issues in relation to reproductive epidemiology. (9 marks)
(c) Similar cases found in same family. Discuss how genetic epidemiology can confirm this. (10 marks)
Marks guide: (a) 1 mark · (b) 3 issues × 3 marks = 9 marks · (c) 4–5 genetic methods × ~2 marks = 10 marks
(a) Study Design — 1 mark

Retrospective cohort study. Use existing records of births, maternal exposure to illegal pickles, and microcephaly diagnoses to determine whether exposure precedes the outcome.

(b) Three Methodological Issues — 9 marks (3 marks each)
Issue 1: Selecting the Appropriate Study Population & Unit of Analysis (3 marks)

Must include not only currently pregnant women but also women of childbearing age (15–49), family members in same household (for genetic confounding), and frequent consumers. Unit of analysis must consider individual vs household/community level. Temporal aspect — exposure (pickle consumption during pregnancy) and outcome (microcephaly) may span different time periods, requiring a longitudinal approach. Failure to define comprehensive study population → selection bias.

Issue 2: Hidden or Missing Data / Exposure Misclassification (3 marks)

Consumption of illegal pickles is sensitive — pregnant women may underreport due to fear of legal repercussions or social stigma. This causes exposure misclassification bias: consumers incorrectly classified as non-exposed → dilutes true association (non-differential misclassification). Solutions: anonymous questionnaires; biological markers (blood/urine for contaminants); indirect questioning.

Issue 3: Confounding by Genetic and Other Environmental Factors (3 marks)

Microcephaly can have genetic causes (primary microcephaly). Familial clustering may be due to shared genetics not shared exposure. Infections (Zika virus), maternal age, nutritional status, other dietary exposures are also confounders. Must distinguish environmental from genetic aetiology through genetic testing of affected families. Multivariable adjustment for all measured confounders is required.

(c) Genetic Epidemiology Confirmation — 10 marks
1. Family Studies & Pedigree Analysis

Construct detailed family trees — identify patterns of inheritance (autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, X-linked). Visualize how the condition is transmitted across generations and identify potential carriers of genetic mutation responsible for microcephaly.

2. Whole Exome Sequencing (WES)

Sequences all protein-coding regions of the genome. Identifies both known and novel mutations. In familial microcephaly — WES can identify mutations in genes (MCPH1, CDK5RAP2, ASPM, CENPJ) associated with primary microcephaly. High diagnostic yield; enables personalized management; cost- and time-effective; streamlines diagnostic process.

3. Chromosomal Microarray Analysis (CMA)

Detects submicroscopic chromosomal alterations — copy number variations (CNVs). Complements WES by providing more comprehensive genetic profile. Together, WES + CMA form an efficient first-line genetic diagnostic workup for familial microcephaly.

4. Genetic Linkage Studies

Identify specific chromosomal regions co-inherited with microcephaly within affected families. Analyse genetic markers in families with multiple affected individuals to pinpoint chromosomal regions containing associated genes. Useful when genetic basis is suspected but gene not yet identified.

5. Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS)

Scan genomes of many individuals to find common genetic variants (SNPs) associated with the condition. When multiple cases within a family share the same genetic variants → strengthens evidence those variants play a role in microcephaly development. Hypothesis-free approach — tests millions of SNPs simultaneously.

By constructing detailed family trees and analyzing genetic markers through linkage studies and GWAS, researchers can pinpoint specific genetic loci involved in microcephaly. Functional studies and examination of gene-environment interactions further elucidate the aetiology — confirming the genetic basis and guiding targeted interventions.

Q4 Drug & Substance Abuse (AMALINA) 20 marks
Outline research methodology to study the pattern of alcohol consumption and risky behaviour among Malaysian population. (20 marks)
Marks guide (20 marks): Study design + justification (~3) + Target population (~2) + Variables (~3) + Sampling method (~3) + Data collection tool + method (~3) + Data analysis (~3) + Ethical considerations (~3)
Study Design

Cross-sectional study — chosen because it allows collection of data at a single point in time, providing a snapshot of current behaviours. Efficient for identifying prevalence and patterns across different segments of the population. Suitable for identifying potential risk factors and outcomes linked to alcohol consumption.

Target Population & Variables

Target population: Malaysian residents aged 18 and above.

Independent variables: Age, gender, ethnicity, education, employment status, income; alcohol consumption patterns (frequency, quantity, type, age of initiation).

Dependent variables: Risky behaviours — drunk driving, unprotected sex/premarital sex, involvement in fights, substance use, gambling, vandalism.

Sampling Method

Stratified random sampling — divide population into strata by state, urban/rural location, age, gender, and ethnicity. Ensures representative sample of the diverse Malaysian population. Enhances generalizability of findings. Sample size determined by power calculation to detect significant differences in alcohol consumption and risky behaviour patterns across subgroups.

Data Collection

Structured questionnaire with sections on:

  • Demographics: Age, gender, ethnicity, education, employment, income
  • Alcohol consumption: Frequency, quantity, type of alcoholic beverages, age of initiation
  • Risky behaviours: Drunk driving, unprotected sex, fights, substance use, gambling, vandalism

Collection method: Self-administered questionnaire preferred over face-to-face interview for sensitive questions — reduces social desirability bias. Online or paper-based. Ask about shorter, more recent periods (past month/week) to reduce recall bias.

Objective measure: Biomarkers (EtG in urine, PEth in blood) to objectively measure alcohol consumption and validate self-report.

Data Analysis
  • Descriptive statistics: Frequencies, means, SD — summarize patterns of alcohol consumption and risky behaviours
  • Multivariate logistic regression: Identify predictors of risky behaviour among alcohol consumers — adjust for potential confounders (age, gender, SES)
Ethical Considerations
  • IRB approval before commencement
  • Informed consent — explain purpose, procedures, risks, benefits, confidentiality
  • Voluntary participation — right to withdraw without consequences
  • Assure confidentiality — data used solely for research purposes
  • Sensitive nature of alcohol & risky behaviour disclosure — anonymous self-administered questionnaires reduce stigma-related underreporting
