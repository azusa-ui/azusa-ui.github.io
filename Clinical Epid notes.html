AdmittedNot AdmittedTotal

Total10114011502

Define, calculate and interpret the following treatment effects:

(a) Absolute Risk (AR) 5 marks
(b) Absolute Risk Reduction (ARR) 5 marks
(c) Number Needed to Treat (NNT) 5 marks
(d) Relative Risk (RR) 5 marks
Model Answer
(a) Absolute Risk — 5 marks

Definition: The probability of an event occurring in a specified group over a defined period.

AR — Current Antibiotic (control)
AR = 53 / 500 = 0.106 (10.6%)
10.6% of pregnant women on current antibiotic were admitted for GUT bacterial infection.

AR — Drug X (treatment)
AR = 48 / 1002 = 0.0479 (4.79%)
4.79% of pregnant women on Drug X were admitted for GUT bacterial infection.
(b) Absolute Risk Reduction — 5 marks

Definition: The absolute difference in event rates between control and treatment group.

ARR = AR(control) − AR(treatment)
ARR = 0.106 − 0.0479 = 0.0581 (5.81%)
Drug X reduces the absolute risk of hospital admission by 5.81% compared to current antibiotic.
(c) Number Needed to Treat — 5 marks

Definition: The number of patients who need to be treated to prevent one additional bad outcome.

NNT = 1 / ARR
NNT = 1 / 0.0581 = 17.2 → 18 patients
Approximately 18 pregnant women need to be treated with Drug X to prevent one additional hospital admission.
(d) Relative Risk — 5 marks

Definition: The ratio of the risk of the outcome in the treatment group to the risk in the control group.

RR = AR(treatment) / AR(control)
RR = 0.0479 / 0.106 = 0.45 (< 1, protective)
Women on Drug X have 45% of the risk compared to those on current antibiotic — 55% lower relative risk (RRR = 0.55).
Q2
Non-Randomized Trial
Threats to internal validity — community exercise programme among obese adults
20 marks +

A non-randomised community trial on the effectiveness of an exercise program is conducted among obese adults. Discuss threats to internal validity in this non-randomized trial.

Model Answer

Internal validity is the degree to which observed effects are truly due to the intervention. Non-randomized trials are vulnerable to:

  • Selection Bias: Without randomization, the exercise group may self-select motivated, healthier participants — differences in outcomes may reflect baseline characteristics rather than the intervention.
  • History: Concurrent external events (national anti-obesity campaigns, dietary guideline changes) may independently change outcomes in one group.
  • Maturation: Natural physiological changes over time (seasonal weight fluctuations, regression to mean) can occur independently of the exercise programme.
  • Testing Effect: Repeated physical assessments may themselves prompt behaviour change, unrelated to the programme content.
  • Instrumentation: Changes in measurement equipment calibration or different assessors at different time points introduce systematic error.
  • Regression to the Mean: Participants selected for high BMI may naturally drift toward the population mean, appearing as programme-induced improvement.
  • Attrition/Dropout Bias: Differential dropout (least fit leaving) leaves only motivated completers, overestimating effectiveness.
  • Confounding: Unmeasured co-interventions (dietary changes, medication) may differ between groups and independently affect outcomes.
  • Contamination: Control group participants may independently begin exercising, reducing observed differences between groups.
  • Hawthorne Effect: Participants may change behaviour simply because they know they are being observed.
Mitigation: Use concurrent control group; propensity score analysis; standardize measurement protocols; intention-to-treat analysis.
Q3
Blinding
Two methods of blinding — Drug A (analgesic) vs placebo in lower back pain
20 marks +

A researcher compares the effect of a new analgesic treatment (Drug A) vs placebo (Drug B) among lower back pain patients. Discuss 2 (TWO) methods of blinding.

Model Answer
Method 1 — Placebo for Active Drug (10 marks)

Definition: A placebo (Drug B) physically indistinguishable from Drug A is administered to the control group. Identical in appearance (colour, shape, size, sheen), taste, texture, weight, odour, and packaging.

Level of blinding: Primarily blinds participants; can also blind outcome assessors.

Advantages: Simple to implement for oral medications; prevents placebo response bias; reduces differential reporting of pain outcomes.

Limitations: Difficult if Drug A has distinctive side effects (drowsiness, flushing) absent in placebo — participants may guess their allocation.

Assessment: After trial, ask participants to guess their assignment — success if correct guessing ≈ 50% in each group.

Method 2 — Double-Dummy Method (10 marks)

Definition: All participants receive two preparations simultaneously — one active and one matching placebo — so both groups are indistinguishable.

Mechanism: Drug A group receives active Drug A + placebo resembling Drug B; Drug B group receives active Drug B (or placebo of A) + placebo resembling Drug A.

Level of blinding: Achieves comprehensive double-blinding — particularly useful when comparing treatments with different formulations (pill vs patch).

Advantages: Maximises blinding integrity; prevents performance bias and detection bias; preferred in high-quality RCTs comparing different drug forms.

Limitations: More complex and expensive; increased pill burden may reduce compliance; logistically challenging for multi-centre trials.

  • NPV = TN/(TN+FN). Increases as prevalence falls.

Post-test odds (−) = 1.50 × LR− → Adjusted NPV = 1/(1+Post-test odds)
At higher prevalence (60%), PPV increases while NPV decreases.
Q5
Ethics in Clinical Trials
Ethical considerations — pharmacist-led low-carbohydrate diet trial in Type 2 DM
20 marks +

A non-randomized trial conducted by community pharmacists among Type 2 DM patients. A low-carbohydrate diet is delivered during a 12-week trial; outcomes are HbA1C, blood pressure, and medication satisfaction. Discuss ethical considerations.

Model Answer

Guided by the four pillars of biomedical ethics (Autonomy, Beneficence, Non-maleficence, Justice) and Emanuel et al. principles of scientific validity and social value.

  • Autonomy & Informed Consent (4 marks): Full written information about the intervention, objectives, allocation process, risks/benefits, and right to withdraw. The pharmacist-patient relationship creates power imbalance — recruitment should use neutral party or explicit safeguards. T2DM patients may have comorbidities affecting cognitive capacity; use plain language forms.
  • Risk-Benefit / Beneficence (4 marks): Low-carbohydrate diet carries hypoglycaemia risk (especially with sulfonylureas/insulin), nutritional deficiencies. Benefits include improved HbA1C and blood pressure. Pharmacists must be trained to monitor adverse effects. Clinical equipoise must be demonstrated.
  • Non-maleficence (3 marks): Minimum necessary data collected; dietary advice must be evidence-based; adverse events must be reported. Non-randomized design increases confounding — conclusions about causation must be appropriately caveated.
  • Justice & Fair Subject Selection (3 marks): T2DM patients are vulnerable (chronic illness, polypharmacy). Inclusion/exclusion criteria must be scientifically justified. Pharmacist recruiting own patients creates conflict of interest — independent oversight recommended.
  • Privacy & Confidentiality (3 marks): Sensitive health data (HbA1C, BP, medication records) stored securely, de-identified. Compliance with PDPA mandatory.
  • Scientific Validity & Social Value (2 marks): Research question must address genuine knowledge gap. Ethics committee (MREC/IRB) approval required before data collection.
  • Post-trial Access (1 mark): If intervention proves beneficial, all participants offered access post-trial (Declaration of Helsinki).

Meta-analysis
Frailty intervention in older persons — PICO question & forest plot interpretation
30 marks +

Frailty among older persons is a rising concern with heterogeneous evidence on management.

(a) Formulate a specific research question for this meta-analysis 5 marks
(b) A meta-analysis was conducted through random-effect model. Egger's test = 0.405. Comment on the forest plot. 25 marks
Model Answer
(a) PICO Research Question — 5 marks
PICO Format
P — Older persons (≥60 years) with confirmed frailty (Fried Frailty Phenotype or Clinical Frailty Scale)
I — Multi-component frailty intervention (exercise ± nutritional supplementation ± cognitive training)
C — Usual care or no intervention
O — Frailty score reduction, functional outcomes (gait speed, grip strength), quality of life
"Among older persons aged ≥60 years with frailty, does a multi-component intervention compared to usual care significantly reduce frailty scores and improve functional outcomes?"
(b) Forest Plot Commentary — 25 marks
  • Structure (3 marks): Each horizontal line = one study; square = point estimate (size ∝ weight); horizontal bars = 95% CI; diamond = pooled effect.
  • X-axis (2 marks): Values left of null favour control; right favour intervention. Null line = 0 (for SMD) or 1 (for RR).
  • Individual study results (3 marks): Describe direction and magnitude; note which studies' CIs cross the null line.
  • Pooled effect (4 marks): Report overall pooled SMD/RR and 95% CI. If CI excludes null, result is statistically significant.
  • Heterogeneity (5 marks): I² <25% = low; 25–50% = moderate; >75% = high. High I² justifies random-effects model.
  • Random-effects justification (2 marks): DerSimonian-Laird method appropriate given expected heterogeneity in frailty populations — produces wider, more conservative CI than fixed-effects.
  • Publication bias — Egger's test (3 marks): p = 0.405. Since p > 0.05, no statistically significant evidence of publication bias.
  • Clinical interpretation (3 marks): If pooled effect favours intervention with significant CI, supports multi-component programmes for older frail persons. Limitations: heterogeneity in frailty measurement and intervention delivery.

Randomization
Stratified randomization — new chemotherapy (Drug A vs B) with sex & age strata
30 marks +

A researcher studies the effect of new chemotherapy (Drug A) versus current treatment (Drug B). Two major prognostic factors: sex (male/female) and age (adult/elderly).

(a) State the most appropriate method of randomization and provide reasons 5 marks
(b) Plan your randomization 25 marks
Model Answer
(a) Method — 5 marks

Stratified randomization (with block randomization within strata) is most appropriate.

  • Two known prognostic factors (sex and age) associated with chemotherapy outcomes — stratification ensures balance across both arms.
  • Unstratified randomization risks imbalance in small-to-moderate samples for categorical prognostic factors.
  • Reduces confounding from key covariates, improving internal validity.
(b) Randomization Plan — 25 marks

Step 1 — Define strata (5 marks): Cross all combinations to create 2×2 = 4 strata:

StratumSexAge Group
1MaleAdult
2MaleElderly
3FemaleAdult
4FemaleElderly

Step 2 — Block randomization within strata (10 marks): Apply permuted block randomization (block size 4 or 6) within each stratum.

Example: Stratum 1 (Male-Adult), block size = 4
Possible blocks: AABB | ABAB | ABBA | BAAB | BABA | BBAA
Randomly select blocks in sequence. Apply same process to Strata 2–4 independently.

Q2
Prognosis & Outcomes
Bias in prognostic studies + characteristics of primary outcomes
30 marks +
(a) Discuss bias in prognostic study 20 marks
(b) Outline the characteristics of primary outcomes 10 marks
Model Answer
(a) Bias in Prognostic Studies — 20 marks

Using NSCLC (non-small cell lung cancer) cohort as example. Bias is broadly categorised into Selection Bias and Information Bias.

1. Selection Bias (8 marks)

  • Susceptibility Bias: Exposed and unexposed groups differ in baseline characteristics. In NSCLC, patients receiving aggressive treatment may be younger with fewer comorbidities — inherently likely to have better outcomes independent of treatment. Minimize by: matching, restriction, multivariate adjustment.
  • Migration Bias: Dropout reasons correlate with both prognostic factor and outcome. Aggressive treatment patients may dropout due to severe side effects, leaving a healthier survivor subset — artificially improving apparent outcomes. Minimize by: intent-to-treat analysis, multiple imputation.

2. Information Bias (8 marks)

  • Measurement Bias: Outcomes detected differently between groups. CT scanning vs chest X-ray produces different recurrence rates — not due to worse prognosis but superior detection sensitivity. Minimize by: standardized diagnostic protocols; blinded outcome assessors.
  • Lead Time Bias: Apparent survival improvement from earlier detection, not actual longer survival. Screened NSCLC patients appear to live longer post-diagnosis because disease is detected earlier. Minimize by: measure disease-specific mortality; compare within same disease stage.

3. Definition and context (4 marks): A prognostic study identifies factors that predict future outcomes in patients with an established diagnosis. The inception cohort design is the gold standard.

(b) Characteristics of Primary Outcomes — 10 marks
  • Clinically important and patient-centred: Reflects outcomes that matter to patients (survival, QOL, symptom relief).
  • Pre-specified in protocol: Defined before data collection to prevent HARKing.
  • Singular: One primary outcome preferred to avoid Type I error inflation.
  • Valid and reliable: Measurement tool validated for the target population.
  • Measurable and quantifiable: Expressible as number or category permitting statistical testing.
  • Responsive to treatment: Capable of changing in response to the intervention within the study time frame.
  • Appropriate scale: Numerical, categorical/binary, or time-to-event — matched to correct statistical test.
  • Feasible to collect: Practical, ethical, achievable within study resources.

Model Answer
(a) Three ICC Models — 12 marks
  • Model 1 — One-Way Random [ICC(1,1)]: Each subject rated by a different, randomly selected subset of raters — no rater rates all subjects. Only subject effect is modelled. Formula: (MSR−MSW)/(MSR+(k−1)MSW).
  • Model 2 — Two-Way Random [ICC(2,1)]: All subjects rated by same raters; these raters are a random sample from a larger pool — results generalisable to other raters. Type: Consistency or Absolute agreement. Use when you want to generalise findings to other raters from the same pool.
  • Model 3 — Two-Way Mixed [ICC(3,1)]: All subjects rated by same fixed set of raters — these specific raters are the only ones of interest (not generalisable). Subject random, rater fixed. Appropriate for test-retest reliability. Mathematically identical to Model 2 but interpretation restricted to those specific raters.
(b)(i) Kappa Calculation — 15 marks
Step 1 — Observed Agreement (P₀) [3 marks]
P₀ = (35+55)/100 = 0.90 (90%)

Step 2 — Expected Agreement by Chance (Pₑ) [6 marks]
Pₑ (+) = (40×40)/10000 = 0.16
Pₑ (−) = (60×60)/10000 = 0.36
Pₑ = 0.16 + 0.36 = 0.52

Step 3 — Kappa (K) [3 marks]
K = (0.90−0.52)/(1−0.52) = 0.38/0.48 = 0.79

Step 4 — SE & 95% CI [3 marks]
SE(K) = √[0.90×0.10/(100×0.48²)] = √[0.09/23.04] = 0.0625
95% CI = 0.79 ± 1.96×0.0625 = (0.67, 0.91)
(b)(ii) Interpretation — 3 marks

K = 0.79 (95% CI: 0.67, 0.91) — substantial to almost perfect agreement between the two radiologists (Landis & Koch 1977: 0.61–0.80 = Substantial; 0.81–1.00 = Almost Perfect). CI entirely exceeds the 0.61 threshold.

Q5
Screening
Factors influencing Positive and Negative Predictive Value
6 marks +

Briefly describe THREE (3) factors influencing the Positive and Negative Predictive Value.

Model Answer — 2 marks each
  • Prevalence: As prevalence increases, PPV increases and NPV decreases. In low-prevalence populations, even a high-sensitivity test will have many false positives and low PPV.
  • Sensitivity: Higher sensitivity → more true positives detected → higher NPV. A highly sensitive test makes a negative result more reliable (SnNOut rule).
  • Specificity: Higher specificity → fewer false positives → higher PPV. High specificity confirms disease on positive result (SpPin rule).
Q6
Screening
Youden's Index — cut-off point determination
10 marks +

Describe the usage of Youden's Index to determine the cut-off point of a diagnostic test.

Model Answer

Definition (2 marks): Youden's Index (J) measures the overall discriminatory ability of a diagnostic test — balancing sensitivity and specificity into a single metric.

Formula (1 mark): J = Sensitivity + Specificity − 1. Range: 0 (no discrimination) to 1 (perfect).

Application to ROC curve (4 marks): For each possible cut-off, Sn and Sp are calculated and J computed. The cut-off with the highest J is the optimal threshold — the point on the ROC curve with greatest perpendicular distance from the chance diagonal.

Example
Cut-off A: Sn=0.80, Sp=0.70 → J = 0.50
Cut-off B: Sn=0.85, Sp=0.75 → J = 0.60 ← optimal
Cut-off C: Sn=0.90, Sp=0.60 → J = 0.50

Clinical consideration (3 marks): Screening tests prefer maximising sensitivity; confirmatory tests prefer maximising specificity. Youden's Index provides the statistically optimal balance, but final cut-off must be determined clinically by weighing consequences of false positives vs false negatives.

Q7
Systematic Review
Role of systematic review in evidence-based practice
20 marks +

Discuss the role of systematic review in evidence-based practice.

Model Answer
  • Integration of evidence (3 marks): Consolidates findings from multiple studies into a distilled summary of best available evidence.
  • Minimising bias (3 marks): Predefined inclusion/exclusion criteria, quality assessment tools (GRADE, Cochrane RoB), and systematic search strategies reduce selection and interpretation bias.
  • Informing clinical decision-making (3 marks): Provides clinicians with up-to-date, comprehensive evidence on treatment options, diagnostic tests, and preventive measures.
  • Policy and guideline development (3 marks): Health authorities use SRs to formulate evidence-informed clinical guidelines. E.g., SR on smoking cessation effectiveness informs national anti-tobacco policies.
  • Efficiency (2 marks): Saves time by avoiding duplication; provides synthesised information in one document rather than requiring review of hundreds of primary studies.
  • Identifying research gaps (2 marks): Highlights inconsistencies and gaps in the literature, guiding future research priorities.
  • Limitations (4 marks): Quality depends on primary studies; publication bias can skew pooled estimates; heterogeneity (I²) complicates synthesis; labour-intensive; may become outdated.

Ethical considerations — web-based education RCT among diabetic patients
20 marks +

A RCT on web-based education among diabetic patients assessing BMI, blood lipid profile, blood pressure, and fasting blood glucose. Discuss the ethical considerations.

  • Scientific Validity (2 marks): Clear research question; validated outcome tools; sufficient sample size. Ethics committee approval (MREC/HPBS) mandatory.

Questions covering ITT vs PP analysis, treatment effect calculations, non-randomized trials, ICC, Cohen's kappa, screening biases, randomization, and placebo characteristics.

Q1
Analytical Approach
Advantages & disadvantages of ITT vs Per-Protocol analysis
10 marks +

Describe the advantages and disadvantages of Intention-to-Treat over Per-Protocol Analysis.

Model Answer
Intention-to-Treat (ITT)

Definition: All randomized participants analysed in originally assigned group regardless of completion or deviation.

  • Advantages: Preserves randomization; reflects real-world conditions; minimises attrition bias; conservative estimate of treatment effectiveness; preferred for superiority trials.
  • Disadvantages: Including non-compliant participants dilutes true treatment effect; requires handling missing data (LOCF, multiple imputation); may mask safety signals from compliant subgroups.
Per-Protocol (PP)

Definition: Only participants who strictly adhered to protocol are included.

  • Advantages: Clearer estimate of true efficacy under ideal conditions; preferred for non-inferiority/equivalence trials.
  • Disadvantages: Loss of randomization benefits; reduced sample size decreases power; overestimates treatment effect; limits generalizability.
Best practice: Report both ITT (primary) and PP (secondary). Convergent results strengthen confidence.

The Single Measure ICC = 0.79 (95% CI: 0.68, 0.88) is appropriate. In clinical practice, pain score is measured by any one rater — the relevant question is whether a single rater's score can be trusted. ICC (Two-way random, consistency, single measure) = 0.79 = good reliability (Koo & Li, 2016: 0.75–0.90 = good).

(b) Justification — 9 marks
  • Two-way (3 marks): All 5 raters assessed all patients. Both subjects and raters are random — raters drawn from a larger pool, results should generalise to other raters from that pool.
  • Random effects (3 marks): Raters were randomly selected and represent a larger pool; results should be generalisable — requires random effects approach.
  • Consistency type (3 marks): Each rater may have a slightly different baseline rating style, but what matters is they rate in the same direction/rank order. Consistent direction is sufficient in clinical pain assessment.

Q6
Screening
Features of screening test + potential biases from screening programmes
30 marks +
(a) Outline features of a screening test 10 marks
(b) Discuss potential biases from screening tests 20 marks
Model Answer
(a) Features of a Screening Test — 10 marks
  • Accurate: High sensitivity (few false negatives) and high specificity (few false positives).
  • Reliable: Consistent results (test-retest; inter-rater reliability).
  • Simple: Minimal preparation, quick, feasible for mass screening.
  • Economical: Cost-effective for large-scale use.
  • Safe: Minimal risk; non-invasive or minimally invasive.
  • Acceptable: Culturally and socially acceptable to patients and clinicians.
  • High PPV: High proportion of positives are true positives.
  • Minimal labelling effect: Should not cause significant psychological or social harm from false labelling.
(b) Biases from Screening Programmes — 20 marks (5 marks each)
  • Lead Time Bias: Earlier detection gives appearance of longer survival — patient doesn't actually live longer. E.g., prostate cancer detected at 60 by screening vs 65 by symptoms; death at 70 in both. Mitigation: measure disease-specific mortality rates.
  • Length Time Bias: Screening preferentially detects slow-growing (indolent) tumours with longer pre-symptomatic phase. Fast-growing cancers are diagnosed symptomatically before screening catches them — screened populations appear to have better prognosis not due to effective screening but because detected tumours are inherently less aggressive. Mitigation: RCTs of screening vs no screening.
  • Overdiagnosis Bias: Screening detects conditions that would never have caused symptoms or death during the patient's lifetime. E.g., thyroid microcarcinomas detected by screening — unnecessary surgery/radiation without benefit. Mitigation: Active surveillance protocols; long-term natural history data.
  • Volunteer/Compliance Bias: Screening volunteers tend to be more health-conscious with healthier lifestyles independent of screening benefit. Better outcomes reflect healthy behaviours, not screening effectiveness. Mitigation: Randomized screening trials; adjust for confounders.

Q8
Blinding
Characteristics of a placebo
10 marks +

Briefly discuss the characteristics of a placebo.

Model Answer

Definition (2 marks): A chemically inert preparation closely resembling the active compound in all physical characteristics but with no pharmacological effect. Permits blinding and isolates true drug effects from psychological/expectation effects.

Physical characteristics (6 marks):

  • Visual appearance: Identical colour, shape, size, sheen
  • Taste & local action: Same taste profile on the tongue
  • Texture: Identical surface texture
  • Weight & specific gravity: Same weight and density
  • Odour: Matching smell — especially critical for herbal preparations or fish oils
  • Packaging: Identical containers, labels, and instructions
Limitation: Creating a perfect placebo is often impossible when the active drug produces characteristic side effects (e.g., flushing with niacin, bradycardia with beta-blockers) absent in the placebo group.

Q1
Analytical Approach
Advantages & disadvantages of ITT vs Per-Protocol — detailed discussion
20 marks +

Discuss advantages and disadvantages of Intention-to-Treat analysis and Per-Protocol analysis.

Model Answer — Extended Version
ITT Advantages (5 marks)
  • Maintains randomization — groups remain statistically comparable
  • Reflects real-world effectiveness — includes inevitable non-compliance and dropouts
  • Conservative estimate — avoids overestimation of efficacy
  • Maximises statistical power using all available data
  • Minimises attrition and exclusion bias
ITT Disadvantages (5 marks)
  • Dilutes treatment effect — non-compliant participants dilute true drug effect toward null
  • Missing data problem — requires statistical strategies (LOCF, multiple imputation)
  • May mask differential safety signals from compliant subgroups
PP Advantages (5 marks)
  • Reflects treatment efficacy under ideal conditions
  • Clinically relevant for motivated, adherent patients
  • Preferred for non-inferiority/equivalence trials
  • Provides "ceiling estimate" of treatment benefit
PP Disadvantages (5 marks)
  • Compromises randomization — excluded non-compliers differ systematically → selection bias
  • Reduced sample size → decreased statistical power
  • Overestimates treatment effect — compliers are not representative
  • Reduces external validity
Conclusion: ITT preferred for superiority trials (primary); PP as secondary. For non-inferiority, both mandatory. Convergent results strengthen conclusions.

Q4
Screening
ROC curve — usage in evaluation of a screening test
10 marks +

Describe the usage of the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve in the evaluation of a screening test.

Model Answer

Definition (2 marks): The ROC curve displays the relationship (trade-off) between sensitivity (y-axis) and 1−specificity (x-axis) across every possible cut-off value. Useful when the optimal threshold is unknown.

  • Assess discriminatory ability — AUC (3 marks): AUROC ranges from 0.5 (no discrimination) to 1.0 (perfect). AUROC 0.7–0.8 = acceptable; 0.8–0.9 = excellent; >0.9 = outstanding.
  • Determine optimal cut-off (3 marks): Cut-off closest to upper-left shoulder of curve maximises both Sn and Sp. Youden's Index (Sn+Sp−1) identifies this point. Clinically, cut-offs adjusted based on context: screening → prioritise sensitivity; confirmatory → prioritise specificity.
  • Compare two tests (2 marks): Test with larger AUC is more accurate overall. If ROC curves do not cross, one test consistently outperforms the other. DeLong's test compares AUCs formally.

Q6
Ethics
Ethical considerations of a preventive trial / community intervention
15 marks +

Describe the ethical considerations of a preventive trial.

Model Answer — guided by the 4 pillars

Guided by Autonomy, Beneficence, Non-maleficence, and Justice. Key tension: balancing individual rights against population-level benefits.

  • Autonomy — Voluntary participation & avoiding excessive incentives (4 marks): Participants must give informed, voluntary consent. Preventive trials recruit healthy volunteers — benefit-to-self less obvious, so consent processes must be especially thorough. Excessive financial incentives may coerce economically disadvantaged participants (undue inducement).
  • Beneficence — Population needs & risk-benefit balance (4 marks): Intervention must address genuine population health need. Benefits to community must outweigh individual-level risks. Risk-benefit assessment performed before and monitored during trial (DSMB).
  • Non-maleficence — Cultural sensitivity & minimising harm (4 marks): Interventions must be culturally appropriate. Mechanisms to detect and report adverse events; protocols for emergency unblinding. Research must not unnecessarily expose participants to risks.
  • Justice — Fair subject selection & community engagement (3 marks): Selection must be equitable — burdens and benefits fairly distributed. Vulnerable populations should not be disproportionately enrolled without additional protections.
Additional: Privacy and data confidentiality; result dissemination (including null results); post-trial continuation of effective interventions; ethics committee approval before initiation.
