What is MANOVA?

▼

Multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) is simply an ANOVA with several dependent variables. MANOVA is a statistical method to test the hypothesis that one or more IV or factors, have an effect or influence on a set of two or more DVs. MANOVA tests for the difference in two or more vectors of means.

Instead of a univariate F value, we obtain a multivariate F value (e.g., Wilks' λ) based on a comparison of the error variance/covariance matrix and the effect variance/covariance matrix. The "covariance" is included because the two measures are probably correlated and we must take this correlation into account when performing the significance test.

DVs should be used together as a set (cross products), which should be biologically plausible and theoretically related (or with some degree of correlation) — decided at design stage. DVs should be "moderately correlated" because they measure separate aspects of the influence (variances) of the IV.

✅ Advantages over multiple ANOVAs

  1. Reduces experiment-wise Type I error — omnibus test protects inflated error probability
  2. Individual ANOVAs may be non-significant, but in combination they might be — variables are more meaningful together
  3. Greater power when two or more DVs are considered in combination
  4. Takes into account the inter-correlations among DVs
  5. Tests effects of many IVs and DVs within a single analysis — saves time, space, cost

⚠️ Disadvantages

  • Substantially more complicated than ANOVA — ambiguity about which IV affects each DV
  • One degree of freedom lost for each DV added
  • Lots of outputs and modelling strategies
  • Assumptions are numerous and difficult to assess
  • "The best recommendation is to avoid using unless necessary"
ℹ️
MANOVA vs. Multiple ANOVAs: Running 3 ANOVAs at α=0.05 inflates familywise error to 1−(0.95)³ = 14.3%. MANOVA maintains α. Additionally, if DVs correlate but individual ANOVAs show no effect, MANOVA may still detect a combined effect.

Research Questions answered by MANOVA

  1. What are the main effects of the independent variables?
  2. What are the interactions among the independent variables?
  3. What is the importance of the dependent variables?
  4. What is the strength of association between dependent variables?
  5. What are the effects of covariates (→ use MANCOVA)?

Test Statistics (Four Multivariate Tests)

▼
StatisticDescriptionBest Used When
Wilks' Lambda (Λ)Ratio of within-group variance to total variance. Ranges 0–1; values near 0 = groups differ greatly. Most widely used — good balance between power and assumptionsDefault choice for most situations; manipulated experimental variables
Pillai's TraceSum of variance explained by discriminant functions. Most conservative and robust to violations (small cells, unbalanced, assumption violations)When Box's M is significant; unequal/small cell sizes; assumption violations
Hotelling's TraceSum of eigenvalues of H·E⁻¹Exactly 2 groups; manipulated IV with clean design and no internal validity problems

💡
For most situations, all four statistics give the same conclusion. When they disagree, use Pillai's Trace as it is the most robust. If Box's M shows unequal covariance matrices → use Pillai's Trace (Mertler and Vannatta, 2002).

Effect Size: Partial Eta Squared (η²)

Cohen's benchmarks: Small: η² = 0.02 Medium: η² = 0.13 Large: η² = 0.26

In SPSS: Options → "Estimates of effect size" → provides partial eta squared (ηp²) column. Ranges 0–1. Interpret like R² in regression.

One-way vs Two-way MANOVA

▼

One-way MANOVA

One categorical IV/factor with ≥2 levels/groups; ≥2 numerical DVs. Tests if levels of a single IV differ on the combination of DVs. Example: Do helmet types (Full Face, Bucket, Half Face) differ on Brain Damage Index and Conscious Level?

Two-way MANOVA

Two categorical IVs/factors (e.g., Gender × Treatment) each with varying numbers of levels; ≥2 DVs. Tests main effects of each IV and their interaction on the combined DVs. If interaction is significant → run simple effect analysis by fixing one factor.

Variable/Data Requirements

VariableRequirements
Independent Variable (IV)One (or more for two-way) categorical IV/factor with ≥2 groups/levels; each factor must be independent
Dependent Variables (DVs)≥2 numerical/continuous variables; moderately correlated (r = 0.3–0.9); biologically/theoretically related
Cell sizesMinimum 10 per DV; more cases per group than number of DVs; preferably balanced. When cell counts >30, normality and equal variances are robust/less concerning

R: Checking Multivariate Outliers (Mahalanobis Distance)

R
Y <- as.matrix(df[, c("y1", "y2", "y3")])
mahal <- mahalanobis(Y, colMeans(Y), cov(Y))
cutoff <- qchisq(0.999, df = ncol(Y))  # df = number of DVs
which(mahal > cutoff)  # row indices of multivariate outliers

SPSS: Mahalanobis Distance (Optional Advanced Check)

Run Regression → Linear using all DVs as IVs and a case ID variable as DV. In Save, check Mahalanobis Distance. Sort MD descending. Critical χ² value at p = 0.001, df = number of DVs (e.g., 3 DVs → critical value = 16.27). Cases with MD > critical value are multivariate outliers.

In practice: check Cook's Distance (save from Multivariate menu) — values < 1.0 (Tabachnick & Fidell) are acceptable. Also check Leverage values < 0.5.

7-Step One-way MANOVA Procedure (Dr Wan Zahiruddin)

▼
📌
Example dataset: MANOVA_1.sav — Effect of helmet types on Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI). IV: Helmet types (Full Face, Bucket, Half Face). DVs: Brain Damage Index + Conscious Level. n = 75 MVA cases.
  1. Hypothesis statements
    H₀: There is no significant difference in TBI (measured by Brain Damage Index and Conscious Level) based on types of helmet worn by motorcyclists (3 types: full, half, and bucket).
    Hₐ: There is a significant difference in TBI in at least one helmet type.
  2. Data exploration / descriptive statistics / univariate outliers / normality
    Check n per group (adequate cell size?), boxplots for outliers, normality (histograms/Shapiro-Wilk), scatter plot of DVs showing correlation (r = 0.691 = moderately correlated → OK for MANOVA)
  3. Univariable analysis
    Run one-way ANOVA for each DV separately — screen for individual effects before MANOVA
  4. Fitting the MANOVA model
    Analyze → GLM → Multivariate; set IV as Fixed Factor, DVs in Dependent Variables. Include Options (descriptive, effect size, homogeneity). Include Post Hoc (Bonferroni) if IV has >2 levels. Read Multivariate Tests table → Wilks' Λ → overall significance, effect size, power.
  5. Checking model assumptions
    5a. Normality — histograms/boxplots of residuals (Save → Residuals)
    5b. Linearity — scatter plots of DVs by groups (elliptical pattern = OK)
    5c. Homogeneity — Box's M (p > 0.001 → OK) + Levene's (p > 0.05 per DV)
    5d. Multicollinearity — Correlate → Bivariate: r should be 0.3–0.9
    5e. Outliers — Cook's distance < 1.0; Leverage < 0.5 (sort descending in data view)
  6. Follow-up: Post-hoc tests and pairwise comparison
    If MANOVA is significant → run univariate ANOVAs per DV (Tests of Between-Subjects Effects). Use Bonferroni-corrected α = 0.05/k where k = number of DVs (e.g., 2 DVs → α = 0.025). Then run pairwise comparisons (Bonferroni) for significant DVs via GLM Multivariate → Post Hoc OR Options → Compare main effects. Report adjusted means (Estimated Marginal Means).
  7. Interpretation, data presentation, and conclusion
    Report in APA format: Descriptive table (means ± SD/CI per group per DV); MANOVA multivariate test table (Wilks' Λ, F, df, p, η²); Univariate ANOVA results per DV; Post-hoc pairwise comparisons; Profile plots.
⚠️
Critical rule: Do NOT run follow-up univariate ANOVAs if the overall MANOVA is non-significant. The omnibus test must be significant first.

Roy-Bargman Step-Down Procedure (Alternative post-hoc)

For follow-up with correlated DVs: The theoretically most important DV is analyzed individually (DV1). The next DV (DV2) is analyzed with DV1 as covariate (controls DV1-DV2 relationship). DV3 is assessed with DV1 and DV2 as covariates, etc. This controls for the relationship between DVs.

Other Approaches (Alternative to MANOVA)

  • Multiple factorial ANOVAs — lesser power than MANOVA
  • Factor analysis on DVs, then factorial ANOVA — lesser power
  • Discriminant Function Analysis (DFA) — switch DV and IV

ℹ️

  1. Go to: Analyze → General Linear Model → Multivariate

  7. Click OK

skimr::skim(df)
df |> group_by(helmet_type) |>
  summarise(across(c(brain_damage_index, conscious_level),
    list(mean = mean, sd = sd, n = ~sum(!is.na(.x))), .names = "{.col}_{.fn}"))

# Correlation between DVs (should be 0.3–0.9)
cor(df[, c("brain_damage_index", "conscious_level")]) |> round(2)

# Scatter plot of DVs by group
ggplot(df, aes(brain_damage_index, conscious_level, colour = factor(helmet_type))) +
  geom_point() + geom_smooth(method = "lm", se = FALSE) +
  labs(title = "DVs by Helmet Type")

# ── A: Assumptions ─────────────────────────────────────────
Y <- as.matrix(df[, c("brain_damage_index", "conscious_level")])

# 1. Multivariate normality (Mardia's test)
MVN::mvn(Y, mvnTest = "mardia")$multivariateNormality

# 2. Box's M test (p > 0.001 acceptable)
biotools::boxM(Y, df$helmet_type)

# 3. Mahalanobis distance (multivariate outliers)
mahal <- mahalanobis(Y, colMeans(Y), cov(Y))
which(mahal > qchisq(0.999, df = 2))

# 4. Normality of residuals per group
df |> group_by(helmet_type) |>
  summarise(p_bdi = shapiro.test(brain_damage_index)$p.value,
            p_cl  = shapiro.test(conscious_level)$p.value)

# ── A: Analyse ─────────────────────────────────────────────
manova_fit <- manova(cbind(brain_damage_index, conscious_level) ~ helmet_type,
                       data = df)

# Multivariate test statistics
summary(manova_fit, test = "Wilks")    # λ = main statistic
summary(manova_fit, test = "Pillai")   # use if Box's M violated
summary(manova_fit, test = "Hotelling")
summary(manova_fit, test = "Roy")

# ── A: Assess ──────────────────────────────────────────────
# Effect size
heplots::etasq(manova_fit, test = "Wilks")

# Univariate follow-up (only if MANOVA significant)
# Bonferroni-corrected α = 0.05 / 2 DVs = 0.025
summary.aov(manova_fit)

# Post-hoc pairwise for significant DVs
pairwise.t.test(df$conscious_level, df$helmet_type, p.adjust.method = "bonferroni")

# Or via emmeans
emm <- emmeans(lm(conscious_level ~ helmet_type, data = df), ~ helmet_type)
pairs(emm, adjust = "bonferroni")

# ── P: Present ─────────────────────────────────────────────
df |> tbl_summary(
  by = helmet_type,
  include = c(brain_damage_index, conscious_level),
  statistic = list(all_continuous() ~ "{mean} ({sd})"),
  missing = "no") |>
  add_p() |> bold_labels() |>
  modify_caption("**Table 1. Descriptive Statistics by Helmet Type**")

What is MANCOVA?

▼

The one-way multivariate analysis of covariance (MANCOVA) can be thought of as an extension of the one-way MANOVA to incorporate a covariate, OR an extension of the one-way ANCOVA to incorporate multiple dependent variables.

This covariate is linearly related to the dependent variables and its inclusion can increase the ability to detect differences between groups of an IV.

MANCOVA looks at the influence of one or more IVs on the DVs while removing the effect of one or more covariate factors. One-Way MANCOVA first conducts a regression of the covariate variables on the DV, eliminating the influence of covariates. Then the residuals are subject to a MANOVA, testing whether the IV still influences the DVs after covariate influence is removed.

Two Key Advantages

1. Explaining within-group variance: MANCOVA looks at the unexplained variance and tries to explain some of it with the covariate(s). This increases the power of the MANOVA by explaining more variability. Note: entering a weak covariate actually decreases statistical power (over-fitting).

2. Controlling confounding factors: Eliminates the covariates' effects on the relationship between IVs and DVs — an effect typically tested using MANOVA.

Typical Research Questions

  • Whether exam performances differed based on test anxiety levels, whilst controlling for revision time
  • Do knowledge and attitude scores on obesity vary by education level after controlling for age?
  • Which disease indexes are better treated by X vs Y drug after controlling for duration of disease and patients' age?
  • Do attitude and feelings scores differ between signed/not-signed donor card groups after adjustment of exposure scores?

Variable Requirements

VariableRequirements
Independent Variable (IV)One categorical IV/factor with ≥2 groups/levels; independent
Dependent Variables (DVs)≥2 numerical/continuous; moderately correlated (r = 0.3–0.9); biologically/theoretically related
Covariate (CV)≥1 numerical/continuous variable; correlated with DVs (preferably r > 0.3); reliable, decided by design, biologically/theoretically plausible; no multicollinearity among covariates

SPSS handles up to 10 covariates in MANCOVA.

✅

MANCOVA Assumptions

▼

A. All MANOVA Assumptions (apply equally)

  1. DVs measured at interval or ratio level (numerical)
  2. Multivariate normality of residuals for each group of IV
  3. Homogeneity of covariance matrices (Box's M test)
  4. Independence of observations (by design)
  5. No univariate or multivariate outliers (inspect residuals)
  6. Linear relationship between each pair of DVs in each level of IV (scatter plots)
  7. No multicollinearity among DVs (r < 0.9)

B. Additional Covariate-Specific Assumptions

AssumptionHow to CheckAction if Violated
Linearity between covariate and DVsScatter plots of covariate vs each DVTransform covariate; consider removing it
Homogeneity of regression slopes — the most critical additional assumption. The slope of the regression of DVs on covariate must be equal (not significantly different) across all groups of IVTest interaction: IV × covariate in the model. Run GLM Multivariate with interaction term (IV*covariate). If interaction is NOT significant (p > 0.05) → assumption met → remove interaction term and run final modelIf violated, MANCOVA is not appropriate; consider separate regression analyses per group or Johnson-Neyman technique
No multicollinearity among covariatesCorrelation between covariates r < 0.9; VIFRemove redundant covariate
⚠️
Critical: Always check homogeneity of regression slopes BEFORE running the final MANCOVA. Run with the interaction term (IV*Covariate) first — if non-significant, proceed with main effects model. If significant, MANCOVA assumptions are violated.

7-Step One-way MANCOVA Procedure (Dr Wan Zahiruddin)

▼
📌
Example dataset: music.MANCOVA.sav — RQ: To determine whether attitude & feelings scores (combined) differ between signed vs. not-signed donor card groups after adjustment of exposure scores towards organ donation issues. IV: Donor group (signed/not-signed). DVs: Attitude score + Feelings score. Covariate: Exposure score. n = 388.
  1. Hypothesis statements
    H₀: There is no significant means difference of attitude scores and feelings scores in combination between signed donor card and not-signed donor card group after adjustment of exposure scores.
    Hₐ: There is a significant difference in means of combined attitude and feeling scores (or at least one) between donor groups after adjustment.
  2. Data exploration / descriptive statistics / normality / outliers
    Check cell sizes (balanced/adequate?), distributions of DVs and covariate, univariate outliers, correlation between DVs, correlation of covariate with DVs (r > 0.3 preferred).
  3. Fitting the MANCOVA model (with the covariate)
    Analyze → GLM → Multivariate → DVs in Dependent Variables box, IV in Fixed Factors, covariate in Covariates box. Include Options (descriptive, effect size, homogeneity). Read Multivariate Tests: preliminary main effect model with covariate.
  4. Checking interaction (between IV*covariate)
    Add interaction term: in MANCOVA dialogue, click Model → Custom → add IV, covariate, and IV*covariate interaction. Check Multivariate Tests for the interaction term. If NOT significant (p > 0.05) → assumption of homogeneity of regression slopes met → remove interaction term → run final model.
    Example: Pillai trace: F(2,383) = 0.688, p = 0.503 → NOT significant → proceed
  5. Checking model assumptions
    After removing interaction term, re-run final model. Check residuals (normality, linearity), Box's M, Levene's, multicollinearity, Cook's distance/Leverage. Note: if Box's M significant → use Pillai's Trace.
  6. Follow-up: Post-hoc tests and pairwise comparison
    If MANCOVA is significant → run univariate ANCOVA per DV. Use Bonferroni-corrected α = 0.05/k (e.g., 2 DVs → α = 0.025). Report adjusted means (Estimated Marginal Means at covariate mean). Run post-hoc pairwise via GLM Multivariate → Post Hoc or Options → Compare main effects.
  7. Result presentations
    Interpretation in text; tables (descriptive + ANCOVA summary + post-hoc); plots (not necessary if no interaction).

What is Data Visualization?

▼

Data visualization is the graphical representation of information and data. This is done using visual elements like charts, graphs, and maps. Data visualization tools provide an accessible way to see and understand trends, outliers, and patterns in data.

Objective: Data visualization tools and technologies are used to analyze massive amounts of information and make data-driven decisions.

Infographics vs. Visualization

Infographics = presentation (tell a story with designed visuals)
Visualization = exploration (see what data have to say)

Why Visualize?

  • Simple and clear
  • Has sufficient information
  • See what data have to say
  • Tell stories with data
  • Graphics broadcast; statistics narrowcast

What Makes Good Graphics? (Edward Tufte)

  • Data substance rather than about methodology or graphic design
  • No distortion to what the data has to say
  • Presence of many numbers in a small space
  • Coherence for large data sets
  • Encourages the eye to compare different pieces of data
  • Reveals the data at several levels of detail — from broad overview to fine structure
  • Serves a reasonably clear purpose: description, exploration, tabulation, or decoration
  • Closely integrated with statistical and verbal descriptions
  • Minimalism and efficiency: complex ideas communicated with clarity, precision and efficiency
  • Gives viewers the greatest number of ideas in the shortest time with the least ink

Two Key Things to Look For

A) Pattern

Plot based on daily data — as time goes by, bumpier graph with more fluctuations. Same data based on monthly basis gives smoother plot. Choose aggregation level appropriate for your research question.

B) Relationship

Unlike statistics — relationship shown in graphs not necessarily for answering hypothesis. Used to compare distributions between groups, show trends, identify correlations.

Questions to Ask Before Making Graphs

  • Which variable or variables do I want to plot?
  • What type is that variable? (factor/categorical or numerical?)
  • Am I plotting a single variable, two variables, or three variables together?
  • What is the purpose? (description, exploration, comparison, trend, correlation)

Core Principles of Statistical Writing

▼
ℹ️
ICMJE Guideline (International Committee of Medical Journal Editors): "Describe statistical methods with enough detail to enable a knowledgeable reader with access to the original data to verify the reported results. When possible, quantify findings and present them with appropriate indicators of measurement error or uncertainty (such as confidence intervals). Avoid relying solely on statistical hypothesis testing, such as P values, which fail to convey important information about effect size."

Main Points (Statistical Writing)

  • Tell a cohesive, concise story
  • Clearly state your hypotheses and how they relate to your analyses
  • Provide enough detail such that your audience can understand what you did and why you did it
  • For commonly used techniques (e.g., OLS regression), a single sentence description may suffice. For complicated or unfamiliar techniques, more explanation is required
  • Describe the model building process clearly (e.g., reference category, coding scheme)
  • Mention assumptions were assessed — but usually not every individual diagnostic test result unless one was grossly violated

Structure: What to Write in a Paper

Methods Section

  • Design: Make clear what type of study. Do not cloak a study in one guise to give it the reputation of another
  • Population: Define the population clearly. If control/comparison groups, present how they are defined
  • Measurement: Explicitly define variables, how they relate to study goals, units of measurement
  • Power and sample size: Document effect sizes, assumptions, and analytic procedures in power calculations
  • Statistical software: Name, version, specific packages (e.g., "IBM SPSS for Windows version 22.0"; "psych version 1.5.8 and lavaan version 0.5-20 in R")

Results Section

  • Two parts: descriptive statistics + analyses
  • Descriptive statistics introduce variables to the audience
  • For each analysis: state the hypothesis, identify variables, confirm assumptions were met, report test name (one- or two-tailed), alpha level, test statistic with df, p-value, effect size, CI
  • Report complications: missing data, attrition, protocol violations, outlier handling
  • Discussion: Think of credibility, generalizability, and robustness

SAMPL Guidelines — Key Reporting Rules by Analysis

▼

Lang & Altman. SAMPL (Statistical Analyses and Methods in the Published Literature) Guidelines. Published in: Science Editors' Handbook, European Association of Science Editors, 2013.

General Principles for Reporting Statistical Methods

  • Identify any statistical procedures used to modify raw data before analysis (transformation, recoding, collapsing)
  • Describe the purpose of the analysis; identify variables; summarise with descriptive statistics
  • Identify the smallest difference considered clinically important
  • Make clear which method was used for each analysis (not just a general list)
  • Verify data conformed to assumptions: skewed data → non-parametric; paired data → paired tests; linear regression → confirmed linearity
  • Indicate whether/how adjustments were made for multiple comparisons
  • Report how outlying data were treated
  • Say whether tests were one- or two-tailed; justify one-tailed tests
  • Report the alpha level (e.g., 0.05) that defines statistical significance
  • Name the statistical package or program

General Principles for Reporting Statistical Results

  • Report numbers with appropriate precision — round as much as reasonable
  • Report total sample AND group sizes for each analysis
  • Report numerators and denominators for all percentages
  • Summarise normal data: mean (SD) — NOT mean ± SD
  • Summarise non-normal data: medians and interpercentile ranges, ranges, or both — report upper and lower boundaries, not just the size
  • Do NOT use SE to indicate variability of a dataset — use SD, IQR, or ranges
  • P-values: report as equalities (P = 0.03) not inequalities (P < 0.05); smallest = P < 0.001; do NOT write "NS" — give the actual P value
  • Report 95% CI for primary outcomes at minimum

Regression-Specific SAMPL Requirements

  • Report the regression equation
  • For multiple regression: report alpha for univariable analysis; whether collinearity and interaction were assessed; variable selection process (forward-stepwise, best subset, etc.)
  • Report β (regression coefficients) with 95% CI and P-values, preferably in a table
  • Report R² for simple regression or R² for multiple regression
  • Specify whether and how the model was validated
  • Do not extend regression line beyond minimum and maximum values of data

Introduction to Correlated Data

▼

Many designs in educational, clinical, and epidemiological research generate multilevel data arising from "nested" data structures, where observations are nested within larger units. Examples:

  • Students (Level 1) nested within Schools (Level 2)
  • Patients (Level 1) nested within Physicians (Level 2) nested within Hospitals (Level 3)
  • Repeated measurements (Level 1) nested within Individuals (Level 2) — longitudinal data
  • Nursing home staff (Level 1) nested within Nursing Homes (Level 2)
  • SBP measurements every 30 min × 4 times (Level 1) per Patient (Level 2)

The primary issue with nested data structures is that they violate the independence assumption required by classic techniques such as ANOVA and OLS multiple regression. Level-1 units within the same level-2 unit tend to be more similar to each other than to units in other clusters (within-cluster dependence). This violation leads to excessive Type I errors and biased standard errors (usually underestimated) when using traditional methods.

Null / Intercept-Only Model

▼

Start with the simplest model — no explanatory variables — to decompose variance:

yₖₐ = β₀ + u₀ₐ + eₖₐ
  • β₀ = overall mean (across all clusters)
  • u₀ⱼ = cluster-specific deviation (Level-2 residual), u₀ⱼ ~ N(0, σ²ᵤ)
  • eᵢⱼ = individual-level residual (Level-1), eᵢⱼ ~ N(0, σ²ₑ)

Intra-class Correlation (ICC) — proportion of total variance attributable to between-cluster differences:

ICC = σ²ᵘ / (σ²ᵘ + σ²ᵉ)

Example (Scottish Schools, null model): σ²ᵤ = 61.02, σ²ₑ = 258.36 → ICC = 61.02/(61.02+258.36) = 0.191. This means 19.1% of variance in attainment is between schools.

Compare null multilevel model vs single-level OLS using log-likelihood ratio test. If multilevel differs significantly, clustering must be accounted for.

📈

Random Intercept Model (RI)

▼

Intercepts vary across clusters; slopes are fixed (same for all clusters):

yₖₐ = β₀ + β₁xₖₐ + u₀ₐ + eₖₐ

Or equivalently:

yₖₐ = β₀ₐ + β₁xₖₐ + eₖₐ    where β₀ₐ = β₀ + u₀ₐ

The intercept for cluster j is β₀ + û₀ⱼ. The slope β₁ is the same for all clusters.

Scottish Schools example: score = 30.559 + 1.215 × cohort90. For each cohort year increase, average score increases by 1.215 points, while each school has its own baseline intercept.

📉

Random Slope Model (RS)

▼

Both intercepts AND slopes vary across clusters:

yₖₐ = β₀ + β₁xₖₐ + u₀ₐ + u₁ₐxₖₐ + eₖₐ

Or:

yₖₐ = β₀ₐ + β₁ₐxₖₐ + eₖₐ    β₀ₐ = β₀ + u₀ₐ    β₁ₐ = β₁ + u₁ₐ
  • u₀ⱼ = random intercept deviation for cluster j
  • u₁ⱼ = random slope deviation for cluster j
  • Both assumed to follow bivariate normal with zero means and covariance matrix

Prof Kim: Random effects u₀ⱼ and u₁ⱼ are assumed to follow normal distributions with zero mean. The variance of the random slope can be large (slopes very different across clusters) or small (slopes nearly identical — single-level regression would suffice).

If model fails to converge: switch to bobyqa optimizer — lmerControl(optimizer = "bobyqa")

Scottish Schools RS model results: Intercept variance = 42.86, Slope variance (cohort90) = 0.16, Corr(u₀,u₁) = −0.39 (schools with higher baseline tend to show slower growth)

Cross-Level Interaction

▼

Interaction between a Level-1 variable and a Level-2 variable. Allows the effect of an individual-level predictor to differ depending on a cluster-level characteristic.

scoreₖₐ = β₀ + β₁cohort90 + β₂female + β₃sclass + β₄schtype + β₅schurban + β₆cohort90×schtype + u₀ₐ + u₁ₐcohort90 + eₖₐ

Prof Kim's result: The interaction cohort90×schtype is ~6× its SE — strong evidence that the effect of cohort year differs for independent vs state schools (β = −0.599, p < 0.001). Independent schools show smaller cohort gains.

Model Comparison & Selection

▼
  • Use anova(model1, model2) with ML estimation (REML=FALSE) to compare nested models via likelihood ratio test (LRT)
  • Compare AIC/BIC — lower is better
  • Log-likelihood difference × 2 = chi-square statistic with df = difference in parameters
  • Start from null model → add fixed effects → add random slopes → test interactions

What is GEE?

▼

Generalised Estimating Equations (GEE) is a marginal (population-averaged) approach to analysing correlated data. Unlike LMM which estimates cluster-specific effects through random effects, GEE estimates the average effect across the entire population, accounting for the correlation between observations within the same cluster without modelling that correlation explicitly.

Prof Kim (Kamarul Imran Musa) explicitly lists GEE as one of the two main modern methods for repeated-measures/longitudinal numerical outcomes: "The more recent and robust methods are: (1) Generalised Estimating Equations (GEE), (2) Mixed Models."

GEE is classified under Marginal Models — one approach among subject-specific effects models, dynamic models, and growth-curve models for longitudinal/panel data (Rabe-Hesketh & Skrondal, 2008).

📐

GEE Model Specification

▼

GEE extends Generalised Linear Models (GLM) to correlated data. The mean model is the same as GLM:

g(μₖₐ) = β₀ + β₁xₖₐ₁ + β₂xₖₐ₂ + ... + βₖxₖₐₖ
  • For continuous outcome: identity link → equivalent to population-averaged linear regression
  • For binary: logit link → population-averaged logistic regression
  • For count: log link → population-averaged Poisson

GEE uses a working correlation matrix to account for within-cluster correlation. The key insight: even if this correlation structure is misspecified, the regression coefficient estimates remain consistent (assuming correct mean model), and standard errors are corrected using the robust (sandwich) estimator.

Var(β̂) = A⁻¹ B A⁻¹    (sandwich estimator)

where A = model-based variance, B = empirical variance from data. This is why GEE is sometimes called Quasi-likelihood with robust SE.

🔗

Working Correlation Structures

▼
Structurecorstr=DescriptionWhen to use
Independence"independence"All correlations = 0; ignores clusteringBaseline only; underestimates SE
Exchangeable"exchangeable"Equal correlation between any 2 obs in same cluster (ρ estimated from data)Cross-sectional clusters; no time ordering
AR(1)"ar1"Correlation decays with time: Corr(t, t+k) = ρᵏLongitudinal data — closer time points more correlated
Unstructured"unstructured"All unique pairwise correlations estimatedFew time points, large n; most flexible but uses many parameters
M-dependent"userdefined"Non-zero correlation only up to m lagsWhen correlation drops to zero after m time points

Choosing structure: QIC (Quasi-likelihood Information Criterion) — lower QIC favours better correlation structure. Use QIC() from geepack. For robustness, exchangeable or AR(1) are most common in epidemiology.

GEE vs LMM: Interpretation Difference

▼

The key conceptual difference — using a longitudinal example (SBP measured 4× per patient):

AspectLMM (subject-specific)GEE (marginal)
β interpretation"For a given patient, a 1-unit increase in x is associated with β change in y""In the population, a 1-unit increase in x is associated with β change in average y"
Random effectsExplicitly estimated (BLUPs for each cluster)Not estimated — nuisance parameters
Missing dataValid under MARValid under MCAR; robust SE partially protects
Policy question"What is the effect for this specific patient/school?""What is the effect if we intervene on the whole population?"

Rule of thumb: If the research question is about the average population-level treatment effect → use GEE. If the question is about between-cluster variability or subject-specific prediction → use LMM.

▼
  1. Research Question: Is there a population-averaged association between X and Y, accounting for correlation within clusters?
  2. Environment: Load geepack, tidyverse, gtsummary
  3. Arrange data: Long format (one row per observation), sorted by cluster ID then time
  4. Assumption checks:
    • Identify cluster variable (id variable)
    • Check correlation within clusters (ICCbin or manual)
    • Confirm outcome distribution (continuous → identity, binary → logit, count → log)
  5. Analyse:
    • Fit independence model first (baseline)
    • Fit exchangeable / AR(1) model

    • Obtain robust SE from sandwich estimator
  6. Present: Report β with 95% CI (robust SE), p-values, working correlation structure, QIC
  7. Post-check: Sensitivity analysis — does changing correlation structure change conclusions?

Introduction to Linear Regression

Linear regression is a supervised learning approach that models the dependence of Y on x as being linear.

Example: Predicting housing prices based on different sizes — given labelled data (actual price of each example), the model learns to predict price for unseen data (regression problem — predicting real-valued outputs).

When two variables are related, it means that when one variable changes, the other will change on average by a certain amount. Y changes with x — the question is how much Y changes with any given change of x.

Univariate Linear Regression

Linear regression with 1 independent variable (attribute), x, and 1 dependent variable (target), Y.

Equation

f(x) = w₀ + w₁x

Slope Interpretation

  • Positive Slope (w₁ > 0): As x increases, y also increases
  • Negative Slope (w₁ < 0): As x increases, y decreases
  • Zero Slope (w₁ = 0): Y does not change with x; no relationship between the variables
Method of Least Squares

The fitted line is most often determined by the method of "least squares".

  1. Deviations from the fitted line are called residuals
  2. Calculate the square of each residual, then total them up — this gives the sum of squares
  3. Repeatedly tweak the regression equation until you get the lowest possible sum of squares
  4. Find the line that minimises the sum of the squared residuals

Residuals are a measure of how far from the regression line data points are. RMSE is a measure of how spread out these residuals are — in other words, it tells you how concentrated the data is around the line of best fit.

Finding Regression Slope and Intercept (Step-by-Step)

Given the equation: y′ = a + bx

Step 1: Prepare Data Table

Calculate ∑x, ∑y, ∑xy, ∑x², and n (number of observations).

Step 2: Calculate b (slope) and a (intercept)

Find b:

b = [n(∑xy) − (∑x × ∑y)] / [n(∑x²) − (∑x)²]

b = [6(20,485) − (247 × 486)] / [6(11,409) − 247²]

b = (122,910 − 120,042) / (68,454 − 61,009)

b = 2,868 / 7,445 = 0.385225


Find a:

a = [(∑y × ∑x²) − (∑x × ∑xy)] / [n(∑x²) − (∑x)²]

a = [(486 × 11,409) − (247 × 20,485)] / [6(11,409) − 247²]

a = [5,544,774 − 5,059,795] / 7,445 = 484,979 / 7,445 = 65.14

Step 3: Insert Values into Equation

y′ = 65.14 + 0.385225x

Introduction to Decision Tree Regression

Decision tree regression is a powerful machine learning technique used to model and predict continuous target variables. This approach constructs a tree-like model of decisions and their possible consequences, allowing for intuitive and interpretable analysis of complex relationships in data.

Structure

  • Hierarchical model that composes of internal decision nodes (m) and leaf nodes
  • Each decision node m implements a test with discrete outcomes labelling the branches
  • Each leaf node has a numeric output instead of a category or class

Example: Predicting House Price

A decision tree splits by features like number of rooms and build-up area:

  • Buildup ≥2500 → Yes: #rooms ≥5 → RM800k / No: RM600k
  • Buildup ≥2500 → No: #rooms ≥5 → RM400k / No: RM300k / RM100k

App Recommendation Example

Recommending apps to new users based on age and gender (classification); same tree concept for regression predicts continuous values.

Building a Decision Tree

How Do We Obtain a Good Split?

  • Metric: Sum of Squared Errors (SSE) for regression (vs entropy/Gini for classification)
  • The goal is to partition the data into subsets such that each subset contains homogeneous values (with minimum standard deviation in the case of regression)
  • The goal is to decrease the standard deviation after the data is split into subsets
  • Hence, the attribute that can provide the highest decrease in standard deviation is chosen

SSE Formula

SSE = ∑(yi − 𝛸)2

The algorithm selects the split that minimizes the sum of the squared deviations from the mean in the two separate partitions.

Example: Predicting Price (in Mil)

Split feature "Year" at Year > 2010:

  • Yes branch: SSE = 0.05167
  • No branch: SSE = 0.01732

The algorithm tries all possible splits for all features and picks the one with minimum total SSE.

Pruning Decision Trees

Since the tree is grown from the Training Set, a fully developed tree typically suffers from overfitting. Overfitting occurs when a decision tree model is too complex and fits the training data too closely, compromising its ability to generalize to new, unseen data.

The tree is pruned to minimize the sum of: (1) the output variable variance in the validation data, and (2) the number of terminal nodes.

Pruning is performed on a last-in first-out basis — the last grown node is the first to be subject to elimination.

Two Types of Pruning

TypeDescription
Pre-pruningEarly stopping criteria — selecting a depth before perfect classification. Stop the tree-building process early before it produces leaves with very small samples.
Post-pruningGrow the tree to perfect classification then prune the tree. Allow the tree to grow until all leaves are pure and/or have no training error (it may be overfitted), then prune (cut) the tree to obtain the optimal tree.
Pre-Pruning (Early Stopping)

Stop the tree-building process early before it produces leaves with very small samples. Three parameters:

ParameterDescriptionExample
maxdepthMaximum depth of a tree — length of the longest path from root node to a leaf node. Stops tree building when depth equals the value set.maxdepth=6
minsplitMinimum number of samples that must exist in a node in order for a split to be attempted.minsplit=70 — a node can be further split only if it has more than 70 samples
minbucketMinimum number of samples in any terminal node.minbucket=5 — every leaf node should have at least 5 samples
If only one of minbucket or minsplit is specified, the code either sets minsplit to minbucket×3 or minbucket to minsplit/3, as appropriate.

Post-Pruning: Cost Complexity Pruning

Tree pruning is based on the cost complexity function. Cost complexity pruning is a method used in decision tree algorithms to simplify the tree and prevent it from overfitting the training data.

Cost Complexity Formula

Rα(T) = R(T) + α|T|
TermMeaning
R(T)Training/learning error — error calculated across the leaf nodes of T
|T|Number of leaf nodes in T
αA positive constant that you choose. Determines how much you penalize the complexity of the tree. Higher α = more weight on simplicity = more aggressively pruned tree.

The algorithm calculates the cost complexity factor at each step during the growth of the tree and decides the number of decision nodes in the pruned tree, cutting back some of the branches.

ColumnMeaning

xerrorCross-validated error
xstdStandard deviation of the cross-validated error
CPCost complexity parameter. Select the cp value (with a simpler tree) that minimizes the xerror.

Pruning: Pre-pruning vs Post-pruning

▼
Overfitting occurs when a decision tree is too complex — it fits training data too closely and cannot generalise to new data. Solution: Pruning = removing unnecessary splits.

Pre-pruning (Early Stopping)

Stop tree-building before it produces leaves with very small samples.

  • Max Depth — length of longest path from root to leaf
  • Min Samples per Leaf — minimum sample count each leaf must have
  • Min Split — minimum samples in a node to attempt a split (e.g., min split = 5: only split if node has > 5 samples)
  • Max Features — restrict number of features considered at each split

  • Tree grows with no training error — may be heavily overfitted
  • After fully grown: remove branches that do not improve generalisation
  • Performed last-in, first-out — last grown node is first pruned
  • Goal: minimise (output variable variance in validation data + number of terminal nodes)
  • Result: simpler tree that generalises better to new data

Use biology as inspiration for mathematical model.

Biological Neurons

  • Two interconnected brain cells (neurons)
  • Possess axons and dendrites that enable the neuron to communicate; transmit and receive electrical and chemical signals
  • A signal is passed over or altered by a synapse. It can also increase or decrease the strength of the connection between neurons

Artificial Neural Network (Perceptron)

  • Has interconnected artificial neurons
  • Possesses similar traits with biological neurons to process information, to learn and to self-organise

A single neuron with inputs and outputs. Implemented as software simulations of the parallel process of interconnected artificial neurons/elements.

Formulae

Single neuron: Y = X₁W₁ + X₂W₂

X₁=3, W₁=0.2; X₂=1, W₂=0.4; X₃=2, W₃=0.1; using Sigmoid transfer function:

Training Neural Networks: Loss & Backpropagation

Loss

  • Neural network needs to be trained to produce the correct output correctly
  • Neural networks learn to output correct predictions like how humans learn — told what we are doing is right or wrong
  • Neural network learns by comparing the output (prediction) with the output that was meant to produce
  • The difference between the prediction and the ground truth is called error (loss)
  • To train a neural network, the loss needs to be reduced
  • The weights need to be adjusted in such a way that the prediction is close to the actual output (loss is minimized)

Backpropagation

  • Algorithm to compute the gradient of the loss function with respect to each weight
  • Propagates error backwards from output layer through the network
  • Updates weights (W₁, W₂) to reduce prediction error
  • Repeat backpropagation and weight update until predicted value is close to actual value

Forward Propagation (Perceptron)

Inputs → Weights → Sum → Non-linearity (activation) → Output

Regularization

Techniques to make predictive models perform well on new inputs. The model generalizes well outside the training data. To prevent overfitting or a high variance, we must use regularization. Variance is reduced to make the model not sensitive to small fluctuations in training data.

Early Stopping

A technique that stops the training algorithm before overfitting occurs. Makes use of a set of data (validation set) to determine if the model is overfitting or not.

  1. During training (backpropagation and weight update), evaluate model using validation set
  2. If the validation accuracy is improving → continue training
  3. If the validation accuracy is not improving → stop the training
  4. At each iteration of validation set evaluation, store a copy of the model parameters if the accuracy is improved

Dropout Regularization

A regularization technique that drops out neurons randomly in a neural network.

  • Each hidden layer is given a probability to remove some of its neurons
  • Neurons of the input layer can also be dropped
  • All the ingoing and outgoing links of the neurons are removed as well
  • Perform training on the diminished network
  • Different iterations — neurons are removed randomly from the network and backpropagation training is performed

Why Dropout Works

  • The neurons cannot co-adapt to other neurons because they cannot expect all the neurons will always be present since neurons are stochastically removed
  • The neurons will be forced to learn good representation of the data while not relying on other neurons
  • Smaller weight parameters make some neurons negligible → neural network becomes less complex → less overfitting
  • During dropout, some neurons get deactivated with a random probability P → neural network becomes less complex → less overfitting

R-Squared (R²) — Coefficient of Determination

R² = fraction of variation accounted for (explained by) the fitted line. R-square is also called coefficient of determination.

R² = 1 − (SSres / SStot)
  • R² = 0 → worst possible regression model
  • R² = 1 → perfect model
  • R² = 0.6 → 60% of variations in dependent variable Y can be explained by the independent variables in the regression model

Example Interpretation

92% of the variation in ice cream sales is explained by the temperature of the day.

Review Question: Calculating R²

Given regression line equation: ŷ = 0.143 + 1.229x (number of lectures per day vs hours spent at university).

To calculate R²: find the sum of the residuals squared (SSres) and the total sum of squares (SStot).

In decision tree output: rel error = 1 − R² (same relationship as linear regression)

RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error)
RMSE = √[ (1/n) ∑(yi − ŷi)2 ]
  • Measures average model error in original units of the outcome variable
  • The lower the RMSE, the better the model
  • Residuals are a measure of how far from the regression line data points are; RMSE is a measure of how spread out these residuals are
  • Tells you how concentrated the data is around the line of best fit
  • More sensitive to large errors (outliers) than MAE because errors are squared before averaging
MAE (Mean Absolute Error)
MAE = (1/n) ∑|yi − ŷi|
  • Measures the average magnitude of the errors of the predictions
  • Measures the average absolute difference between the actual and predicted values
  • If absolute value is not taken, the average error becomes the Mean Bias Error — measures average model bias

Interpretation

  • RMSE = 0 → perfect fit
  • Lower RMSE → better model, predictions closer to true values
  • Higher RMSE → large deviation between residuals and ground truth
  • RMSE is in the same units as the outcome variable (unlike MSE)
RMSE is more interpretable than MSE because the units match the outcome. Use MAE if you have outliers (less sensitive to extreme errors).

Introduction

Research results are no longer trustworthy when an instrument (questionnaire / tool) used to collect the data is not valid. Therefore, the quality of an instrument is absolutely important for meaningful empirical research.

Quality of an instrument can be assessed based on validity and reliability.

Establishing evidence of validity is an ongoing process that must be shared by test developers and test users.

Validity vs Reliability — Dart Board Illustration

Consider the dart boards shown below (consider red circle is on target, other than red circle is off target):

Results are neither consistent nor accurateDarts scattered randomly — no pattern, not on target
Results are consistent but off-targetDarts clustered together but in the wrong area
Results are both consistent and accurateDarts clustered together and on target — ideal

While reliability is necessary, it alone is not sufficient. Therefore, validity of the questionnaire is necessary.

Types of Validity
  • Content validity — Experts in the field
  • Criterion validity — Comparison with another scale
    • Concurrent — At least two scales to compare; compare with one or more well established scale(s)
    • Predictive — Two scales taken at different timeline
  • Construct validity — Factor analysis
    • Convergent — Items that belong to the same factor share a high proportion of variance in common.
    • Discriminant validity — The extent to which one factor is distinct from the other factor
Construct Validity

Construct — measured by a tool / questionnaire

How well a tool/questionnaire measures a theoretical concept that it is supposed to measure.

Evidence of how relationship between items and components reflect construct.

Analyses:

  • Factor analysis
  • Reliability
Factor Analysis

Factoring — converting the items into factors and then extracting just a few factors which are able to account for a substantial proportion of the variance in the items.

  • Group items that have something in common
  • Simplify long list of items into smaller group or factor

Types of Factor Analyses

  • Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA)
  • Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA)

Common Key Words

  • Construct = factor = concept = domain = idea
  • Factoring = grouping = simplify
PCA vs Factor Analysis
Principal Components Analysis (PCA)Factor Analysis (FA)
Composites descriptive of the information contained in the variables (items)Measured variables are indicators of the factors
Causal flow is from the measured variables to the latent (factor)Causal flow is from the factor to the measured indicator variables
Extraction Methods
  • PCA — Uses 1s on the diagonal of the correlation matrix in the process of extraction. Is not a factor analysis.
  • Principal axis factoring — Uses estimates of communalities (shared variance) on the diagonal in the extraction process
  • Unweighted and generalised least squares factoring — Minimise the off-diagonal differences between observed and reproduced correlation matrices
  • Maximum likelihood — Uses weights proportional to the relatedness of the variables; maximise the probability of having sampled the correlation matrix from a multivariate normally distributed population
  • Alpha factoring — Extraction strategy is designed to maximise the reliability
  • Image factoring — The shared factor variance is estimated via multiple regression to predict the value of the variable
Rotation Methods

Factor rotation is designed to achieve simple structure for interpretation. It is an iterative process of ‘twisting’ the factor structure in the multidimensional space until a satisfactory ‘fit’ is established.

Orthogonal Factor RotationOblique Factor Rotation
Keeps the factors independent of each other during the rotation processDoes not require the rotation process to keep the factors uncorrelated; permits the factors to be correlated up to a certain degree
Methods: Varimax*, quartimax, equimaxMethods: Direct oblimin, promax

Summary Points
  • Make sure your objective is clearly defined and operationalized.

SPSS Output Key Interpretations

KMO & Bartlett's Test

  • KMO — Indicate degree of common variance among the items. Higher KMO indicates items share common factors. Lower KMO indicates lack of common factors among the items. When KMO is high, there are common factors among the items to warrant an EFA.
  • Bartlett's Test of Sphericity — If p <0.05, significant test indicates that there are sufficient non-zero inter-correlations among the items to warrant an EFA.

Communalities Table

  • % of item variance explained by the extracted factors
  • For example, the extraction communality of 0.659 for Mastery 1 is the amount of variance in this item that is explained by the factors
  • >0.5 is recommended (Hair et al., 2010). However, could be based on researcher's judgment on a particular item with communality <0.5. >0.25 is considered acceptable.

Total Variance Explained

  • Eigenvalues (EG) — how worthwhile a factor is in terms of the items
  • Cut-off point for EG >1
  • The cumulative % column indicates that the extracted factors explain approximately X% of the total item variance

Scree Plot

  • Look for the arrow pointing to the ‘elbow’ in the scree plot which shows substantial decline
  • Number of distinct EG above this point — scree plot suggests how many factors should be retained
  • Based on both EG and scree plot: determine number of distinct factors

Pattern Matrix (after rotation)

  • Values represent direct correlations between items and factors
  • Check factor loadings >0.3 (commonly used), >0.5 recommended
  • Check for cross-loading — items with comparable loadings across two or more factors may be considered for removal

Factor Correlation Matrix

  • Values represent correlation between factors
  • <0.85 recommended (Kline, 2011)
  • If >0.85 — multicollinearity between factors; factors not distinct; may be combined with theoretical support. Also indicates poor discriminant validity.

Reliability Output

  • Higher Cronbach's alpha value indicates higher reliability, but should not exceed 0.95
  • >0.95 indicates that some items are redundant
  • >0.60 cut-off value commonly used by researchers
  • Corrected item-total correlation — correlation between value of an item to total value of other items in a construct. >0.5 recommended. Negative correlation indicates reverse coding of the item
  • Squared multiple correlation — High value indicates high contribution of the item to the reliability of the construct. Low value indicates the removal of the item. >0.5 recommended (Hair et al., 2010)
  • Alpha if item deleted — If value is more than overall alpha, might indicate removal of the item to improve the Cronbach's alpha

Factor Correlation MatrixCorrelation between factors< 0.85 = discriminant validity confirmed

Pattern Matrix vs Structure Matrix — Key Difference

Pattern Matrix (Use This)

Shows the unique contribution of each factor to each item after accounting for factor intercorrelations. This is the primary table to report for oblique rotation (Promax). Use these loadings for interpretation.

Structure Matrix (Reference Only)

Shows the simple correlation between each item and factor (includes shared variance). Values are always larger than Pattern Matrix. Use to detect cross-loading items (if Pattern is clean but Structure is high on multiple factors).

Item-Level Statistics — What to Check

StatisticMeaningDecision Rule
Corrected Item-Total Correlation (CITC)Correlation of each item with the remaining items' total score. Low value = item measures something different from the rest.> 0.50 recommended; > 0.30 acceptable. Negative → item is reverse-coded or measuring opposite direction

Interpretation Decision Tree

Step 1 — Is alpha acceptable?
  → α ≥ 0.60: Proceed. Report alpha and its level (acceptable/good/excellent)
  → α < 0.60: Review items. Check CITC for low values and consider removing items.

Step 2 — Check individual items
  → CITC < 0.30: Flag the item — it may not belong to this construct
  → CITC negative: Item is reverse-coded or measuring the opposite — reverse score it
  → Alpha if deleted > current alpha: Consider removing item (check content validity first)

Step 3 — Final decision
  → Remove the WORST item first (lowest CITC or highest "alpha if deleted")
  → Re-run and check if alpha improves
  → Document all items removed and the reason

Parallel Analysis (fa.parallel) — How to Interpret

Parallel Analysis generates random simulated data with the same n and p. It compares the observed eigenvalues against those from random data:

  → Observed eigenvalue > simulated eigenvalue: Factor is real — RETAIN it
  → Observed eigenvalue < simulated eigenvalue: Factor is due to chance — DO NOT retain

Decision: Count the number of factors where the blue line (observed) is ABOVE the red dashed line (simulated). This is the number of factors to retain.

Example: If parallel analysis suggests 3 factors, set nfactors = 3 in fa()

Item Removal Process (Iterative)

Remove ONE item at a time, then re-run EFA. Priority for removal:
  1. Items with individual KMO < 0.50
  2. Items with h² < 0.25 (low communality)
  3. Items with cross-loading (com > 1.5; loads ≥ 0.30 on ≥ 2 factors) — remove the item with the highest cross-factor loading first
  4. Items with loading < 0.30 on all factors

CFA Summary — Simplified (Quick Reference)

▼
What is CFA? Confirmatory Factor Analysis confirms whether your survey items actually measure the constructs you think they measure. It tests a pre-specified factor structure — unlike EFA which explores one.

✅ Step 1: Check Data Quality

Before running CFA, check normality of each item:

Data DistributionUse This Estimator
Normally distributedML (Maximum Likelihood)
Mildly non-normalMLR (Robust ML)
Severely non-normal / ordinalWLSMV

Check: Mardia's test — skewness p < 0.05 → non-normal → use MLR/WLSMV

✏️ Step 2: Write the Model

Specify which items belong to which construct using lavaan syntax:

model = "
  depression =~ Q1 + Q2 + Q3
  anxiety    =~ Q4 + Q5 + Q6
"
fit = cfa(model, data = df,
          estimator = "ML")

=~ means "is measured by". Each line = one latent construct and its indicators.

🔍 Step 3: Check Results — Three Things

A. Model Fit — Does model match data?
IndexGood Fit
CFI> 0.95
TLI> 0.95
RMSEA< 0.08
SRMR< 0.08

✅ All good → Model fits. Proceed.
❌ Any fail → Check Modification Indices.

B. Factor Loadings (λ) — Do items measure the construct?
LoadingMeaning
> 0.70Excellent ✅
0.50 – 0.70Good ✅
< 0.50Weak — remove ❌
> 1.0Heywood case ⚠️

Simple formula:
Q1 = λ × Construct + Error
e.g. Q1 = 0.82 × Depression + e
→ 0.82 = how strongly Q1 measures depression

C. Factor Correlations — Are factors distinct?
r between factorsMeaning
< 0.85✅ Factors distinct
≥ 0.85⚠️ Too similar

If > 0.85 → factors may be measuring the same thing → consider merging them

🔧 If Model Doesn't Fit — Use Modification Indices (MI)

modificationIndices(fit, sort. = TRUE, maximum.number = 10)

MI valueAction
> 10 (large)High priority to fix
3.84 – 10Consider if theoretically justified
< 3.84Ignore
Two Common Fixes:
  1. Correlate error terms — for items with similar wording
    Q9 ~~ Q10 (add to model)
  2. Remove the item — if it loads poorly or causes misfit
⚠️ Each change must be theoretically justified. Change ONE at a time, then re-run. Watch for Heywood cases (λ > 1.0) after adding correlated errors.

📋 What to Report in Your Write-Up

  1. Estimation method (ML / MLR / WLSMV) + normality test result
  2. Fit indices table — CFI, TLI, RMSEA (90% CI), SRMR for each model version
  3. Factor loadings table — standardised λ + p-value for each item
  4. Model revision details — which items removed / errors correlated, and why
  5. Path diagram — semPaths(fit, ...)
  6. Reliability — McDonald's Omega (ω > 0.70) per factor
  7. Convergent validity — AVE ≥ 0.50 per factor

What is a Heywood case?
An inadmissible solution where the CFA estimator produces mathematically impossible values:
• Standardized factor loading > 1.0
• Negative error (residual) variance
• Factor correlation > 1.0

Why does it occur?
• Model is misspecified (wrong structure)
• Too few indicators for a factor (PA2 only has 3 items — adding residual correlation over-parameterizes)
• Multicollinearity among Q9 and Q10 (both high loadings: 0.844, 0.856)
• Small sample relative to model complexity

Rule: Any Heywood case = inadmissible solution → ALWAYS reject

💡 Key Principles for CFA:
• Never revise model based on statistics alone — must have theoretical/content justification
• Heywood case = inadmissible solution — always reject without exception
• Maximum 2–3 revisions — excessive modification = overfitting (capitalizing on chance)
• CFA is confirmatory — do not add items not present in original EFA solution
• Always report both initial and final model for transparency and reproducibility
• Cross-loading MIs are generally not added unless strongly content-justified

AVE = mean of (Std.all)² for each item in the factor. Example: if 3 items have λ = 0.70, 0.80, 0.75 → AVE = (0.49 + 0.64 + 0.56)/3 = 0.563 ≥ 0.50 ✅

Step 4 — Discriminant Validity

Factor correlationr < 0.85 between any two factorsFactors are sufficiently distinct (Brown, 2015)
Fornell-Larcker criterion√AVE of each factor > correlation with any other factorEach factor shares more variance with its own items than with other factors

Step 5 — Modification Indices (MI)

Modification indices suggest which parameters, if freed, would most improve model fit (lower χ²).

  → MI > 10 (or > 3.84 at α=0.05): Consider freeing this parameter
  → Common modification: Allow residuals of similar items to correlate (e.g., items with similar wording)
  → Caution: Each modification must be theoretically justifiable — never modify purely for statistical reasons
  → Modify one parameter at a time, then re-run the model
  → Report all modifications made and the theoretical rationale

CFA Interpretation Decision Tree

Step 1: Check global fit → CFI ≥ 0.95, RMSEA ≤ 0.08, SRMR ≤ 0.08
  → If good fit: proceed to Step 2
  → If poor fit: inspect modification indices → make 1 justified change → re-run

Step 2: Check all factor loadings λ ≥ 0.50 and p < 0.05
  → Remove items with λ < 0.30 or p ≥ 0.05 → re-run

Step 3: Calculate AVE per factor (≥ 0.50) and CR/ω (≥ 0.70)

Step 4: Check factor correlations < 0.85 for discriminant validity

Step 5: Report final model with all fit indices, loadings, AVE, CR

What to Report in a Thesis/Paper

  • Global fit: χ²(df), p, CFI, TLI, RMSEA (90% CI), SRMR
  • Factor loadings: Standardised λ, SE, z, p for each item
  • Convergent validity: AVE per factor (≥ 0.50)

What is SEM?

▼

Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) is a multivariate statistical modeling technique that aims to explain the structure of relationships among multiple variables (Hair, Black, Babin & Anderson, 2010).

Key Requirement: SEM needs strong theoretical specification of the model ahead of the analysis — to verify (confirm) our theory on the relationships. It is a confirmatory, not exploratory, technique.

SEM consists of TWO components (Bartholomew et al., 2008):

ComponentAlso CalledWhat It Does
Measurement ModelCFA partDeals with latent variables (factors) and the relationships between observed items and the factors. Defines how constructs are measured.
Structural ModelPath analysis partDeals with how latent variables relate to each other. Specifies directional (causal) or non-directional (correlational) paths between constructs.

Similar to CFA — SEM examines how well the variance-covariance matrix produced from the model fits the variance-covariance matrix of the observed data → Goodness of Fit.

SEM 8-Step Process — Detailed Guide

▼

SEM textbooks (Diamantopoulos & Siguaw, 2000; Bollen & Long, 1993; Hoyle, 1995; Kline, 2011; Wang & Wang, 2011; Raykov & Marcoulides, 2000; Schumacker & Lomax, 2004) describe up to eight steps in the modern application of SEM. These steps ensure the analysis is theory-driven, statistically sound, and reproducible.

Step 1 — Model Conceptualisation
  • Define the theoretical framework for the model before looking at the data.
  • Identify all latent constructs (factors) and their observed indicators (items).
  • Specify the direction and nature of relationships between constructs (correlational, causal, mediating, moderating).
  • Clearly distinguish exogenous variables (independent/predictor — no incoming paths from other latent variables) from endogenous variables (dependent/outcome — have at least one incoming path).
  • Justify every path based on theory or prior empirical evidence — SEM is confirmatory, not exploratory.
  • Common frameworks used: literature review, conceptual/theoretical framework (e.g., Theory of Planned Behaviour, Health Belief Model).
Step 2 — Path Diagram Construction
  • Draw a visual representation of the model using standard SEM notation.
  • Circles/Ellipses = latent (unobserved) variables; Rectangles/Squares = observed (manifest) variables.
  • Single-headed arrows (→) = causal/directional paths; Double-headed arrows (⇔) = correlations/covariances.
  • Small circles with arrows pointing to endogenous variables = error terms (residuals).
  • Each indicator/observed variable gets an error term. Each endogenous latent variable gets a disturbance (residual) term.
  • The path diagram helps detect identification problems before running the model and communicates the model clearly to reviewers.
Example path diagram (SEM with 2 factors):

  [Q4]◤           ◦[Q8]
  [Q6]◤▶(F1)↔(F2)▶[Q9]
  [Q7]◤           ◦[Q10]
  [Q11]◤

  F1 =~ Q4+Q6+Q7+Q11 (measurement model)
  F2 =~ Q8+Q9+Q10    (measurement model)
  F2 ~ F1            (structural path)
Step 3 — Model Specification (lavaan Syntax)
  • Translate the path diagram into mathematical model equations using lavaan syntax.
  • Specify the measurement model (=~) for each latent variable and its indicators.
  • Specify the structural model (~ for regression, ~~ for covariance) for paths between constructs.
  • Choose the estimator: ML (default, multivariate normality assumed), MLR (robust ML, Yuan-Bentler, for non-normal data — use when Mardia kurtosis p < 0.05), WLSMV (for ordinal/categorical indicators).
  • Decide on scaling method: lavaan defaults to fixing the first indicator loading to 1.0 (marker variable method).
# lavaan operator reference
# =~   "is measured by"  → measurement model (factor → items)
# ~    "is regressed on" → structural path (Y ~ X)
# ~~   "is correlated with" → covariance/correlation
# :=   "is defined as"  → computed parameter (e.g., indirect effect)

model = "
  F1 =~ Q4 + Q6 + Q7 + Q11   # measurement
  F2 =~ Q8 + Q9 + Q10
  F2 ~ F1                     # structural path
"
fit = sem(model, data = data, estimator = 'ML')
Step 4 — Model Identification
  • A model is identified if it is theoretically possible to obtain a unique solution for each parameter.
  • Identification is determined by the relationship between known information (observed variances and covariances) and unknown parameters (to be estimated).
TypeConditiondfImplication
Under-identifiedParameters > knownsdf < 0✘ Cannot be estimated — need to constrain parameters or add indicators
Just-identifiedParameters = knownsdf = 0⚠ Saturated model — perfect fit by definition, no model test possible
Over-identifiedParameters < knownsdf > 0✓ Testable — degrees of freedom allow chi-square test of fit
Rule of thumb: Each latent variable needs at least 3 indicators for identification (2 is possible with constraints; 1 is not identifiable without fixing parameters). Aim for over-identified models with df > 0.
Step 5 — Sample Size & Data Collection
  • SEM requires larger samples than conventional regression due to the number of parameters estimated.
  • Rules of thumb:
    • Minimum n = 100–200 (Hair et al., 2010)
    • 10–20 observations per free parameter
    • 5–10 observations per indicator
    • Kyriazos (2018): n ≥200 for stable CFA/SEM estimates
  • Monte Carlo simulation (via Mplus or simsem in R): increasingly used to estimate required power and sample size for a specific SEM model. Requires hypothesised population values for each parameter from theory or prior empirical findings.
  • Data requirements: continuous indicators (Likert with ≥5 categories often treated as continuous), check normality (Mardia's kurtosis), no severe outliers, missing data handled (FIML preferred over listwise deletion in lavaan).
  • Missing data: use missing = 'fiml' in sem() for Full Information Maximum Likelihood estimation rather than listwise deletion.
Step 6 — Parameter Estimation
  • lavaan uses iterative optimisation to find parameter values that minimise the discrepancy between the model-implied covariance matrix and the observed covariance matrix.
  • Default estimator: ML (Maximum Likelihood) — assumes multivariate normality. Produces ML chi-square test statistic.
  • MLR (Robust ML, Yuan-Bentler) — use when normality assumption violated (Mardia kurtosis p < 0.05). Produces Satorra-Bentler scaled chi-square. Specify: estimator = 'MLR'.
  • WLSMV — for ordinal/binary indicators using polychoric correlations. Specify: estimator = 'WLSMV'.
  • Convergence: lavaan reports "ended normally after X iterations." If it does not converge, the model may be misspecified or under-identified.
  • Parameters estimated per factor: loadings (λ), residual variances (θ), factor variances (Φ), factor covariances/correlations.
EstimatorWhen to Uselavaan Code

MLRNon-normal continuous data (Mardia kurtosis p < 0.05)estimator = 'MLR'

Step 7 — Model Fit Evaluation
  • Evaluate global fit using multiple fit indices (no single index is sufficient alone):
IndexGood FitAcceptable
χ² p-value>0.05>0.05
CFI≥0.95≥0.90
TLI≥0.95≥0.90
RMSEA (+ 90% CI)≤0.05 (CI <0.08)≤0.08 (CI <0.10)
SRMR≤0.05≤0.08
  • If global fit is inadequate, inspect localised misfit:
    • Modification Indices (MI): MI > 3.84 suggests adding a path may improve fit (χ² reduction ≈ MI value). Prioritise theoretically justified MIs only.
    • Standardised Residuals (SR): |SR| > 2.58 indicates a pair of variables is poorly fitted by the model.
    • Parameter estimates: check all loadings are significant (p < 0.05) and λ > 0.50; check for Heywood cases (loading > 1 or negative variance — always reject model if present).
summary(fit, fit.measures = T, standardized = T)
modindices(fit, sort = T, maximum.number = 10)  # top MIs
residuals(fit, type = "standardized")           # SRs
Step 8 — Model Respecification (if needed)
  • If fit is inadequate, respecify the model guided by MIs, SRs, and theory — NOT purely statistically driven.
  • Permissible modifications:
    • Add correlated error terms (Q9 ~~ Q10) — only if theoretically justified (e.g., same wording, same method)
    • Remove poorly-fitting indicators (low loading or high SR) — requires theoretical justification
    • Add or remove structural paths based on theory
    • Move an indicator to a different factor if content warrants it
  • Not permissible: Making modifications purely to improve fit without theoretical basis (capitalisation on chance — leads to overfitting).
  • Model comparison:
    • For nested models: use chi-square difference test (Satorra-Bentler scaled χ² difference for MLR estimator)
    • For non-nested models: compare AIC/BIC (lower = better)
  • Cross-validation: Ideally validate the respecified model on a new/holdout sample to confirm it is not sample-specific.
  • Report all models: Report both the initial and final model with justification for each change.
# Model comparison (nested models with ML estimator)
anova(fit.model1, fit.model2)

# AIC / BIC comparison (any models)
AIC(fit.model1, fit.model2)
BIC(fit.model1, fit.model2)

# For MLR estimator — Satorra-Bentler scaled chi-sq difference
lavTestLRT(fit.model1, fit.model2, method = "satorra.bentler.2010")

Causal Relationships in SEM

▼

A causal (regression) relationship assigns a directional path from an independent (exogenous) variable to a dependent (endogenous) variable. In lavaan, use the ~ operator (single tilde).

Relationship Typelavaan SyntaxInterpretation
Observed → ObservedQ4 ~ Q11Q11 (IV) predicts Q4 (DV) — simple regression
Latent → LatentF2 ~ F1F1 (exogenous) causes F2 (endogenous) — SEM path
Observed → LatentF2 ~ Q1Observed predictor on latent outcome
Latent → ObservedQ4 ~ F1Latent predictor on observed outcome
Key principle: The direction of the arrow in SEM is based on theory, not statistical analysis. Always specify the causal direction based on your research hypothesis before running the model.

Mediation — Concept & Theory

▼

A mediator Z is a third variable that intervenes between two related variables X and Y (Hair et al., 2010). Z has two roles: it is a DV in one equation and an IV in another (Awang, 2012). Z mediates the relationship of X to Y.

WITHOUT mediator Z:          X ─────────────────────────────────────> Y
                                          (direct effect: c)

WITH mediator Z:             X ──────────> Z ──────────> Y     (indirect: a × b)
                              └─────────────────────────> Y     (direct: c’)
                                   (total = c’ + a×b)
PathDescriptionlavaan code
aX → Z (IV to mediator)Z ~ a*X
bZ → Y (mediator to DV)Y ~ b*Z + c*X
c’X → Y direct effect (with Z in model)c in above
a×bIndirect effect (mediation)ind := a*b
c’+a×bTotal effecttotal := c + a*b

Bootstrap for indirect effect: Use bootstrap CI (n=1000–5000) to test indirect effect (a×b). If 95% CI does NOT include zero → significant mediation.

Mediation in lavaan: Code Template

▼
# Mediation model in lavaan
# X = exogenous (IV), Z = mediator, Y = endogenous (DV)

model.med = "
  # Measurement model (if latent variables)
  F1 =~ Q4 + Q6 + Q7 + Q11  # F1 = X (exogenous)
  F2 =~ Q8 + Q9 + Q10        # F2 = Y (endogenous)

  # Structural model with labels for indirect effect
  Z  ~ a*F1              # a path: X → mediator Z
  F2 ~ b*Z + c*F1        # b path: Z → Y; c = direct effect X → Y

  # Define indirect and total effects
  ind   := a*b           # indirect effect
  total := c + a*b       # total effect
"

fit.med = sem(model.med, data = data,
              se = 'bootstrap', bootstrap = 1000)
summary(fit.med, fit.measures = T, standardized = T, ci = T)

# Check indirect effect confidence interval
parameterEstimates(fit.med, boot.ci.type = 'bca.simple')[
  parameterEstimates(fit.med)$label %in% c('ind','total'), ]
Report: Report the indirect effect (a×b), its 95% bootstrap CI, and whether the CI excludes zero. Also report the direct effect (c') and total effect. State whether mediation is complete, partial, or none.

Moderation — Concept & Theory

▼

A moderator M is a third variable that changes the relationship between two related variables X and Y (Hair et al., 2010). M moderates the causal effect that an IV has on a DV (Awang, 2012). Also commonly known as interaction.

WITHOUT moderator:     X ─────────────────────────────────────────> Y

WITH moderator M:      X ────────────────────────────────────────> Y
                       M ────────────────────────────────────────> Y
                      X*M (interaction) ─────────────────────────> Y
TypeConditionInterpretation
Complete ModerationX*M significant; X alone NOT significantM completely changes the X→Y relationship. X only has an effect in the presence of M.
Partial ModerationX*M significant; X alone also significant; a (with X*M) < a (without X*M)M partially changes the X→Y relationship. Both X and X*M contribute.
No ModerationX*M NOT significantM does not change the X→Y relationship.
Note (Dr Wan): Moderation is typically applied to observed variables only. For latent variable moderation: (a) use multi-group CFA for categorical moderators; (b) create interaction latent variable (items of X × items of M) for numerical moderators. This is complex and beyond basic SEM scope.

The effect of X on Y was stronger/weaker at high vs low levels of M."

Q1 · 25 marksMANCOVA — Cognitive Rehabilitation in Deaf Children▾
Scenario: Quasi-experimental study, pre-test/post-test + control group. 24 deaf children (n=12 experimental, n=12 control). DVs: auditory perception + verbal intelligibility. Covariate: pre-test scores.
(a) Rationales & assumptions of MANCOVA [8 marks]

Rationales (4):

  1. Multiple correlated DVs: Auditory perception and verbal intelligibility are two related continuous outcomes. MANCOVA analyses them jointly, preserving their correlation structure and avoiding inflated Type I error from separate ANCOVAs.
  2. Control covariate: Pre-test scores are included to statistically equate groups on baseline differences — critical in quasi-experimental designs without full randomisation.
  3. Reduce Type I error: Simultaneous analysis with one omnibus test controls familywise error rate better than multiple ANCOVAs.
  4. Increase statistical power: Partialling out covariate variance reduces MSE, increasing sensitivity to detect the true group effect.

Assumptions (4):

  1. Multivariate normality of DVs within each group.
  2. Homogeneity of variance-covariance matrices (Box's M test): p > 0.001 acceptable (lenient, Box's M is sensitive to non-normality).
  3. Absence of multicollinearity: DVs should be correlated (r = 0.3–0.8) but not too highly (r < 0.90).
  4. Homogeneity of regression slopes: Interaction IV×covariate must be non-significant (p > 0.05), meaning the covariate-DV relationship is the same across groups.
  5. Linearity between covariate (pre-test) and each DV (post-test) within groups.
  6. No multivariate outliers (assessed via Mahalanobis distance).
(b) Describe the covariate & reasons [5 marks]

Covariate: Pre-test scores of both auditory perception and verbal intelligibility (baseline measurements before the programme).

Reasons:

  1. In a quasi-experimental design, participants are not randomly assigned — pre-existing group differences can confound results. Pre-test scores control for these baseline differences.
  2. Adjusting for pre-test scores reduces unexplained variance in post-test scores, lowering MSE and improving precision.
  3. The covariate should be linearly related to DVs (pre-test and post-test of the same construct are expected to be strongly correlated).
  4. Covariates should be uncorrelated with the grouping variable; high inter-covariate correlation (r > 0.7) indicates multicollinearity.
  5. Residuals from the covariate-adjusted model should be normally distributed and homoscedastic.
(c) Report findings — Wilks' Λ F(2,19)=4.03, p=0.0001 [12 marks]

The experimental group showed a notable increase in auditory perception from pre-test (M=5.63, SD=0.56) to post-test (M=7.29, SD=0.60), while the control group showed minimal change (pre: M=5.67, SD=0.49; post: M=5.72, SD=0.47). Similarly, verbal intelligibility increased markedly in the experimental group (pre: M=2.58, SD=0.23; post: M=4.01, SD=0.23) compared to marginal change in controls (pre: M=2.55, SD=0.23; post: M=2.61, SD=0.19).

The one-way MANCOVA revealed a statistically significant overall difference in combined post-test scores between groups after controlling for pre-test scores [Wilks' Λ = F(2,19) = 4.03, p < 0.001]. Pre-test auditory perception [F(1,19)=4.90, p=0.002] and pre-test verbal intelligibility [F(1,19)=6.05, p<0.001] were both significant covariates.

Follow-up univariate ANCOVA revealed significant group effects on auditory perception [F(1,20)=24.54, p<0.001, η²=0.56] and verbal intelligibility [F(1,20)=27.56, p<0.001, η²=0.68], both large effect sizes.

Conclusion: The cognitive rehabilitation programme significantly improved both auditory perception and verbal intelligibility in the experimental group compared to controls, even after adjusting for baseline scores. Large effect sizes (η²=0.56 and 0.68) indicate clinically meaningful programme impact.
